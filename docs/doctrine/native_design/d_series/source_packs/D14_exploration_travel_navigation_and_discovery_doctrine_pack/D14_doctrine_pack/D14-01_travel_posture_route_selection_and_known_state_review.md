## Purpose

This file defines the front end of travel and exploration.

## Five-checkpoint front end

```text
1. Travel / exploration intent checkpoint
2. Posture checkpoint
3. Route / area checkpoint
4. Known-state review checkpoint
5. Risk and handoff checkpoint
```

## Intent checkpoint

Intent must be clear enough to classify the procedure as route travel, area exploration, site approach, site entry, pursuit/evasion, scouting, mapping, resource search, platform navigation, realm crossing, or source-local exploration. Broad intent should be clarified rather than converted into a generic travel roll.

## Posture checkpoint

Travel posture is a declaration of priority, not a generic bonus. Posture changes what is easier, harder, visible, missed, exposed, delayed, protected, or handed off.

Core posture families: speed, safety, stealth, scouting, mapping, tracking, foraging/resource search, concealment, diplomatic approach, survey/analysis, ritual sensing, sensor sweep, platform caution, resource conservation, pursuit, evasion, combat readiness, social blending, and source-local posture.

Examples: speed may improve progress but increase hazard exposure; stealth may reduce detection but slow progress; mapping may improve future route certainty but increase exposure; sensor sweep may reveal contacts but create emissions; ritual sensing may detect domain pressure but risk false resonance or hidden attention.

## Route / area checkpoint

D14 supports both route declarations and area declarations. A route is a path, vector, corridor, lane, trail, road, district sequence, jump line, platform route, realm crossing, or approach path. An area is a region, district, ruin, site, wilderness, platform section, city sector, plane-local region, battlefield, or unknown search space.

Routes are functional, not necessarily geometric. Route states include known, partially_known, suspected, rumored, hidden, false, blocked, dangerous, contested, watched, restricted, unstable, source_local, and unmapped. A route may carry multiple states.

## Known-state review checkpoint

Known-state categories: confirmed_known, party_believes, rumored, suspected, mapped_but_unverified, false_or_compromised, hidden_from_party, protected_hidden, and source_local. This prevents rumors, player guesses, map notes, sensor readings, generated table output, or donor boxed text from becoming world truth.

D10 owns actual truth. D11 owns safe presentation. D14 owns the review procedure.

## Risk and handoff checkpoint

D14 identifies likely owner-file handoffs before travel proceeds: D02 for uncertainty; D03 for resource pressure; D05 for methods; D06 for Techniques/domains; D07 for exposure/harm; D08 for actor substrate; D09 for maps/tools/platforms; D10 for world-state and hidden information; D11 for presentation; D12 for active-scene transition; D13 for long Projects; D15 for institutional territory; D16 for later hazards/opposition; D17 for supplies/licenses/scarcity; D18 for long journeys and time skips.

## Anti-drift rules

Posture is not a flat modifier. Route geometry is not required unless geometry matters. Party-facing known-state is not actual truth. Protected hidden state is not revealed during setup.
