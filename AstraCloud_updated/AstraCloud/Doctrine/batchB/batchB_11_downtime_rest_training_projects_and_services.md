# batchB_11_downtime_rest_training_projects_and_services.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s doctrine for **structured non-encounter time, scheduling, activity conversion, and carry-forward state**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a **universal non-encounter time procedure layer** capable of absorbing donor systems built around downtime modes, rest cycles, convalescence periods, training blocks, retraining windows, personal work, service use, maintenance, side projects, off-screen development, absent-character advancement, and other meaningful time expenditures without collapsing those functions into a donor-shaped “between adventures” chapter.

This file is not merely a rest section.
It is the procedure layer for:
- downtime-state classification,
- non-encounter time segmentation,
- rest opportunity and interruption,
- recovery-support activity,
- training and retraining,
- consolidation and disciplined practice,
- work, maintenance, and upkeep,
- service use,
- personal and shared projects,
- off-screen and absent-character activity,
- overlap and exclusivity of long-form actions,
- interruption, expiry, and decay,
- asynchronous party timing,
- delegated activity,
- and conversion of elapsed time into durable carry-forward states.

In Astra terms, this file governs how:
- time is converted into progress when play is no longer running scene-by-scene,
- structured non-encounter activity differs from open narrative lull,
- rest differs from recovery outcome,
- training differs from retraining,
- projects differ from one-off tasks,
- services enable activity without replacing adjacent doctrines,
- different activities can overlap, partially overlap, exclude one another, or reset under interruption,
- players can push, stack, split, or delegate non-encounter activity without leaving doctrine,
- and non-encounter time hands off cleanly into survivability, economy, crafting, faction, and settlement systems.

## Core design stance

Astra must be able to absorb donor systems where meaningful non-encounter time is expressed as:
- a formal downtime mode,
- a rest period with activity restrictions,
- a limited-activity phase,
- a week-long retraining block,
- a month-long recovery period,
- a long-term project clock,
- a service-enabled progression channel,
- an off-screen side adventure,
- a cultivation or meditation cycle,
- an institutional learning window,
- a repair-and-readiness period,
- a money-making interval,
- a fallout-management interval,
- or a mixed schedule where several compatible activities share the same span.

Astra must therefore treat non-encounter time as a doctrine layer rather than as narrative filler.

B11 is not responsible for determining what every activity ultimately means in body, item, economic, factional, or settlement terms.
It is responsible for giving Astra a stable grammar for **how non-encounter activity behaves procedurally through time**.

## Core doctrine terms

### Non-encounter time
**Non-encounter time** is any meaningful span of play in which activity is no longer resolved through immediate scene-by-scene or turn-by-turn procedure, but where elapsed time still produces mechanical, social, strategic, or developmental consequences.

### Downtime
**Downtime** is structured non-encounter time in which one or more actors choose among recognized activity families that convert time into progress, relief, readiness, access, or fallout management.

### Open lull
An **open lull** is low-pressure narrative time that does not yet require formal downtime resolution.
Open lull can become structured downtime when time, activity choice, or carry-forward consequences matter.

### Downtime state
A **downtime state** is the currently active procedural class of structured non-encounter time.
Different downtime states support different activities, constraints, and outputs.

### Interval
An **interval** is the minimum or chosen unit of structured non-encounter time used to resolve an activity.
Intervals may be measured in hours, days, nights, weeks, months, phases, sessions, segments, or other donor-appropriate units.

### Activity family
An **activity family** is a recognized procedural class of non-encounter action, such as rest, training, retraining, work, project work, maintenance, service use, or recovery support.

### Carry-forward state
A **carry-forward state** is any durable output of non-encounter activity that persists into later play.
Examples include restored readiness, partial recovery progress, retraining progress, completed project stage, improved access, earned income, reduced fallout, maintained infrastructure, learned doctrine, or renewed obligations.

### Overlap class
An **overlap class** describes whether two activities may coexist in the same interval.
Overlap classes are distinct from activity success.

### Interruption class
An **interruption class** describes how an activity behaves if broken, paused, compromised, or forcibly ended.

### Expiry
**Expiry** is the loss of usefulness or validity of a carry-forward state after time, neglect, or changed conditions.

### Decay
**Decay** is the deterioration of progress, readiness, maintenance state, relationship upkeep, project momentum, or similar downtime outputs when they are not sustained.

### Delegated downtime
**Delegated downtime** is non-encounter activity performed on behalf of an actor by assistants, institutions, services, companions, or other authorized agents.

### Asynchronous downtime
**Asynchronous downtime** is structured non-encounter time in which different actors or groups operate on different timescales, durations, or completion points.

### Service
A **service** is any external enabling channel that can be used during non-encounter time to alter what activities are possible, faster, safer, more effective, or more accessible.
A service is not identical to a place, institution, or item.

### Project
A **project** is any non-encounter effort whose output is achieved through cumulative progress over multiple intervals rather than through one completed interval alone.

## Doctrine commitments

### 1. Structured non-encounter time is the spine of the file
Astra must not reduce downtime to “between adventures.”
The primary procedural question is how elapsed time is being converted into progress, recovery support, readiness, access, income, standing, or fallout.

### 2. Downtime state must remain plural
Astra must not collapse all non-encounter time into one generic mode.
Open lull, structured downtime, recovery downtime, training downtime, project downtime, service downtime, and absent-character downtime are distinct procedural states.

### 3. Rest, recovery support, and recovery outcome must remain distinct
Rest is an activity state and opportunity structure.
Recovery support is an enabling or assisting activity.
Recovery outcome belongs to survivability doctrine.

### 4. Training, retraining, and consolidation must remain distinct
Improving, reconfiguring, and consolidating are not procedurally identical and must not be treated as one generic “practice” action.

### 5. Projects must remain broader than one progress model
Astra must support deterministic throughput, project clocks, phased projects, milestone projects, and service-mediated projects without assuming one is universal.

### 6. Services must remain enabling channels, not hidden replacements for adjacent doctrine
A service may accelerate, unlock, support, or structure an activity.
It does not replace body-state law, item law, wealth law, or institution law.

### 7. Opportunity cost must remain real
If a donor system constrains what can be done in non-encounter time, Astra must preserve that constraint rather than laundering all activities into free simultaneous montage.

### 8. Carry-forward states are mandatory outputs
Downtime is meaningful because it leaves durable consequences.
A non-encounter activity should produce a next state, not vanish into narration.

## Downtime-state doctrine

Astra recognizes at least the following downtime-state classes.

### Open lull
Low-pressure time with no immediate need for formal scheduling.
Open lull can support brief narrative actions, casual interaction, or soft preparation.
It becomes structured downtime when activity choice, elapsed time, or durable consequence matters.

### Structured downtime
A formal non-encounter state in which activities are selected, scheduled, and resolved against explicit time intervals or activity slots.

### Recovery downtime
A downtime state centered on rest opportunity, convalescence support, stabilization follow-through, rehabilitation, and other activity that primarily exists to preserve or improve later survivability outcomes.
B02 still owns the body-state consequences.

### Training downtime
A downtime state centered on disciplined repetition, consolidation, practice, learning, retraining, specialization, or reconfiguration.

### Project downtime
A downtime state centered on cumulative progress across multiple intervals.
Projects may be personal, shared, delegated, or institution-mediated.

### Service downtime
A downtime state centered on using external enabling channels such as teachers, healers, repositories, chambers, archives, lodgings, teleporters, clinics, guild offices, or other structured supports.

### Off-screen or absent-character downtime
A downtime state used when a character or group is backgrounded, unavailable, traveling off-screen, recovering away from the main action, or otherwise not present in primary scene procedure but still entitled to lawful progress or lawful non-progress.

A donor may use fewer or more downtime states.
Astra must preserve the distinction whenever it is procedurally meaningful.

## Time-unit and interval doctrine

Structured non-encounter activity must be resolvable across multiple time scales.
Astra must support at least:
- hour-scale intervals,
- rest-window intervals,
- full-day intervals,
- multi-day intervals,
- week-scale intervals,
- month-scale intervals,
- phase- or slot-based intervals,
- project segments,
- and donor-local abstract intervals.

No single unit is universally authoritative.
The correct interval is the one that preserves donor pressure, scheduling logic, and activity granularity.

### Time aggregation
Astra must support lawful conversion from finer to coarser resolution when scene-by-scene play is no longer needed.
Time aggregation must not erase meaningful activity differences.

### Time granularity selection
Choose the narrowest interval that preserves:
- activity incompatibility,
- interruption risk,
- project pacing,
- access windows,
- and carry-forward outputs.

Choose a broader interval when scene detail is not needed and finer resolution would add bookkeeping without doctrinal value.

## Overlap, exclusivity, and simultaneity doctrine

Non-encounter activities do not all interact the same way.
Astra must support at least the following overlap classes.

### Compatible
Two activities can occur in the same interval without degrading one another.

### Partially compatible
Two activities can coexist, but one or both operate at reduced throughput, reduced protection, reduced focus, or reduced reliability.

### Exclusive
Two activities cannot meaningfully coexist in the same interval.
Choosing one blocks the other.

### Interruptible
An activity may coexist with interruptions or partial engagement, but doing so may change its progress model, output quality, or reset risk.

### Reset-on-interruption
An activity requires uninterrupted commitment and loses all or most progress if interrupted.

### Consecutive-only
An activity requires a continuous chain of compatible intervals rather than scattered pieces.

Astra must not assume universal free stacking.
If a donor system allows compatible combinations, preserve them.
If it enforces hard exclusivity, preserve that instead.

## Rest, respite, and interruption doctrine

### Rest as activity state
Rest is a structured non-encounter activity with threshold requirements, activity constraints, enabling conditions, and interruption rules.
Rest is not identical to idleness.

### Rest opportunity
A rest opportunity is any window in which the conditions for a recognized rest state are present.
Rest opportunity may depend on safety, shelter, time, environment, permissions, or donor-specific requirements.

### Rest environment
The rest environment may affect the quality, legality, efficiency, or availability of rest.
Proper conditions, bad conditions, unsafe regions, service support, and environmental distortion may all matter.

### Rest interruption
A rest interval may be:
- preserved under minor interruption,
- degraded under interruption,
- reset by interruption,
- or forbidden outright by donor-local pressure.

### Rest outputs
Rest produces carry-forward states such as:
- restored readiness,
- improved recovery opportunity,
- reduced strain,
- refreshed resources,
- or legally completed rest thresholds.

The body-state meaning of those outputs belongs to B02 and other adjacent files where applicable.

## Training, retraining, and consolidation doctrine

### Improvement training
Improvement training is directed practice intended to strengthen, deepen, or advance an existing capability or progression path.

### Retraining
Retraining is deliberate reconfiguration of an already held choice, method, allocation, or specialized competency.
Retraining is not identical to gaining something new.

### Consolidation
Consolidation is an activity family focused on stabilizing, integrating, refining, or securing already gained capability, progress, or resources.
Consolidation is especially important for donor systems with meditation, cultivation, attunement, doctrinal refinement, or similar processes.

### Doctrine-school or structured learning
This activity family covers learning mediated by formal institutions, repositories, shards, manuals, archives, instructors, schools, or other structured channels.

### Access-mediated learning
Some learning cannot proceed from private practice alone.
Astra must support learning activities whose availability depends on services, infrastructure, archives, teachers, shards, licenses, or institutional access.

Training outputs may include:
- progress toward advancement,
- retraining stage completion,
- doctrinal understanding gained,
- consolidation stage completed,
- specialization prepared,
- or access-unlocked learning opportunity.

Batch A remains authoritative for progression axes and character-building law.
B11 governs only the non-encounter time procedure by which training-type activities are scheduled and resolved.

Structured non-encounter time may be used for:
- professional work,
- earn-income activity,
- personal or group maintenance,
- equipment or base upkeep coordination,
- supply organization,
- reputation maintenance,

### Maintenance state
Astra must support maintenance as a carry-forward concern.
Maintenance is not identical to repair.
Maintenance may preserve readiness, reduce decay, sustain service access, or prevent deterioration without directly rebuilding a damaged object or system.

## Service doctrine

Services are first-class enabling channels in structured non-encounter time.
A service may:
- unlock an activity,
- accelerate an activity,
- reduce risk,
- increase output quality,
- satisfy an access condition,
- support compatible overlap,
- or provide a lawful substitute for otherwise unavailable local capability.

### Service classes
Astra must support at least the following service classes:
- medical and recovery support,
- lodging and shelter support,
- training and instruction support,
- repository and archive support,
- research support,
- transit and scheduling support,
- trade or work-channel support,
- spiritual, ritual, or cultivation support,
- maintenance and readiness support,
- and donor-local specialized services.

### Service-use outputs
Service use may create carry-forward states such as:
- access granted,
- activity enabled,
- time reduced,
- risk reduced,
- throughput increased,
- recovery-support quality increased,
- learning channel unlocked,
- or readiness preserved.

A service is not the same thing as the institution that hosts it.

## Personal, group, delegated, and asynchronous downtime doctrine

### Personal downtime
An activity is personal when it is bound to one actor’s own time, body, focus, or choices.

### Group downtime
An activity is group downtime when a party, team, crew, household, sect, or similar unit engages it as a shared undertaking.

### Delegated downtime
An activity is delegated when another actor, service, cohort, institution, or hired specialist performs all or part of it on behalf of the primary beneficiary.
Delegation may preserve, reduce, or replace the primary actor’s own time burden depending on donor pressure.

### Asynchronous party timing
Astra must support cases where different actors require different durations, recoveries, or project schedules.
The file must not assume that all characters complete downtime on the same clock.

### Absent-character downtime
A backgrounded or absent character must have a lawful state.
That state may be:
- progressing under off-screen downtime,
- unavailable and static,
- progressing through limited absent-character rules,
- or constrained by donor-local absence law.

Astra must not treat absence as automatic progress or automatic non-progress.
It must map donor pressure explicitly.

## Interruption, expiry, and decay doctrine

### Interruption classes
Astra must support at least:
- pause-and-resume,
- degrade-on-interruption,
- reset-on-interruption,
- donor-local custom interruption behavior.

### Expiry classes
A downtime output may be:
- persistent,
- temporary,
- window-limited,
- service-dependent,
- maintenance-dependent,
- or donor-local.

### Decay classes
Progress or readiness may:
- remain stable indefinitely,
- decay slowly,
- decay quickly,
- require upkeep to preserve,
- or collapse under neglect.

These classes are necessary so Astra can absorb donors where rest states, projects, maintenance, or training gains do not all persist in the same way.

## Time pressure, opportunity cost, and slot doctrine

Structured non-encounter time may be constrained by:
- available intervals,
- activity slots,
- exclusivity rules,
- access windows,
- service capacity,
- environmental restrictions,
- institutional permissions,
- fallout pressure,
- recovery burden,
- party desynchronization,
- or donor-local time scarcity.

### Opportunity cost
If choosing one activity meaningfully reduces the ability to pursue another, Astra must preserve that tension.
Opportunity cost is often the reason downtime matters.

### Slot-bounded downtime
Some donors constrain downtime by number of actions or phases rather than by continuous clock time.
Astra must support that model directly.

## Carry-forward state doctrine

Downtime matters because it changes the future.
A valid non-encounter activity should usually create one or more carry-forward states such as:
- completed rest threshold,
- supported recovery interval,
- convalescence stage advanced,
- training progress recorded,
- retraining stage recorded,
- consolidation stage secured,
- project clock advanced,
- project stage completed,
- service access granted,
- income or maintenance output created,
- reduced fallout,
- improved readiness,
- ongoing obligation created or serviced,
- or new access or timing constraint introduced.

Carry-forward state is the doctrinal answer to “what changed because this time passed?”

## Live-play stress patterns and doctrine response

Astra must assume players will routinely:
- stack multiple goals into the same interval,
- try to recover while studying or maintaining,
- push services to replace missing competencies,
- delegate work to institutions, companions, or hires,
- split party schedules across different time burdens,
- optimize limited downtime slots,
- convert off-screen time into power, access, or leverage,
- and treat downtime as fallout management rather than only growth.

These are normal uses of structured non-encounter time.
This file must therefore preserve lawful multi-goal tension, lawful delegation, lawful asynchrony, lawful interruption behavior, and lawful carry-forward outputs.

## Conversion invariants

### 1. Preserve donor downtime pressure
Do not flatten regimented, activity-limited, service-dependent, or long-recovery systems into freeform montage.
Do not inflate light-touch systems into bookkeeping-heavy calendar simulation.

### 2. Preserve opportunity-cost structure
If a donor makes downtime meaningful by forcing hard or soft choice between recovery, growth, money, access, projects, and fallout management, preserve that choice.

### 3. Preserve lawful overlap behavior
If a donor allows overlap, preserve it.
If a donor forbids overlap, preserve that instead.
Do not assume universal free simultaneity.

### 4. Preserve carry-forward outputs
Downtime activity must leave durable next states when the donor intends it to matter.
Do not erase consequential non-encounter time into narrative vapor.

### 5. Preserve lawful absence handling
Absent, backgrounded, or off-screen actors must receive lawful treatment rather than arbitrary favoritism or erasure.

## Lawful incomplete mapping

When a donor construct clearly uses meaningful non-encounter time but does not fully specify its scheduling or interaction behavior, Astra must not invent false certainty.
A construct may instead be classified as:
- directly mapped,
- normalized,
- interval-assigned,
- slot-assigned,
- project-assigned,
- service-mediated,
- absent-state assigned,
- or quarantined pending later doctrine.
