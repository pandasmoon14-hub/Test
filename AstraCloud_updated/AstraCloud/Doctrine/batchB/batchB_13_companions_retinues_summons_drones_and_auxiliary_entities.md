# batchB_13_companions_retinues_summons_drones_and_auxiliary_entities.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s doctrine for **linked secondary actors, presences, and delegated assets under procedural play**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a **universal auxiliary-presence layer** capable of absorbing donor systems built around companions, familiars, summons, eidolon-like partners, drones, cohorts, retinues, servitors, spirit allies, projected avatars, living tools, bonded beasts, escort units, support swarms, and other secondary presences that are not the primary holder but are too operationally significant to be treated as flavor.

This file is not merely a companions section.
It is the doctrine layer for:
- auxiliary actor and auxiliary presence doctrine,
- primary-holder and holder-class doctrine,
- bond and control topology,
- autonomy and command-bandwidth doctrine,
- persistence, dismissal, replacement, recovery, and loss consequence,
- single-entity, group-entity, and collective auxiliary doctrine,
- role assignment and non-combat task doctrine,
- deployment state and carry-forward state,
- scaling and investment-channel doctrine,
- failure, disobedience, corruption, and drift doctrine,
- legality and organized-access handoff for auxiliaries,
- and lawful conversion of donor auxiliary constructs into Astra-native form.

In Astra terms, this file governs how:
- secondary presences extend a holder’s reach without collapsing into free throughput,
- entity flavor is subordinated to relation model, control topology, and persistence class,
- auxiliaries linked to a person, party, institution, item, platform, site, or temporary effect are modeled lawfully,
- temporary summons, bonded allies, cohorts, drones, and quasi-entities attached to items or infrastructure remain distinguishable,
- combat and non-combat auxiliary uses remain first-class,
- and auxiliary doctrine hands off cleanly to survivability, items, downtime, social pressure, institutions, and platform operations.

## Core design stance

Astra must be able to absorb donor systems where secondary presences are expressed as:
- bonded beasts,
- familiars,
- summoned allies,
- eidolon-like partners,
- drones and technical companions,
- cohorts, crews, and retinues,
- spirit attendants,
- projected presences,
- living weapons or semi-sentient host-bound objects,
- escort units and assigned guards,
- swarm-like or collective auxiliaries,
- or other linked presences that operate under a holder’s intent, permission, bond, or support.

Astra must therefore treat auxiliary presences as a doctrine layer rather than as a side effect of creature stats, item tags, social followers, or vehicle crew notes.

B13 is not responsible for determining every stat block an auxiliary may have.
It is responsible for giving Astra a stable grammar for **how linked secondary presences exist, act, persist, recover, scale, and fail**.

## Core doctrine terms

### Auxiliary actor
An **auxiliary actor** is a secondary being or operative presence that can take actions, perform tasks, or meaningfully affect procedure while linked to a primary holder, group, institution, effect source, item, site, or platform.

### Auxiliary presence
An **auxiliary presence** is a secondary operational presence that may not always behave like a conventional creature or person but still extends reach, absorbs risk, performs tasks, creates support, or requires lawful modeling.
An auxiliary presence may be projected, host-bound, semi-sentient, swarm-like, site-tethered, or otherwise conditional.

### Primary holder
A **primary holder** is the actor or structure to which an auxiliary is linked for purposes of authority, command, support, identity, provisioning, or persistence.

### Holder class
A **holder class** is the type of anchor to which an auxiliary is linked.
Typical holder classes include:
- individual holder,
- shared party or crew,
- institution or faction,
- item or relic,
- platform or vehicle,
- site or infrastructure node,
- and temporary effect source.

### Bond model
A **bond model** is the governing relationship by which an auxiliary is linked to a holder.
Examples include training bond, ritual bond, technological link, conjuration tether, service assignment, contract tie, social command chain, or host-dependence.

### Control topology
**Control topology** describes how intent moves between holder and auxiliary.
It is broader than “who gives commands.”
It includes fully commanded, standing orders, shared-bandwidth, delegated-task, reactive, and independent-bonded relations.

### Autonomy
**Autonomy** is the degree to which an auxiliary can convert intent, role, or standing orders into action without continuous direct command.

### Command bandwidth
**Command bandwidth** is the amount of attention, action capacity, procedural load, or support burden required to direct, maintain, or coordinate an auxiliary.

### Persistence class
A **persistence class** describes how an auxiliary remains in existence over time and across scenes.
Examples include persistent bonded, persistent replaceable, dismissible, recallable, time-limited, rebuildable, cohort-replenishable, host-dependent, and unique/irreplaceable.

### Role class
A **role class** describes the operational function an auxiliary is currently assigned or optimized to perform.
Identity and role are not the same thing.

### Operational state
**Operational state** is the current functioning condition of an auxiliary.
Examples include active, deployed, recalled, recovering, unstable, inactive, compromised, lost, reassigned, or dismissed.

### Deployment state
A **deployment state** describes where and how an auxiliary is presently manifested relative to the holder or environment.
Examples include adjacent, remote, embedded, recalled, mounted, projected, off-screen tasked, tethered, or unmanifested.

### Carry-forward state
A **carry-forward state** is any durable result of auxiliary use or treatment that persists beyond the immediate scene.
Examples include injury, fatigue, loyalty strain, repair need, standing-order continuation, assignment lock, hostile attention, bond stress, or gained experience/investment.

## Core principles

### Relation-over-flavor principle
Astra must not organize auxiliary doctrine primarily by species or flavor family.
“Beast,” “drone,” “summon,” “familiar,” and “retinue” are donor surface labels.
The doctrinal center is the relationship topology between auxiliary and holder.

### Auxiliary presence principle
Not every auxiliary is a clean creature.
Some are projected presences, host-bound intelligences, living tools, swarm minds, semi-sentient relic functions, infrastructure-bound guardians, or other quasi-entities.
B13 must support auxiliary presence as well as auxiliary actor.

### Bandwidth principle
An auxiliary’s operational value is not only what it is, but how much bandwidth it consumes and how independently it converts intent into action.
Astra must not accidentally grant free throughput by failing to classify autonomy and command burden.

### Persistence principle
The difference between a recallable temporary helper, a replaceable assistant, a project-built drone, a replenishable cohort, and a soul-bonded once-in-a-lifetime ally is doctrinal, not cosmetic.
Persistence and loss consequence must therefore remain explicit.

### Identity-role-state principle
The same auxiliary may differ by identity, role, and current state.
Astra must preserve those distinctions.
An auxiliary’s identity does not automatically determine its role, and neither automatically determines its current operational state.

### Non-combat support principle
Auxiliaries are not only attack multipliers.
Astra must explicitly support scouting, sensing, hauling, guarding, relaying, witnessing, delivery, maintenance, symbolic legitimacy, off-screen labor, and environmental specialization.

### Boundary preservation principle
B13 must not absorb full creature schema, item ontology, faction legitimacy law, or platform operations.
It governs the auxiliary-side doctrine and hands off other concerns to the appropriate files.

## Auxiliary actor/presence and primary-holder doctrine

### Auxiliary-presence doctrine
An auxiliary exists when a linked secondary presence has enough procedural significance that the system must model:
- who or what it is linked to,
- how it receives or interprets direction,
- what roles it can fill,
- how long it persists,
- what it costs to direct or maintain,
- how it can fail or be lost,
- and what carry-forward consequences attach to it.

### Primary-holder doctrine
Every auxiliary must identify its primary holder class.
The holder is not always a character.
Astra must support auxiliaries linked to:
- an individual,
- a shared party or crew,
- an institution or command structure,
- an item, relic, or host object,
- a platform or vehicle,
- a site or infrastructure node,
- or a temporary effect source.

Holder class matters because it changes:
- who can command,
- who bears responsibility,
- who pays upkeep,
- what happens if the holder is absent or destroyed,
- and where access and legitimacy questions hand off.

## Bond and control topology doctrine

### Bond doctrine
A bond is the relation that anchors auxiliary to holder.
Bonds may be:
- trained,
- contractual,
- ritual,
- conjured,
- technological,
- biological,
- spiritual,
- social/organizational,
- host-dependent,
- or project-built.

A bond may be:
- exclusive or non-exclusive,
- transferable or non-transferable,
- stable or degradable,
- consensual or imposed,
- revocable or irrevocable,
- and shallow or identity-defining.

### Control-topology families
Astra must support at least the following control topologies:

#### Fully commanded
The auxiliary does not meaningfully act toward task fulfillment without direct command or activation.

#### Standing-order autonomous
The auxiliary acts within previously established instructions until the task completes, fails, or conditions change.

#### Shared-bandwidth partner
The auxiliary and holder share a coordinated operating framework such that separate existence does not imply separate throughput.

#### Independent bonded ally
The auxiliary has its own meaningful will or behavioral initiative while remaining aligned, loyal, or bound.

#### Delegated task entity
The auxiliary can be assigned a discrete task or mission and resolve it with limited direct oversight.

#### Group or cohort delegated entity
A group-scale auxiliary operates through command structures, morale, quality, or discipline rather than one-to-one control.

#### Reactive or triggered auxiliary
The auxiliary acts only when its triggering conditions are met, whether magical, technological, behavioral, or situational.

## Autonomy and command-bandwidth doctrine

### Autonomy families
Astra must support these autonomy families:
- fully commanded,
- partially autonomous under standing orders,
- shared-bandwidth partner,
- independent bonded ally,
- group/cohort delegated,
- background or off-screen task entity,
- and reactive or conditional auxiliary.

### Command-bandwidth classes
Astra must support auxiliary relations where command burden is:
- negligible,
- bounded but recurring,
- substantial and action-linked,
- shared with other systems,
- off-screen and scheduling-linked,
- or institutionally externalized.

### Command doctrine commitments
Astra must not assume:
- that every auxiliary gets full independent throughput,
- that every auxiliary consumes an identical command cost,
- or that every auxiliary can be meaningfully active in every scene.

The governing doctrinal questions are:
- how much bandwidth does the auxiliary consume,
- when does that bandwidth matter,
- what autonomy does it possess between commands,
- and what procedural risks arise when bandwidth is insufficient.

## Persistence, dismissal, recovery, replacement, and loss consequence

### Persistence classes
Astra must support at least:
- persistent bonded auxiliaries,
- persistent but replaceable auxiliaries,
- dismissible or recallable auxiliaries,
- time-limited auxiliaries,
- project-built auxiliaries,
- cohort-scale replenishable auxiliaries,
- rebuildable auxiliaries,
- host-dependent auxiliaries,
- site-tethered auxiliaries,
- and unique or effectively irreplaceable auxiliaries.

### Dismissal and recall
Some auxiliaries can be safely withdrawn from active manifestation or returned to a reserve state.
Others cannot.
Astra must preserve that distinction.

### Recovery doctrine
Auxiliary recovery may occur through:
- ordinary rest/support,
- repair,
- healing,
- retraining or recalibration,
- resummoning or reconjuration,
- replenishment of numbers,
- or replacement by a new entity.

B13 owns the classification of recovery route.
Adjacent files own the meaning of the underlying healing, repair, downtime, or service procedures.

### Replacement doctrine
Replacement is not universal.
Some auxiliaries are expendable and replaceable.
Some are replaceable but costly.
Some are replaceable only through special service or project channels.
Some are non-replaceable because the bond itself is unique.

### Loss consequence doctrine
Loss of an auxiliary may cause:
- simple absence,
- downtime burden,
- replacement cost,
- investment loss,
- bond trauma,
- command gap,
- organizational sanction,
- service interruption,
- social fallout,
- or persistent impairment to the holder or the auxiliary channel itself.

## Single-entity, group-entity, and collective auxiliary doctrine

### Single-entity auxiliaries
A single-entity auxiliary is one discrete secondary actor or presence.
Examples include one familiar, one bonded beast, one drone, one spirit attendant, or one projected partner.

### Group-entity auxiliaries
A group-entity auxiliary is a small organized body treated as one doctrinal auxiliary relation.
Examples include a cohort, retinue, support squad, handler team, or summon wing.

### Collective auxiliaries
A collective auxiliary is a swarm-like, distributed, or massed secondary presence whose value depends on group behavior rather than individual identity.
Examples include drone swarms, spirit flocks, vermin clouds, and distributed support constructs.

Astra must not flatten single auxiliaries, groups, and collectives into the same control and loss assumptions.

## Role classes and task assignment doctrine

### Role classes
Auxiliaries may serve roles such as:
- combat support,
- scouting and recon,
- sensory extension,
- carrying and hauling,
- maintenance and repair support,
- delivery and relay,
- symbolic legitimacy or prestige display,
- guard and intercept,
- off-screen labor,
- technical or magical support,
- healing/support assistance,
- infiltration,
- environmental specialization,
- escort and protection,
- and institutional enforcement or service execution.

### Task assignment doctrine
Role and task assignment may be:
- explicit and immediate,
- standing and persistent,
- conditional,
- delegated for off-screen resolution,
- shared between multiple auxiliaries,
- or prevented by current state, bandwidth, legality, or deployment limits.

Astra must preserve the fact that an auxiliary’s role can change without changing its identity.

## Entity state, deployment state, and carry-forward state doctrine

### Entity identity
Entity identity answers what the auxiliary is by underlying type or relation.
Examples include bonded beast, temporary elemental, technical drone, cohort, spirit ally, or living tool.

### Role assignment
Role assignment answers what the auxiliary is presently tasked to do.
Examples include scout, medic, bodyguard, hauler, relay, or combat support.

### Operational state
Operational state answers what condition the auxiliary is presently in.
Examples include active, recovering, inactive, remote-deployed, compromised, unstable, dismissed, bonded-but-unmanifested, or unavailable.

### Deployment state
Deployment state answers how and where the auxiliary is currently manifested relative to holder and environment.
Examples include adjacent, remote, mounted, embedded, recalled, projected, tethered, site-bound, or off-screen tasked.

### Carry-forward state
Carry-forward states include any lasting consequences that matter in later scenes, such as:
- repair need,
- healing need,
- loyalty strain,
- discipline drift,
- corruption risk,
- assignment lock,
- depletion,
- visibility or suspicion,
- credential requirement,
- or bond stress.

## Scaling and investment-channel doctrine

### Scaling principle
Auxiliaries do not all scale through the same route.
Astra must preserve scaling channel as a doctrine property rather than assuming that every auxiliary simply rises with holder level.

### Scaling channels
Possible scaling channels include:
- owner progression,
- direct investment of resources,
- gear or module improvement,
- replacement tier,
- service infrastructure,
- training or retraining,
- ritual reinforcement,
- bond deepening,
- and fixed/non-scaling status.

### Investment doctrine
An auxiliary may require recurring or one-time investment in:
- resources,
- downtime,
- upgrades,
- attention,
- training,
- infrastructure,
- or relationship maintenance.

B13 classifies the channels.
Batch A progression and adjacent Batch B files own the detailed mechanics those channels may invoke.

## Failure, disobedience, corruption, and drift doctrine

### Failure doctrine
Auxiliaries are not always perfect converters of intent into action.
Astra must support lawful failure classes such as:
- misexecution,
- incomplete tasking,
- non-response,
- overload,
- bandwidth shortfall,
- deployment failure,
- recall failure,
- and environmental mismatch.

### Disobedience and deviation
Some auxiliaries may deviate from instruction because of:
- autonomy,
- morale,
- personality,
- instinct,
- corruption,
- external control,
- degraded bond,
- or conflicting obligations.

Astra must leave room for such outcomes without making them universal defaults.

### Corruption and drift
Auxiliary relations may weaken, distort, or become liabilities over time through:
- bond strain,
- possession,
- hacking,
- panic,
- hunger,
- contamination,
- hostile takeover,
- institutional recall,
- or identity drift.

This doctrine is required for corpus-scale conversion even when a donor does not emphasize it.

## Boundary doctrine

### Auxiliary entity vs item
If the construct is primarily a persistent or conditional secondary presence with agency, taskability, bond logic, or operational state, it belongs in B13 even when host-bound to an item.
If it is primarily an item object with passive or activated effects, it belongs in B03.

### Auxiliary entity vs platform
If the construct is primarily a linked secondary actor or presence, B13 owns it.
If it is primarily a vehicle, mount-frame, mech, ship, or other operations platform, B14 owns it.
Border cases should classify both the auxiliary relation and the platform relation rather than collapsing one into the other.

### Auxiliary entity vs social asset
If the construct is primarily a retinue, cohort, escort group, or delegated support body with operational behavior, B13 owns the auxiliary-side doctrine.
If the question is primarily leverage, standing, legitimacy, or organized access around that body, B10 and B12 own those layers.

### Auxiliary entity vs full creature/NPC schema
B13 classifies relation, control, persistence, and role.
Creature- or NPC-schema ownership remains outside this file.

## Lawful incomplete mapping and anti-hallucination rules

When donor material clearly indicates an auxiliary relation but leaves some aspects underspecified, Astra conversion must not invent false certainty.
Auxiliary constructs should be classified as one of the following:
- directly mapped,
- normalized,
- holder-assigned,
- persistence-assigned,
- control-topology assigned,
- placeholder-classified,
- or quarantined pending later doctrine.

Astra must not:
- assume that every auxiliary is a creature,
- assume that every auxiliary is a pet,
- assume that every auxiliary grants independent free throughput,
- assume that every auxiliary is replaceable,
- assume that every auxiliary is combat-focused,
- or collapse host-bound or projected presences into generic magic-item or status language when the donor construct behaves like a linked secondary presence.

## Drafting note for later conversion work

When converting donor material into Astra-native content, always identify:
1. the auxiliary’s holder class,
2. its bond and control topology,
3. its autonomy and command burden,
4. its persistence and replacement class,
5. its role and deployment state,
6. its scaling and investment channels,
7. its likely failure or drift risks,
