# batchB_15_settlements_bases_capitals_and_infrastructure_operations.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension doctrine for **site shells, civic capacity, built infrastructure, and infrastructure-mediated opportunity**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a conversion-stable framework for inhabited, governed, service-bearing, and infrastructure-dependent places as operational environments rather than as lore-only settings.

B15 is not a "towns and cities" chapter.
It is the doctrine layer for any place whose dominant function is one or more of the following:
- habitation,
- governance,
- districting or zoning,
- civic or base-scale infrastructure,
- service concentration,
- local or regional throughput,
- refuge, staging, or claim support,
- defense and resilience at site scale,
- or the generation of structured opportunity and structured friction through built environment.

In Astra terms, this file governs how sites such as villages, forts, compounds, capitals, orbital habitats, colonies, ports, research enclaves, stations, embassy hubs, bastions, mobile capitals, and other hybrid civic environments are classified, what they can lawfully output, what they depend on, what constrains them, and how they change over time.

## Core design stance

Astra must be able to absorb donor material where places are represented as:
- small settlements with limited services,
- fortified outposts,
- party- or crew-scale strongholds,
- districted cities,
- neutral diplomatic hubs,
- sect compounds,
- orbital stations,
- starports and base networks,
- civic-platform hybrids,
- mobile capitals,
- frontier colonies,
- research enclaves,
- embassy cities,
- lawless market nodes,
- treaty-bound sanctuaries,
- or partially ruined but still functioning site shells.

Astra must therefore treat sites as **structured opportunity environments** rather than as descriptive flavor blocks.

A site is not defined only by who lives there.
A site is defined by:
- what kind of place it is,
- how it is governed,
- what infrastructure sustains it,
- what services and outputs it concentrates,
- what access surfaces and approach vectors it exposes,
- what pressures and bottlenecks it generates,
- and how its condition changes what characters can lawfully do there.

B15 must not collapse into a lore encyclopedia.
It must also not reduce sites to simple service kiosks.
A site should always be capable of outputting both **opportunity** and **friction**.

## Core doctrine terms

### Site
A **site** is any inhabited, governed, defended, serviced, or infrastructure-bearing place whose built or organized environment changes what activity is possible there.

### Site shell
A **site shell** is the doctrinal classification of a place as an operational environment.
It defines the place’s dominant function, scale, structural layers, and environment-bearing significance.

### Governance shell
A **governance shell** is the site-level administrative, civic, military, corporate, sectarian, treaty-bound, neutral, or otherwise authoritative structure through which site order is maintained.

### Infrastructure shell
An **infrastructure shell** is the bundle of built systems, utilities, logistics channels, communications, defenses, processing loops, and service-enabling structures that keep the site functioning.

### Service shell
A **service shell** is the set of lawful capabilities the site offers by virtue of its built environment and operating institutions.

### Pressure shell
A **pressure shell** is the set of persistent frictions, bottlenecks, vulnerabilities, obligations, shortages, crowding effects, dependencies, or crisis tendencies naturally generated by the site.

### District or zone
A **district** or **zone** is a lawful operational partition inside a larger site whose access surface, service concentration, security posture, governance overlay, resource function, or social meaning differs from the wider shell.

### Access surface
An **access surface** is the set of sanctioned, restricted, monitored, contested, or informal ways by which actors can approach, enter, move through, or use a site.

### Approach vector
An **approach vector** is the traversal-facing route or channel by which a site can be reached or entered.

### Site output
A **site output** is any durable capability, opportunity, or support the site makes available right now.

### Pressure output
A **pressure output** is any durable friction, bottleneck, restriction, or instability the site generates right now.

### Site state
A **site state** is the current operational condition of the site as a whole or of a major district or subsystem.

### Nested site
A **nested site** is a site inside a larger site that carries its own local shell, access surface, service logic, or pressure profile.

## Site identity and scale doctrine

Astra recognizes that site identity and site scale are not interchangeable.
A place may be small but strategically central, large but operationally thin, static but service-rich, mobile but civic, or physically vast but functionally narrow.

B15 therefore classifies sites by both **dominant function** and **scale class**.

### Dominant-function classes
A site’s dominant function may be one or more of the following:
- habitation site,
- fortified base,
- logistics node,
- market and trade hub,
- diplomatic or neutral hub,
- research or archive enclave,
- training or cultivation site,
- industrial or processing site,
- transit hub,
- sanctuary or refuge,
- command and governance center,
- frontier outpost,
- or hybrid civic site.

### Scale classes
B15 should support at least the following scale classes:
- camp or outpost,
- fortified point or station,
- district settlement,
- town or city,
- capital,
- colony,
- orbital habitat or station,
- civic platform,
- mobile capital,
- and mega-site or composite site.

Scale class affects:
- how many districts or subsites are expected,
- how many infrastructure classes are plausibly present,
- whether service concentration is broad or narrow,
- how site degradation is expressed,
- and whether governance and access surfaces are simple or layered.

Astra must not assume that every donor “settlement” is equivalent to a medieval town.

### Governance shell classes
A site may have one or more of the following governance shell classes:

- military command shell,
- faction-controlled shell,
- multilateral embassy shell,
- sect or doctrinal shell,
- corporate or private shell,
- lawless or weak-governance shell,

### Governance shell outputs
Governance shells can output:
- hearing space,
- permit or claim validation surfaces,
- security stability,
- dispute channels,
- civic legitimacy,
- emergency response authority,

### Governance shell pressures

- competing authorities,
- or selective protection.

Governance shell doctrine in B15 should never replace B12’s organizational access doctrine.
It should instead state what kind of site-level administrative environment exists.

## District and zone doctrine

Large and medium sites should not be treated as undifferentiated interiors.
Astra uses district and zone doctrine to model lawful internal partitions.

A district or zone may vary from the surrounding site in:
- access surface,
- security posture,
- prestige or deprivation,
- concentrated service class,
- local governance overlay,
- environmental conditions,
- defensive posture,
- resource or waste function,
- cultural meaning,
- traffic density,
- or civic symbolism.

### District and zone classes
Common district or zone classes include:
- administrative core,
- market or trade district,
- industrial or processing district,
- embassy or diplomatic district,
- habitation district,
- archive/research district,
- transport or port district,
- training or cultivation district,
- military or security zone,
- undercity or maintenance zone,
- sacred or ceremonial precinct,
- restricted or elite quarter,
- quarantine or hazard sector,
- and informal or illicit zone.

### District doctrine rules
A district is not only a neighborhood label.
A district must be capable of carrying:
- its own access surface,
- its own service concentrations,
- its own security or scarcity posture,
- and its own pressures.

A site may contain nested districts, overlapping districts, or districts with layered jurisdictions.

## Infrastructure class doctrine

Infrastructure doctrine concerns the built systems that make a site function.
B15 does not need to simulate each system numerically, but it must classify their operational significance.

### Major infrastructure classes
Astra should support at least the following infrastructure classes:
- transport infrastructure;
- access and routing infrastructure;
- communications infrastructure;
- defense and shielding infrastructure;
- power or energy infrastructure;
- life-support infrastructure;
- water and air infrastructure;
- waste and recycling infrastructure;
- medical infrastructure;
- archive and research infrastructure;
- educational and training infrastructure;
- market and trade infrastructure;
- industrial and fabrication infrastructure;
- storage and logistics infrastructure;
- credentialing and civic administration infrastructure;
- and ritual, metaphysical, or cultivation infrastructure where relevant.

### Infrastructure outputs and pressures
Every infrastructure class should be understood through **paired outputs and pressures**.

Examples:
- a teleporter or transit hub outputs mobility, rapid arrival concentration, and route integration, but also creates queue pressure, security focus, dependency, and sabotage vulnerability;
- a market outputs trade throughput and goods access, but also creates crowding, illicit flow, supply dependence, and scarcity bottlenecks;
- an archive outputs knowledge access and institutional memory, but also generates secrecy, classification, theft risk, and access bottlenecks;
- a shield network outputs protection and livability, but also creates maintenance burden, power dependency, and catastrophic vulnerability if compromised.

This output/pressure pairing is mandatory doctrine, not optional flavor.

## Service shell doctrine

A site’s service shell defines what that place makes possible by virtue of its built environment and operating structures.

B15 does not own the full downstream law of those services.
It owns the fact that they exist at site level and what kind of opportunity environment they create.

### Site-level service classes
Common service classes include:
- lodging and refuge,
- healing and medical support,
- training and instruction,
- archive and research access,
- market and trade access,
- transport and routing access,
- storage and warehousing,
- fabrication or industrial support,
- communication and coordination services,
- diplomatic or hearing space,
- legal or administrative processing,
- claim validation or registration,
- security and escort services,
- ritual or cultivation support,
- and emergency or disaster response services.

### Service shell rules
A site may be:
- service-rich,
- service-narrow,
- service-gated,
- service-strained,
- service-layered by district,
- or dependent on external service routes.

B15 must distinguish service presence from:
- permission to use the service,
- cost to use the service,
- and mechanical effects of service use.

Those hand off to B12, B04, B11, B02, B06, or other relevant files.

- partially denied,

- degraded,

### Site-state rules

Site degradation should be modeled as a change in shell function, not merely as visual ruin.

## Nested-site doctrine

Large sites often contain smaller sites with distinct shells.
Astra explicitly supports nested-site classification.

Examples include:
- an embassy inside a neutral city,
- a bastion inside a district,
- a teleporter hall inside a market quarter,
- a lab inside a fortress,
- a temple precinct inside a capital,
- a black market inside a lawful port,
- or an archive buried beneath an academy.

### Nested-site rules
A nested site may carry its own:
- service shell,
- access surface,
- governance overlay,
- pressure outputs,
- district partition,
- or state class.

A nested site must not be flattened into generic flavor simply because it exists inside a larger shell.

## Hybrid platform-settlement classification

Some donor constructs pressure both B14 and B15.
Examples include:
- mobile capitals,
- civic ships,
- orbital habitats,
- giant living cities,
- or stations that are simultaneously transport nodes and inhabited civic shells.

### Dominant-function rule
A construct is primarily B15 when its dominant doctrinal job is:
- habitation,
- governance,
- districting,
- infrastructure operation,
- or service-bearing civic environment.

A construct is primarily B14 when its dominant doctrinal job is:
- movement,
- deployment,
- transport,
- frame operation,
- crewed platform action,
- or active service frame behavior.

Hybrid constructs must be classified by dominant function, then explicitly cross-referenced to the adjacent file.
B15 must not silently absorb B14.
B14 must not silently absorb B15.

## Personal strongholds, bases, and civic environments

B15 should support personal, party, or organizational sites without collapsing them into pure settlement fiction.

A bastion, hideout, monastery, safehouse network, stronghold, colony block, orbital module, or command post may all count as B15-primary if their doctrinal role is site-shell and service output rather than frame operation.

These constructs should be classified by:
- site scale,
- governance shell,
- infrastructure shell,
- service shell,
- and pressure shell.

This lets Astra absorb stronghold-heavy donors, city-heavy donors, station-heavy donors, and hybrid campaign structures through one doctrine layer.

## Conversion invariants

### 1. Preserve site function
Do not reduce a donor site to lore if the donor treats it as a capability-bearing environment.

### 2. Preserve site friction
Do not reduce a donor site to a neutral service kiosk if the donor treats it as restricted, crowded, stratified, or vulnerable.

### 3. Preserve district meaning
Do not flatten materially distinct districts or zones into a single undifferentiated site shell.

### 4. Preserve infrastructure dependency
If the donor site depends on ports, shielding, teleporter nets, archives, life support, waste processing, or other support loops, preserve that dependency at doctrine level.

### 5. Preserve site state
If a donor site is damaged, sanctioned, under siege, rebuilding, or otherwise altered, preserve that as an operational site-state rather than visual flavor.

### 6. Preserve dominant function
Do not classify a civic shell as a frame or a frame as a civic shell merely because the donor construct is hybrid.

## Lawful incomplete mapping

For a large donor corpus, many sites will be only partially specified.
B15 must therefore support lawful incomplete mapping.

A site construct may be classified as:
- **directly mapped** when the donor clearly specifies its shell, services, and pressures in Astra-stable terms;
- **normalized** when the donor site is clear in function but needs Astra-native simplification or terminology alignment;
- **scale-assigned** when the donor site’s main known quality is its scale and general role, but detailed service or pressure structure remains partial;
- **placeholder-classified** when the donor site is clearly important but underspecified beyond broad shell identity;
- **hybrid-crossfile** when the donor site is clearly split across B14 and B15 concerns and requires dominant-function tagging plus cross-reference;
- or **quarantined pending later doctrine** when the donor construct cannot yet be mapped without forcing false certainty.

Astra must never invent fake precision merely because a donor site is important.

## Anti-hallucination rules

- Do not assume every site has a market, hospital, archive, garrison, and port.
- Do not assume every capital is a city and every base is a fort.
- Do not assume every district is civic rather than military, ritual, industrial, or diplomatic.
- Do not assume every service-rich site is open-access.
- Do not assume every site with governance has stable legitimacy.
- Do not assume every hybrid mobile construct is primarily a vehicle.
- Do not assume every settlement uses terrestrial logistics or ordinary atmosphere.
- Do not assume every donor site needs a full operational write-up when a lawful shell classification is all that is warranted.

B15 defines Astra’s doctrine for **site shells, civic capacity, and infrastructure-mediated opportunity**.

It explains:
- what kinds of sites exist,
- how site scale and dominant function are classified,
- how governance, infrastructure, service, and pressure shells differ,
- how districts and zones partition a place,
- how sites are approached and entered,
- how support and waste loops sustain or strain them,
- what outputs and pressures a site generates,

This file should always be read as doctrine for **how inhabited and built environments function operationally**, not as a setting gazetteer or lore chapter.
