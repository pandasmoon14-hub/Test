# batchB_10_social_conflict_influence_reputation_and_negotiation.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s doctrine for **social pressure, leverage, standing, and negotiated outcomes under procedural play**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a **universal social-procedure layer** capable of absorbing donor systems built around persuasion, bargaining, intimidation, mediation, negotiation, hearings, public audience pressure, rumors, favors, obligations, institutional standing, faction reputation, legitimacy, and long-tail social fallout without collapsing those functions into a donor-shaped “charisma chapter.”

This file is not merely a diplomacy section.
It is the procedure layer for:
- social pressure,
- leverage use,
- requests and concessions,
- negotiation and mediation,
- coercion and fearful compliance,
- trust and obligation,
- audience management,
- legitimacy and authority,
- reputation and standing,
- social risk and fallout,
- and conversion of social pressure into actionable next states.

In Astra terms, this file governs how:
- pressure moves through people, groups, institutions, and audiences,
- different social approaches create different kinds of outcomes,
- immediate scene pressure differs from long-term standing,
- public and private outcomes diverge,
- leverage can be material, informational, emotional, institutional, or reputational,
- players can pursue strange, indirect, or multi-party social strategies without leaving doctrine,
- and social procedure hands off cleanly into information, wealth, concealment, faction, and encounter systems.

## Core design stance

Astra must be able to absorb donor systems where social pressure is expressed as:
- convincing someone to help,
- making a good impression,
- making a request,
- negotiating an exchange,
- securing a favor,
- extracting compliance through fear,
- brokering peace between rivals,
- influencing one target through another,
- speaking before a crowd, council, jury, or bureaucracy,
- managing suspicion, scandal, or rumor,
- building long-term reputation with groups,
- leveraging title, rank, proof, or institutional backing,
- and dealing with the fallout after a social success or failure.

Astra must therefore treat social interaction as a doctrine layer rather than as a narrow set of “talk skills.”

B10 is not responsible for determining what information is true.
It is responsible for giving Astra a stable grammar for **how social pressure behaves procedurally**.

## Core doctrine terms

### Social pressure
**Social pressure** is any procedural state in which progress, safety, access, cooperation, standing, or conflict outcome depends on influencing the choices, attitudes, permissions, expectations, or relationships of people, groups, audiences, or institutions.

### Leverage
**Leverage** is any factor that can materially change the willingness, risk tolerance, incentives, fear, obligations, or public posture of a social target.
Leverage is broader than eloquence.

### Attitude
**Attitude** is the current general disposition a target holds toward a person, group, proposal, or issue.
Attitude affects openness but does not by itself determine compliance.

### Request
A **request** is an asked-for action, concession, omission, endorsement, permission, or relationship change sought from a target.

### Concession
A **concession** is any yielded point, softened stance, temporary allowance, partial agreement, or altered posture granted under social pressure.

### Compliance
**Compliance** is the degree to which a target will actually do what is being sought.
A target can comply without trusting, approving, or agreeing.

### Trust
**Trust** is the degree to which a target believes another party is reliable, sincere, predictable, safe, or aligned.
Trust is not the same as attitude, obedience, or reputation.

### Obligation
**Obligation** is a socially recognized debt, duty, expectation, vow, favor balance, or binding commitment created or activated through social procedure.

### Legitimacy
**Legitimacy** is the recognized right to speak, decide, demand, rule, arbitrate, judge, represent, or enter a social procedure with authority.
Legitimacy is distinct from popularity and from reputation.

### Reputation
**Reputation** is the persistent social standing of a person or group as understood by a broader social field.
Reputation may be positive, negative, feared, controversial, respected, distrusted, or unknown.

### Standing
**Standing** is the current operational position a person or group holds within a social environment.
Standing may derive from reputation, legitimacy, obligations, past conduct, faction alignment, or public perception.

### Audience
An **audience** is any observing group whose reactions matter procedurally, whether or not they are the formal decision-maker.
An audience may be public, private, institutional, factional, distributed, or remote.

### Social risk
**Social risk** is the danger that a social attempt will create suspicion, backlash, hostility, exposure, embarrassment, scandal, obligation, retaliation, or degraded standing even if it gains partial compliance.

### Social topology
**Social topology** is the procedural shape of the social interaction.
Examples include one-to-one, one-to-many, many-to-many, mediated settlement, institutional hearing, rumor network, or proxy/emissary interaction.

### Social lead output
A **social lead output** is any actionable next state produced by social procedure, such as a favor owed, a contact gained, an introduction, a truce, a sanction threat, a recruitment attempt, a public narrative shift, or a new pressure point.

## Doctrine commitments

### 1. Social pressure is the spine of the file
Astra must not reduce social procedure to “roll persuasion.”
The primary procedural question is how social pressure is being applied, what leverage is available, what social shape the interaction takes, and what kind of outcome is actually being produced.

### 2. Social state must remain plural
Astra must not collapse attitude, compliance, trust, obligation, legitimacy, and reputation into one scalar relationship score.
These are distinct procedural layers and must remain distinct.

### 3. Leverage must remain broader than charm
Astra must support proof, gifts, food, medicine, trade, blackmail, pity, fear, kinship, authority, ritual right, military credibility, institutional backing, favors, hospitality, public embarrassment, and similar vectors as lawful social pressure.

### 4. Social outcomes must remain broader than yes/no
Astra must support partial agreements, delayed compliance, fearful compliance, negotiated alternatives, public/private splits, face-saving outcomes, obligation creation, standing changes, and fallout.

### 5. Scene pressure and long-term standing must remain distinct
Astra must differentiate immediate social procedure from persistent relationship, reputation, and institutional-memory consequences.

### 6. Public and private outcomes must remain distinguishable
Winning over a room is not the same as swaying the decision-maker.
Securing a private promise is not the same as changing public narrative.
Astra must preserve this distinction.

### 7. Strange and indirect play must remain lawful
Players will triangulate through third parties, use resources as leverage, split audiences, weaponize rumor, exploit title or protocol, turn one obligation into another, or deliberately accept backlash for a desired result.
B10 must treat those as supported procedure patterns, not edge-case breakdowns.

### 8. Donor pressure must be preserved
Astra must not flatten social-heavy, intrigue-heavy, faction-heavy, court-heavy, espionage-heavy, diplomacy-heavy, or horror-social systems into generic charm checks.
Astra must also not inflate lightweight systems into overbuilt politics simulators where the donor does not require them.

## Social-state doctrine

### Core rule
Social procedure in Astra is not governed by a single relationship value.
It is governed by interacting social states.

### Attitude state
Attitude tracks disposition toward a person, proposal, or issue.
Astra should support attitude states such as:
- hostile,
- resistant,
- wary,
- neutral,
- receptive,
- favorable,
- allied.

Attitude affects openness, but it does not by itself guarantee action.

### Compliance state
Compliance tracks what the target is actually prepared to do.
Astra should support compliance states such as:
- refusal,
- resistance,
- hesitation,
- provisional willingness,
- conditional compliance,
- active compliance,
- committed support.

A target may comply while remaining hostile.
A target may like the actor while still refusing.

### Trust state
Trust tracks whether the actor is believed, relied upon, or treated as safe.
Astra should support trust states such as:
- distrust,
- guarded tolerance,
- neutral uncertainty,
- cautious trust,
- strong trust,
- dependence.

Trust often changes more slowly than attitude.

### Obligation state
Obligation tracks debts, favors, duties, promises, vows, and commitments.
Astra should support obligation states such as:
- none,
- soft expectation,
- acknowledged favor,
- active debt,
- binding commitment,
- called obligation,
- broken obligation.

Obligation can exist even under poor attitude.

### Legitimacy state
Legitimacy tracks recognized authority, jurisdiction, right of access, or procedural standing.
Astra should support legitimacy states such as:
- no standing,
- tolerated standing,
- recognized standing,
- empowered standing,
- superior or binding authority,
- disputed legitimacy,
- revoked legitimacy.

Legitimacy can alter what kinds of requests or demands are even thinkable.

### Reputation and standing state
Reputation and standing track how a broader social environment receives the actor.
Astra should support states or profiles such as:
- unknown,
- known,
- respected,
- trusted,
- feared,
- controversial,
- infamous,
- discredited,
- politically protected,
- politically exposed.

Reputation is not necessarily linear.
A person may be feared and respected simultaneously.

## Leverage-vector doctrine

### Core rule
Leverage is any factor that can change social outcome probability, posture, cost, or persistence.
Astra must not assume speech alone is the normal or superior social vector.

### Leverage-vector families
Astra recognizes at least the following leverage families.

#### Alignment leverage
Shared values, common goals, ideology, kinship, oath, tradition, grief, faith, or mutual enemy.

#### Informational leverage
Proof, secrets, testimony, records, confessions, contradictions, blackmail material, corroboration, expert interpretation.

#### Material leverage
Money, gifts, food, medicine, services, trade, transport, supplies, access rights, debt relief.

#### Authority leverage
Title, office, legal standing, ritual right, command authority, institutional backing, delegated jurisdiction.

#### Social-capital leverage
Introductions, endorsements, testimonials, witness support, faction alignment, elite sponsorship, kin networks.

#### Threat leverage
Fear, intimidation, retaliation risk, exposure risk, military superiority, sanction threat, social ruin.

#### Hospitality and care leverage
Shelter, treatment, healing, cooking, rescue, protection, mercy, funeral rites, aid under crisis.

#### Performance and framing leverage
Public speaking, spectacle, honor displays, evidence presentation, ritualized conduct, face-saving narrative control.

#### Time and urgency leverage
Deadlines, crises, impending attack, expiring opportunity, disease spread, collapsing trust, procedural clocks.

#### Structural leverage
Control of process, venue, audience, agenda, access sequence, who speaks first, who is excluded, what evidence is recognized.

### Leverage doctrine commitments
- Leverage may be direct or indirect.
- Leverage may be visible to the target, visible only to the audience, or concealed.
- Leverage may improve compliance while worsening trust.
- Leverage may create obligation even when it fails to secure immediate agreement.
- Different leverage vectors may stack, conflict, or undermine one another.

## Social-topology doctrine

### Core rule
Not all social procedures have the same shape.
Astra must preserve the procedural difference between private persuasion, public influence, formal hearings, mediated settlement, and distributed rumor pressure.

### Social topology families

#### One-to-one private pressure
A direct interaction between one actor and one target with limited observer impact.

#### One-to-many audience pressure
One actor attempts to shape a crowd, room, jury, congregation, network, or gathering.

#### Many-to-many negotiation
Multiple parties pursue overlapping, competing, or contingent outcomes.

#### Mediated settlement
A third party attempts to broker peace, trade, compromise, or face-saving settlement among others.

#### Institutional hearing
The relevant target is embedded in protocol, office, law, guild rule, tribunal structure, or formal procedure.

#### Rumor-network pressure
Influence propagates through rumor, gossip, public narrative, witnesses, or social contagion rather than one direct exchange.

#### Proxy or emissary interaction
The actor works through a representative, intermediary, sponsor, advocate, fixer, herald, translator, or subordinate.

### Topology commitments
Different topologies change:
- who the real target is,
- which leverage vectors matter,
- how public/private outcomes split,
- how reputation moves,
- and what fallout will persist.

## Social-procedure families

### Social procedure is plural
Astra should support distinct but interoperable social families rather than one generic talk action.

### Impression
Attempting to improve receptivity, soften resistance, create goodwill, or establish social footing.

### Request
Seeking permission, aid, access, information, noninterference, or action from a target.

### Bargain
Exchanging concessions, goods, terms, or future commitments.

### Coerce
Securing compliance through fear, threat, pressure, force implication, or overwhelming asymmetry.

### Mediate
Attempting to reconcile rivals, broker terms, maintain balance, or redirect conflict into structure.

### Frame / perform
Shaping public perception, narrative, legitimacy, morale, or audience interpretation.

### Inquire socially
Obtaining information through contact, rumor, gatekeeping, witness handling, networking, or social access.

### Petition / advocate
Appealing upward through protocol, institution, law, rite, title, or recognized process.

### Recruit / align
Attempting to secure allegiance, enlistment, ideological commitment, or recurring support.

### Manage fallout
Containing scandal, restoring standing, renegotiating broken trust, repairing obligation, or reframing failure.

## Awareness vs compliance vs trust vs obligation doctrine

### Core rule
These social layers must not be collapsed into one another.

- A target may agree without trusting.
- A target may trust without agreeing.
- A target may comply because of fear.
- A target may publicly refuse while privately accepting an obligation.
- An audience may change its view while the formal decision-maker does not.
- Reputation may shift even when the immediate request fails.

Any donor construct that blurs these should be mapped carefully rather than copied as hidden Astra law.

## Social risk and fallout doctrine

### Core rule
Every social attempt has a fallout profile.
Even success may create costs.

### Social risk families
Astra should support at least:
- offense risk,
- exposure risk,
- public embarrassment,
- scandal creation,
- obligation burden,
- retaliation risk,
- reputation loss,
- distrust growth,
- institutional attention,
- suspicion or surveillance,
- future noncompliance,
- rivalry activation.

### Fallout commitments
- Coerced compliance often creates future hostility.
- Public victory can weaken private trust.
- Secret bargains may fail publicly if exposed.
- Short-term gain can worsen long-term standing.
- Some leverage vectors work once but permanently damage future options.

## Lawful incomplete mapping

### Core rule
At corpus scale, donor systems will often imply meaningful social pressure without fully specifying every threshold, state, or outcome branch.
Astra must therefore support lawful incomplete mapping rather than false certainty.

### Allowed incomplete mapping states

#### Directly mapped
The donor construct maps cleanly into Astra’s social-pressure grammar.

#### Normalized
The donor construct is preserved in meaning but re-expressed through Astra-native state distinctions.

#### Topology-assigned
The donor construct is underdefined in detail but its social shape is clear enough to classify into one-to-one, audience, negotiation, mediation, institutional, rumor-network, or proxy form.

#### Standing-assigned
The donor construct clearly affects reputation, trust, obligation, legitimacy, or compliance, but not enough detail exists to fully specify the whole state interaction.

#### Placeholder-outcome classified
The donor construct clearly produces a social lead or outcome class, but the exact persistence, magnitude, or fallout remains reserved for later content.

#### Quarantined pending doctrine
The donor construct depends on a later or absent doctrine layer and cannot yet be mapped honestly without invention.

## Anti-hallucination rules

### 1. Do not equate eloquence with leverage
A socially elegant argument is not automatically the strongest available vector.

### 2. Do not equate compliance with trust
A coercive success is not the same as a genuine alliance.

### 3. Do not equate popularity with authority
A beloved actor may lack standing.
A lawful officeholder may be widely hated.

### 4. Do not equate one target with the real audience
Some scenes are really about the observers, institution, or rumor field.

### 5. Do not equate immediate victory with stable standing
A short-term gain can create long-term weakness.

### 6. Do not collapse social shape
Convincing one guard, winning a senate chamber, brokering peace, and suppressing scandal are not the same procedural shape.

### 7. Do not invent certainty
If the donor leaves social persistence, legitimacy effect, or obligation structure unclear, do not fabricate confidence.
Use lawful incomplete mapping.
