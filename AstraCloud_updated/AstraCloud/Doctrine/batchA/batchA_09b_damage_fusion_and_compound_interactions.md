## Status
Draft — Reserved Expansion

## Authority
This file is the authoritative doctrine file for Astra's **compound-harm and fused-family interaction architecture**.

It defines:
- the distinction between ordinary multi-family harm, compound harm, and fused harm,
- the default Astra rule that multi-family harm does not generate emergent behavior unless explicitly defined,
- how compound behavior is classified as source-local, environment-local, state-local, or doctrine-promoted,
- how compound events are resolved when multiple damage families are present,
- how mixed resistance, vulnerability, immunity, absorption, restoration, conversion, and related interaction states are handled in compound contexts,
- how carrier-, target-, environment-, and order-sensitive compound behavior may be represented,
- how compound events may create source-local or doctrine-level secondary states,
- how higher-arity compound events are handled without automatic escalation into invented fusion logic,
- and how donor-specific compound harm behavior is normalized without polluting Astra doctrine.

It does **not** define:
- Astra's base damage-family taxonomy,
- ordinary resistance architecture,
- the full meaning of conditions or effect states,
- ability-object schema,
- resource-economy or backlash law,
- or a universal catalog of every possible family combination.

Those belong to other doctrine files.

## 1. Purpose

`batchA_09` establishes what a damage family is, what interaction states exist, and how ordinary multi-family harm is classified.

This file exists for the next problem:
**what happens when the coexistence of multiple damage families produces behavior that is more than ordinary multi-family harm.**

Astra therefore uses this file to answer questions such as:
- when is a multi-family event merely multi-family,
- when does it become compound,
- when does it become fused,
- when may one family modify the behavior of another,
- when may a compound event create a secondary persistent state,
- and when does repeated source-local behavior deserve doctrine-level promotion.

This file exists so Astra can recognize and resolve compound harm without:
- inventing fusion behavior by vibe,
- turning every mixed packet into a new canonical family,
- or letting donor spectacle rewrite the base taxonomy.

## 3. Core Compound-Harm Principles

### 3.1 Ordinary multi-family harm remains the default
A harm event may carry one or more damage families.

If no explicit compound rule exists, Astra resolves the event as **ordinary multi-family harm**.

No emergent behavior is inferred solely because multiple families are present.

### 3.2 Compound behavior must be explicit
Compound or fused behavior is legal only when it is explicitly established by one or more of the following:
- doctrine,
- source-local rule,
- target-local rule,
- carrier-local rule,
- environment-local rule,
- or state-local rule.

If none of those define compound behavior, Astra does not invent it.

### 3.3 Base taxonomy remains in `09`
This file never redefines what a core family is.

It only governs what happens when already-classified families interact beyond ordinary coexistence.

### 3.4 Source-local by default, doctrine-promoted only when useful
Most compound behavior should remain source-local.

A compound interaction becomes doctrine-level only when it:
- recurs across multiple sources,
- solves a stable conversion problem,
- and does not distort or overcomplicate the base harm taxonomy.

### 3.5 Arity does not create behavior by itself
A compound or fused harm event may involve two or more damage families.

Astra recognizes higher-arity compound events, including arity two, three, four, and beyond.

However, no emergent behavior is inferred solely from the number of families present.

If a source does not define a higher-arity interaction, Astra resolves the event under the ordinary multi-family rules from `09`.

### 3.6 Lower-arity behavior does not imply higher-arity behavior
If a source defines behavior for Family A + Family B and also for Family B + Family C, Astra does **not** infer a doctrine-level result for Family A + Family B + Family C.

Higher-arity compound behavior must be explicit.

### 3.7 Compound behavior may be local without creating a new family
A compound event may have emergent behavior without creating a new canonical damage family.

Most compounds should remain:
- multi-family harm with source-local behavior,
- compound events with special resolution,
- or secondary-state creators,
rather than becoming newly named Astra families.

## 4. Ordinary Multi-Family Harm vs Compound Harm vs Fused Harm

### 4.1 Ordinary multi-family harm
An event is **ordinary multi-family harm** when:
- it carries more than one damage family,
- and those families do not produce explicit emergent behavior beyond lawful coexistence.

Default example shape:
- each family is checked against the target separately,
- each family contributes its own lawful outcome,
- and the event is resolved without inventing catalytic or transformed behavior.

### 4.2 Compound harm
An event is **compound harm** when:
- more than one family is present,
- and the source, target, carrier, state, or environment explicitly says the families interact in a special way,
- but that behavior remains local rather than becoming a fully promoted Astra-wide doctrine pattern.

Compound harm may:
- modify resolution order,
- alter the effective interaction state of one family because of another,
- create a source-local secondary state,
- change spread or persistence,
- or change how an event is packaged.

### 4.3 Fused harm
An event is **fused harm** when:
- multiple families interact in a stable, explicit, and emergent manner,
- and that emergent behavior is important enough to be treated as a recognized compound mode rather than merely co-present families.

Fused harm may still remain source-local.
It does **not** automatically become a new canonical family.

### 4.4 Promotion threshold
A compound or fused pattern should only be promoted from source-local behavior to doctrine-level behavior when it is repeated, useful, and stable.

A flashy donor combo is not enough by itself.

## 5. Default Assumption: No Emergent Behavior Without Explicit Rule

### 5.1 Default no-hallucination rule
If a source deals multi-family harm and no explicit compound rule is defined, Astra resolves all families independently in the default `09` order.

No emergent behavior is invented.

### 5.2 Default anti-fanfiction rule
Astra does not infer that:
- two families create a new compound state,
- three families create a more dramatic version of a two-family interaction,
- or four families create a new cosmological phenomenon,
unless the source or doctrine explicitly says so.

### 5.3 Untyped or structural harm remains separate unless stated otherwise
Untyped, structural, generic, integrity, hull, shock, stress, or provisional harm may coexist with typed families.

Their coexistence does not automatically produce fusion behavior.

## 6. Resolution Order for Compound and Multi-Family Harm

### 6.1 Default ordinary multi-family order
Unless a compound rule says otherwise, Astra resolves a multi-family harm event in the following order:

1. Identify all damage families present.
2. Determine whether any explicit compound rule applies.
3. If no explicit compound rule applies, resolve each family independently under `09`.
4. Combine the resulting source-local packet according to the originating effect or carrier.
5. Apply any downstream state packages or non-damage consequences through `08`.

### 6.2 Compound override rule
If a valid compound rule exists, it may override the ordinary separate-family order for that specific event.

The override must be explicit.

### 6.3 Order-sensitive compounds
If the order of family interaction matters, that order must be defined by doctrine or source.

Order sensitivity is never inferred automatically.

### 6.4 Separate resolution remains the baseline
Even in high-arity events, Astra defaults to separate family resolution unless a valid compound rule states otherwise.

## 7. Mixed Interaction-State Handling

### 7.1 Mixed profiles are legal
A target may respond differently to different families in the same event.

For example, it may be:
- resistant to one family,
- vulnerable to another,
- immune to a third,
- and restored by a fourth.

This does not break the framework.

### 7.2 Default mixed-state rule
When no explicit compound rule changes the process, each family is checked against the target's lawful interaction state independently.

### 7.3 Interaction states remain family-bound unless explicitly linked
Resistance, vulnerability, immunity, absorption, restoration, conversion, flat reduction, and similar interaction states do not automatically spill from one family onto another in the same event.

Cross-family interaction only occurs when explicitly defined.

### 7.4 Compound interaction may alter another family's outcome only by rule
A compound rule may state that one family:
- intensifies another,
- weakens another,
- changes another's carrier behavior,
- changes another's persistence,
- or changes another's effective interaction state.

If no such rule exists, the families remain independently resolved.

### 7.5 Defense layers remain distinct
Armor, shielding, soak, guard, DR, barriers, temporary ablatives, and similar defense layers may modify incoming harm but are not themselves damage families.

Compound behavior may reference defense layers, but may not erase the distinction between family interaction law and defense-layer law.

## 8. Compound-State and Secondary-State Creation

### 8.1 Compound events may create secondary states
A compound or fused event may create a secondary state package when the source or doctrine explicitly defines such behavior.

### 8.2 Secondary states are not automatic
The presence of multiple families does not automatically create:
- a new condition,
- a new track,
- a new environmental state,
- or a new ongoing affliction.

Those must be explicitly defined.

### 8.3 Secondary-state ownership
A secondary state may be:
- actor-bound,
- item-bound,
- frame-bound,
- zone-bound,
- link-bound,
- scene-bound,
- or structure-bound,
according to the source rule.

### 8.4 Secondary-state meaning remains downstream
If a compound event creates a state package, the meaning and lifecycle of that state package still resolve through `batchA_08_status_condition_and_effect_architecture.md`.

This file only governs that such a state may arise from compound interaction.

### 8.5 Persistent compound states should usually remain local
Most secondary states produced by compounds should remain source-local unless repeated enough to justify doctrine promotion.

## 9. Carrier-, Target-, and Environment-Sensitive Compounds

### 9.1 Carrier sensitivity
Compound behavior may depend on carrier type, such as:
- strike,
- beam,
- cloud,
- field,
- contamination vector,
- aura,
- zone,
- barrier,
- or embedded object.

Carrier sensitivity must be explicit.

### 9.2 Target sensitivity
Compound behavior may depend on target class, such as:
- biological actor,
- spiritual actor,
- machine,
- item,
- armor,
- vehicle,
- barrier,
- site,
- or environmental structure.

### 9.3 Environment sensitivity
Compound behavior may depend on environmental context, such as:
- conductive medium,
- volatile medium,
- corrupted terrain,
- sanctified space,
- vacuum-like conditions,
- submerged conditions,
- ritual field,
- or unstable reality region.

Environment-sensitive compounds are legal, but must be explicit.

### 9.4 Nested carriers are legal
A compound effect may live on one carrier and repeatedly create secondary effects on another.

For example:
- a zone may repeatedly apply actor conditions,
- an item may inflict compound harm on its wielder,
- or a scene state may alter all incoming harm events of certain families.

This remains lawful if the carrier relationship is explicitly defined.

## 10. Source-Local vs Doctrine-Promoted Compound Behavior

### 10.1 Source-local default
The default assumption is that compound behavior is source-local.

This keeps Astra from turning every donor-specific combo into permanent doctrine.

### 10.2 Doctrine-promotion criteria
A source-local compound behavior may be promoted into broader Astra doctrine only when it:
- appears repeatedly across donor material,
- maps cleanly across multiple systems,
- helps conversion more than it harms clarity,
- and does not require rewriting the base family taxonomy.

### 10.3 Promotion restraint rule
Even repeated spectacle is not enough.

Astra should promote only the patterns that improve durable conversion architecture.

### 10.4 New canonical families are rare
Most compound behaviors should not become new canonical families.

A new canonical family should be the exception, not the reward for sounding dramatic.

## 11. Compound Arity Rule

### 11.1 Arity-neutral doctrine
A compound or fused harm event may involve two or more families.

Astra handles compound behavior through a single doctrine logic that scales by arity rather than by creating separate cosmological castes for every family count.

### 11.2 Arity categories
Astra may recognize, for descriptive purposes:
- arity-1 events,
- arity-2 compounds,
- arity-3 compounds,
- arity-4 compounds,
- and higher-arity events.

These are classification aids, not separate doctrinal species with automatic new law.

### 11.3 No automatic escalation by arity
Higher arity does not automatically create stronger, stranger, or more canonical compound behavior.

A three-family event is not automatically more emergent than a two-family event.
A four-family event is not automatically a fused doctrine event.

### 11.4 Safe default for higher arity
Unless a source-local or doctrine-level rule explicitly defines higher-arity compound behavior, all families in a multi-family event resolve independently under `batchA_09_damage_family_and_resistance_taxonomy.md`.

## 12. Donor Mapping Rules

### 12.1 Donor language must be classified before being honored
If a donor presents language such as:
- plasma burn,
- soulfire,
- entropy frost,
- warp lightning,
- corruption flame,
- sanctified void,
- hellshock,
- phase-acid,
- or similar fused phrases,
Astra does not automatically treat that phrase as a new canonical family.

First determine whether the donor is describing:
- ordinary multi-family harm,
- source-local compound behavior,
- a source-local secondary state,
- a carrier-specific interaction,
- or a truly repeated pattern worthy of doctrine promotion.

### 12.2 Map first, promote later
The correct default is:
map the donor language into Astra families and local behavior,
then decide whether any broader doctrine promotion is justified.

### 12.3 Quarantine overfit language
If a donor phrase is too specific, too setting-bound, or too ornamental to justify doctrine promotion, it remains source-local.

### 12.4 No mandatory inheritance from donor cosmology
A donor's metaphysical explanation for why two families interact does not automatically become Astra canon.

Astra may preserve the behavior without importing the donor's worldview.

## 14. Core Doctrine Summary

Astra recognizes that a harm event may carry multiple damage families.
By default, such an event remains ordinary multi-family harm and is resolved without inventing emergent behavior.

Compound or fused behavior exists only when explicitly established by doctrine or by a valid local rule tied to source, carrier, target, state, or environment.

Higher-arity events are legal, but no new behavior is inferred solely from the number of families present.

Most compound behavior remains source-local.
Only repeated, useful, conversion-stable patterns should be promoted into broader Astra doctrine.
