## Authority
This file is the authoritative doctrine file for the **Astra resource, cost, recharge, upkeep, exhaustion, debt, and backlash architecture** for activated functions.

It defines:
- the economy-side doctrine surrounding ability objects and other activated functions,
- the families of resource carriers Astra recognizes,
- how costs may be paid, reserved, occupied, delayed, staged, or deferred,
- how cadence, recharge, replenishment, upkeep, and refresh are represented,
- how overextension, denial, drain, oversubscription, debt, and backlash are classified,
- and how donor resource economies are mapped into Astra without collapsing into one donor-native model.

It does **not** define:
- lawful access or gate satisfaction,
- practical capability,
- ability payload structure,
- the meaning of referenced conditions or damage families,
- full progression law,
- or donor-specific numerical tables for every resource system.

If any later Batch A file appears to contradict this file on the **economy, cadence, reservation, upkeep, refresh, overuse, or catastrophe architecture surrounding activated functions**, this file governs unless deliberately revised.

---

## 1. Purpose

Astra requires a distinct doctrine layer for the economy surrounding activated functions.

Many donor systems treat this layer badly. They hide cost, cadence, recharge, fuel, upkeep, burnout, and catastrophe inside ability text, item text, combat rules, or progression notes, then act surprised when the framework turns into fermented soup.

This file exists to prevent that.

Its purpose is to define how an activated function, maintained mode, installed module, ritual, item function, platform routine, or similar construct becomes:
- available,
- committed,
- paid for,
- reserved,
- sustained,
- refreshed,
- denied,
- overextended,
- or catastrophically mishandled.

This file therefore governs the **economy around activated functions**, not merely one mana pool and not merely one cooldown model.

## 3. Core Resource-Economy Principles

### 3.1 Economy surrounds activated function
Astra treats resource economy as a layer that surrounds an activated function or maintained state.

The same ability object may therefore be:
- lawfully available,
- structurally valid,
- and still unusable, unstable, suppressed, exhausted, hazardous, or debt-laden under the current resource state.

### 3.2 No single universal carrier
Astra does not assume that all activated functions are paid for by one universal pool.

Donor systems may express economy through:
- points,
- slots,
- charges,
- once-per-interval cadence,
- cooldowns,
- recharge windows,
- occupied bandwidth,
- maintained concentration,
- action commitment,
- bodily strain,
- heat,
- corruption,
- catalyst consumption,
- token spend,
- tag burn,
- progress tracks,
- debt,
- or donor-local hybrids.

### 3.3 Reservation is distinct from expenditure
Astra distinguishes between:
- **spent cost**, which is consumed and gone,
- **reserved capacity**, which is tied up or committed while a state persists,
- and **ongoing upkeep**, which must continue to be paid to maintain an effect or mode.

These are related, but they are not interchangeable.

### 3.4 Economy includes quantity, state, and legality-of-use pressure
A resource carrier is not defined only by how much exists.

It may also have:
- quality state,
- stability state,
- contamination state,
- calibration state,
- corruption state,
- keying/alignment state,
- occupancy state,
- or donor-local conditions that affect whether the resource is fit to use.

### 3.5 Economy pressure includes opportunity cost
Not all cost is a pool or charge.

Economy pressure may come from:
- action consumption,
- stance commitment,
- reaction lockout,
- hand occupancy,
- concentration burden,
- attention bandwidth,
- positional sacrifice,
- body-state commitment,
- or other opportunity and commitment costs.

### 3.6 Economy-side consequence families are distinct
Astra distinguishes between several negative economy-side families, including:
- exhaustion,
- debt,
- denial,
- contamination,
- misfire,
- instability,
- backlash,
- and oversubscription.

These may interact, but they are not the same thing.

## 4. Resource Carriers and Cost Families

### 4.1 Resource carriers
A **resource carrier** is the thing that holds, expresses, or limits economy-side availability.

A resource carrier may be:
- an internal pool,
- a charge bank,
- a slot family,
- a cooldown state,
- a once-per-interval flag,
- a maintained reservation,
- a bodily-risk channel,
- a fuel stock,
- an item charge set,
- a platform-energy system,
- a crew pool,
- a catalyst inventory,
- a bandwidth track,
- a tag family,
- a token bank,
- a progress or stability track,
- a debt meter,
- or another lawful donor-local carrier.

### 4.2 Cost families
A cost may be expressed as one or more of the following families:
- pool spend,
- charge spend,
- slot use,
- cadence spend,
- cooldown initiation,
- reservation/occupancy,
- upkeep/maintenance,
- action commitment,
- bodily risk,
- self-damage,
- self-debuff,
- instability gain,
- corruption gain,
- debt accrual,
- catalyst or consumable spend,
- platform resource spend,
- crew/shared resource spend,
- and donor-local special cost.

### 4.3 Value forms
Resource and cost values may be represented as:
- numeric,
- stepped,
- banded,
- categorical,
- state-based,
- clock-based,
- tokenized,
- tagged,
- or hybrid.

### 4.4 Costless but cadence-bound functions
A function may have no explicit spend cost and still be limited by:
- once-per-interval cadence,
- trigger-state requirement,
- installed-state requirement,
- cooldown state,
- hazard threshold,
- or another non-spend limiting structure.

Astra recognizes this as lawful resource architecture.

## 5. Payment Timing and Trigger Timing

### 5.1 Timing matters
Astra does not assume all cost is paid at declaration.

### 5.2 Payment timing classes
A cost may be:
- immediate,
- prepaid,
- reserved on declaration,
- paid on activation,
- paid on successful resolution,
- paid on hit or contact,
- paid on completion,
- paid on sustain tick,
- paid on ending a maintained state,
- paid on interruption,
- paid on overpush,
- paid only on failure,
- paid only on catastrophic trigger,
- delayed,
- staged,
- conditional,
- or donor-local in timing.

### 5.3 Staged and contingent payment
Some functions pay in stages.

Examples of staged structure include:
- reserve capacity now, pay upkeep later,
- activate now, pay consequence later,
- pay basic cost now and hazard cost only if overextended,
- or pay a closing cost when the state ends.

### 5.4 Trigger-linked cost
A cost may be linked to:
- declaration,
- target acquisition,
- effect landing,
- trigger window opening,
- sustained occupancy,
- broken concentration,
- or another event in the function lifecycle.

## 6. Availability States and Cadence Models

### 6.1 Availability states
An activated function or economy-bearing subsystem may be in states such as:
- ready,
- available,
- limited,
- reserved,
- occupied,
- spent,
- cooling,
- recharging,
- suppressed,
- locked,
- unstable,
- exhausted,
- overdrawn,
- denied,
- contaminated,
- hazardous,
- or donor-local equivalents.

### 6.2 Cadence models
Astra supports cadence patterns such as:
- at-will,
- limited-use,
- charge-based,
- cooldown-based,
- recharge-window,
- once-per-round,
- once-per-scene,
- once-per-rest,
- once-per-day,
- once-per-trigger,
- upkeep-bound,
- occupancy-bound,
- and donor-local cadence structures.

### 6.3 Safe use versus hazard threshold
A function may be:
- safely usable,
- usable with rising risk,
- or catastrophically unsafe beyond a threshold.

Astra therefore distinguishes normal-use threshold, hazardous-use threshold, and overdraw threshold where needed.

### 6.4 State-limited use
A function may be unavailable not because the carrier is empty, but because the holder, platform, or environment is in the wrong state.

Examples include:
- wrong stance,
- occupied concentration,
- jammed channel,
- wrong mode,
- damaged platform,
- suppressed ritual field,
- or donor-local state conflicts.

## 7. Recharge, Refresh, and Replenishment Models

### 7.1 Refresh is not one thing
Astra recognizes that resource recovery may occur in many lawful ways.

### 7.2 Recharge classes
A function or carrier may refresh by:
- automatic time passage,
- recharge roll,
- round or tick reset,
- scene reset,
- rest cycle,
- once-per-day reset,
- maintenance action,
- catalyst replacement,
- replenishment trigger,
- kill/harvest/absorb event,
- environmental recharge,
- platform support,
- crew support,
- external aid,
- successful alignment/calibration,
- or donor-local recovery behavior.

### 7.3 Replenishment doctrine
Astra recognizes **replenishment** as a distinct recovery mode in which a function or carrier is restored by a defined trigger, event, conversion, or support process rather than by simple time passage.

### 7.4 Partial and graded refresh
Refresh may be:
- full,
- partial,
- conditional,
- degraded,
- unstable,
- delayed,
- or contaminated.

A resource returning is not automatically a resource returning in ideal condition.

## 8. Reservation, Maintenance, and Occupancy Logic

### 8.1 Reservation doctrine
Some activated functions do not merely spend a cost.

They reserve:
- slot space,
- concentration space,
- bandwidth,
- active drone control,
- attunement capacity,
- installed module space,
- stance commitment,
- summon control capacity,
- or other economy-bearing occupancy.

### 8.2 Occupancy is not expenditure
Reserved or occupied capacity may remain unavailable to other functions even though it has not been consumed.

### 8.3 Upkeep and sustain
A maintained function may require:
- recurring payment,
- recurring affirmation,
- recurring concentration,
- periodic calibration,
- continuing crew or platform support,
- or other sustain conditions.

### 8.4 Maintenance-state pressure
A function may remain active only while the maintenance state remains valid.

If that state is broken, the function may:
- end cleanly,
- collapse,
- trigger debt,
- trigger instability,
- trigger refund logic,
- or trigger backlash, depending on donor mapping.

### 8.5 Occupancy limits and contention
An actor, platform, or site may have a finite number of maintainable, reservable, or concurrent economy commitments.

Astra therefore supports occupancy limits and contention rules rather than assuming infinite maintained states.

## 9. Overextension, Exhaustion, Debt, and Instability

### 9.1 Overextension
**Overextension** occurs when a function is pushed beyond normal safe economy bounds.

This may include:
- spending beyond safe threshold,
- extending duration without normal support,
- forcing use while suppressed,
- drawing from a depleted or contaminated carrier,
- or escalating a function beyond stable load.

### 9.2 Exhaustion
**Exhaustion** is a state of reduced future capacity caused by use, strain, or overspend.

It may manifest as:
- reduced availability,
- worsened recovery,
- lowered safe-use threshold,
- temporary function lockout,
- or donor-local fatigue states.

### 9.3 Debt
**Debt** is an economy-side state where use now creates a delayed burden later.

Debt may include:
- cooldown debt,
- delayed strain,
- future action penalty,
- post-scene collapse,
- healing burden,
- delayed corruption,
- deferred recoil,
- destabilization,
- or donor-local aftercare obligation.

Debt is related to backlash, but it is not identical to immediate catastrophe.

### 9.4 Instability
**Instability** is a state where future use becomes more volatile, less reliable, or more hazardous due to present or cumulative pressure.

### 9.5 Hazard families remain distinct
Astra distinguishes:
- safe use,
- strained use,
- overextended use,
- exhausted state,
- unstable state,
- and catastrophic failure.

These should not be flattened into one generic "tired" bucket.

## 10. Backlash, Misfire, and Catastrophic Consequence Families

### 10.1 Backlash doctrine
**Backlash** is a formal family of negative economy-side consequences triggered by misuse, overextension, unstable activation, hazardous carriers, or catastrophic failure conditions.

### 10.2 Backlash is not the same thing as ordinary failure
A function may fail without backlash.

A function may also succeed and still produce economy-side consequence if the donor model allows hazardous success.

### 10.3 Backlash families
Backlash may manifest as:
- self-damage,
- self-debuff,
- self-stun or lockout,
- carrier corruption,
- contamination spread,
- function burnout,
- involuntary discharge,
- unstable transformation,
- environmental spillover,
- target misassignment,
- debt spike,
- exhaustion spike,
- or donor-local catastrophe.

### 10.4 Misfire and catastrophe
Astra distinguishes:
- **misfire**, where a function resolves incorrectly or incompletely,
- and **catastrophe**, where the outcome crosses into a major negative event family.

### 10.5 Backlash ownership
This file defines backlash as an economy-side doctrine family.

It does not define every specific condition, damage family, or narrative consequence that a given backlash instance may reference.

## 11. Shared, External, Platform, and Delegated Resources

### 11.1 Resource holder diversity
A resource carrier may belong to:
- an actor,
- an item,
- a relic,
- an implant,
- a frame,
- a vehicle,
- a drone,
- a summon,
- a site,
- a ritual environment,
- a crew,
- an institution,
- a network,
- or another lawful donor-local holder.

### 11.2 Shared pools
Multiple actors or functions may draw from one shared carrier.

### 11.3 Delegated and platform-bound spend
A function may be held by one subject but paid for by another.

Examples include:
- actor activates, platform pays,
- crew issues command, ship power pays,
- user invokes relic, relic charge pays,
- summon acts, controller bandwidth pays,
- or donor-local cross-holder arrangements.

### 11.4 Contention and priority
When multiple functions or actors draw from the same carrier, Astra recognizes:
- contention,
- priority order,
- occupancy conflict,
- oversubscription,
- and arbitration rules.

A shared pool is not automatically a free buffet with dramatic lighting.

## 12. Denial, Drain, Theft, Suppression, and Contamination

### 12.1 External denial exists
Resource availability may be altered by external effects.

### 12.2 Denial families
External economy pressure may include:
- suppression,
- jamming,
- drain,
- theft,
- lockout,
- premature cooldown,
- recharge denial,
- contamination,
- corruption,
- desynchronization,
- bandwidth theft,
- catalyst spoilage,
- or donor-local denial states.

### 12.3 Suppressed is not empty
A suppressed carrier is not necessarily depleted.

A function may be unavailable because the carrier is jammed, sealed, corrupted, or denied rather than because its quantity is zero.

### 12.4 Quality-sensitive availability
A carrier may remain present but unusable because it is:
- tainted,
- unstable,
- improperly keyed,
- misaligned,
- uncoded,
- uncalibrated,
- or otherwise in the wrong quality state.

## 13. Substitution, Mitigation, and Conversion Rules

### 13.1 Substitution doctrine
Astra allows donor mapping in which one cost carrier may substitute for another under governed rules.

### 13.2 Lawful substitution examples
Possible structures include:
- health instead of pool,
- debt instead of immediate spend,
- charge instead of cooldown,
- catalyst instead of strain,
- crew support instead of platform strain,
- stability loss instead of resource spend,
- or donor-local lawful conversions.

### 13.3 Mitigation
Astra recognizes mitigation structures such as:
- catalyst to reduce backlash,
- support action to reduce upkeep,
- calibration to improve recharge,
- insulation to reduce contamination risk,
- crew assist to reduce shared strain,
- or donor-local damping mechanisms.

### 13.4 Conversion is not free improvisation
Substitution and mitigation must remain rule-governed.

Astra does not assume every resource model can be casually converted into every other one just because a donor would like a loophole.

## 14. Failure, Interruption, Refund, and Partial-Payment Handling

### 14.1 Failed activation handling must be explicit
A function that does not resolve as intended still interacts with economy doctrine.

### 14.2 Failure-handling classes
A failed, interrupted, resisted, countered, or illegal activation may:
- consume full cost,
- consume partial cost,
- reserve but not spend,
- trigger delayed cost,
- refund cost,
- convert cost into debt,
- trigger instability,
- or follow donor-local consequence rules.

### 14.3 Interrupted maintenance
If a maintained state is interrupted, Astra must allow lawful handling for:
- immediate collapse,
- graceful end,
- partial refund,
- no refund,
- backlash trigger,
- debt trigger,
- or donor-local interruption law.

### 14.4 Illegal attempt handling
Attempted use without lawful access belongs to `06`, but `07b` may still need to represent whether an illegal attempt has economy-side effects such as waste, lockout, contamination, or backlash.

## 15. Concurrency, Stacking, and Active-Instance Pressure

### 15.1 Concurrency exists at the economy layer
An actor or platform may lawfully own many functions but lack sufficient economy capacity to run them all at once.

### 15.2 Concurrent-maintenance pressure
Astra must support the idea that several active states may compete for:
- concentration,
- bandwidth,
- channels,
- reserved slots,
- summon control capacity,
- installation space,
- active stance occupancy,
- or donor-local concurrency limits.

### 15.3 Stacking versus oversubscription
Stacking several economy-bearing states is not automatically lawful.

Astra must allow doctrine for:
- lawful coexistence,
- diminishing returns,
- replacement,
- occupancy conflict,
- and oversubscription penalties or failures.

### 15.4 Function families may share one carrier
Multiple related functions may draw from one carrier and therefore interact even when their individual ability records are distinct.

## 16. Donor Mapping Rules

### 16.1 Classify by economy behavior, not donor label
Before importing a donor construct, Astra must determine whether the donor mechanic is actually:
- a cost carrier,
- a reservation structure,
- a cooldown state,
- a cadence limiter,
- a maintenance obligation,
- a debt structure,
- a hazard threshold,
- a backlash family,
- a denial state,
- or a mixed construct that must be split.

### 16.2 Common donor mapping families
At minimum, Astra must be able to map:
- mana/energy/psi pools,
- spell slots and prepared slots,
- charges and ammunition,
- heat and overclock systems,
- cooldown timers and recharge rolls,
- once-per-scene/rest/day cadence,
- concentration and attunement occupancy,
- tag burn and token spend,
- progress-clock or threshold resources,
- strain/stress/corruption models,
- platform fuel and crew pools,
- catalyst/component systems,
- and debt/aftercare systems.

### 16.3 Attribute-less and descriptor-heavy donors
A donor does not need a classical numerical pool to map into Astra economy doctrine.

State tags, charges, clocks, debt flags, consumed descriptors, and similar carriers remain lawful if their behavior can be classified into Astra's economy architecture.

### 16.4 Split mixed donor constructs when necessary
If one donor mechanic bundles:
- access,
- cost,
- resolution,
- payload,
- and backlash
into one block, Astra should split that mechanic across the relevant files rather than importing the bundle whole.
