# batchA_05_competency_and_skill_translation_architecture.md

## Status
Draft — Core Now

## Authority
This file is the authoritative doctrine file for Astra's **capability layer** and for the translation of donor-side "skills" and related practical competency constructs into Astra-native form.

It defines:
- what Astra treats as a practical non-activated capability,
- how capabilities differ from access, abilities, resources, traits, and other neighboring structures,
- the allowed classes and expression forms of capabilities,
- how capabilities interact with resolution without becoming the resolution system,
- how nonstandard, externalized, shared, temporary, or compressed capability expressions are handled,
- and how donor-side constructs such as skills, proficiencies, approaches, descriptors, themes, tags, trades, roles, and mixed forms are classified before conversion.

It does **not** define:
- access or licensing doctrine,
- active ability schema,
- resource-spend or recharge logic,
- damage or condition logic,
- package grants in full detail,
- or a final exhaustive Astra-wide capability list.

If any later Batch A file appears to contradict this file on the **classification, ownership, or translation of practical non-activated know-how**, this file governs unless deliberately revised.

---

## 1. Purpose

Astra requires a controlled layer for practical know-how.

That layer must be broad enough to absorb donor systems that use:
- classical skill lists,
- proficiencies,
- expertise ladders,
- professions and trades,
- knowledge trees,
- approaches,
- descriptors,
- themes,
- tags,
- roles,
- or other non-classical expressions of competence.

This file exists to prevent four common failures:
- **skill-label capture**, where a donor's use of the word “skill” is treated as Astra law,
- **capability / access / ability collapse**, where training, permission, and activated effects are jammed together,
- **micro-fragmentation**, where every donor adds tiny bespoke expertise labels until the framework becomes brittle,
- and **sheet-local bias**, where all useful capability is assumed to live only inside one character's native stat block.

Astra classifies practical constructs by function first. Donor labels are evidence, not verdicts.

## 2. Scope Boundaries

### 2.1 This file must include
- doctrine for practical non-activated capability,
- capability classes and families,
- capability granularity and specialization rules,
- expression-form rules for ratings, tiers, descriptors, tags, professions, and similar donor forms,
- boundary rules separating capability from access and ability,
- doctrine for personal, shared, delegated, tool-mediated, platform-supported, and externally granted capability,
- doctrine for persistent, conditional, temporary, suppressed, replaced, and overlay-driven capability states,
- language-, literacy-, code-, and fluency-like capacities where they function as task-relevant know-how,
- reduced and compressed capability expression for nonstandard actors,
- donor mapping rules for skill-like and non-classical practical constructs,
- and translation discipline for mixed donor constructs that perform more than one doctrinal job.

### 2.2 This file must not include
- domains, licenses, schools, traditions, or other access-gating law in full,
- active abilities, maneuvers, spells, powers, cyberware actions, or technique objects,
- resource cost, cooldown, recharge, slot, fuel, or backlash systems,
- condition or effect architecture,
- full package frameworks for classes, paths, professions, or backgrounds,
- damage-family rules,
- or a universal final capability catalog for all Astra content.

### 2.3 Interpretation rule
If a question asks **what an actor knows how to do, is trained to do, can practice, can perform, can investigate, can operate, or can execute as a matter of learned practical capacity**, it belongs here.

If a question asks **whether the actor is allowed to use something, whether the actor can activate a discrete effect, how a cost is paid, or how an effect is resolved after activation**, it belongs to another file.

### 2.4 Preferred internal term
For Astra-native doctrine, this file prefers **capability** as the principal term.

The filename retains `competency_and_skill_translation` for project continuity and stack stability. The doctrine inside the file treats `capability` as the preferred Astra-native label for practical non-activated know-how.

## 3. Core Capability Principles

### 3.1 Capability is practical non-activated know-how
A **capability** is a learned, trained, practiced, or otherwise established capacity used to perform tasks, solve problems, interpret information, navigate situations, or execute procedures without requiring the construct itself to be a discrete activated effect object.

A capability may reflect:
- training,
- practice,
- expertise,
- craft knowledge,
- procedural fluency,
- technical operation,
- social technique,
- cultural understanding,
- fieldcraft,
- investigative skill,
- or similar practical capacity.

### 3.2 Capability is classified by job, not donor label
A donor may call something a skill, discipline, knack, knack tree, role, approach, descriptor, feature, or theme.

Astra does not inherit that label automatically.

Before conversion, Astra must classify whether the donor construct is actually functioning as:
- capability,
- access,
- active ability,
- resource interaction,
- trait or passive modifier,
- background or identity layer,
- or a mixed construct that must be split.

### 3.3 Capability is not access
Practical capacity does **not** automatically confer lawful access.

Examples:
- trained surgery does not automatically grant authorization for restricted nanomedicine,
- ritual literacy does not automatically authorize forbidden rites,
- vehicle operation capability does not automatically grant legal clearance to pilot restricted military frames,
- advanced hacking capability does not automatically grant network credentials or secure system access.

Capability answers **can this actor do it competently?**

Access answers **is this actor lawfully or structurally qualified to use, learn, equip, invoke, or enter it?**

### 3.4 Capability is not ability
A capability is not the same thing as a discrete activated effect object.

If a donor construct has its own:
- activation timing,
- target logic,
- range,
- duration,
- explicit outcome payload,
- usage cadence,
- recharge state,
- or independent effect text,
it is likely functioning as an **ability** rather than a capability, even if the donor calls it a skill.

### 3.5 Capability supports resolution but does not become resolution
Capabilities contribute to task adjudication, but they do not replace the resolution framework.

A capability may:
- enable a lawful attempt,
- improve likelihood of success,
- broaden what approaches are viable,
- support special handling of information or procedure,
- or justify access to a more favorable resolution posture.

A capability does not, by default, define the full adjudication engine or become a miniature spell wearing work boots.

### 3.6 Capability architecture prefers breadth with lawful specialization
Astra prefers **stable broad capability families with optional specialties** over uncontrolled micro-skill fragmentation.

A narrower split is only justified when:
- the donor requires meaningful separation,
- later conversion pressure is likely to preserve that distinction across multiple systems,
- or the merged form would become too vague to adjudicate cleanly.

### 3.7 Capability may be classical or non-classical in expression
A donor system may express practical capability through:
- skills,
- proficiencies,
- expertise bands,
- trades,
- professions,
- approaches,
- descriptors,
- themes,
- tags,
- roles,
- or other constructs.

Astra recognizes these as potential **capability carriers** without assuming they are all equivalent by default.

### 3.8 Not all useful capability is sheet-local
A capability may be:
- personal,
- shared,
- delegated,
- tool-mediated,
- platform-supported,
- crew-based,
- companion-assisted,
- environment-enabled,
- or externally granted.

Astra therefore does not assume that every meaningful practical capacity lives solely inside one humanoid character sheet.

## 4. Capability Classes

### 4.1 General capability
A broad practical field of trained performance or application.

Examples of functional shape include:
- stealth-like operation,
- investigation-like inquiry,
- medicine-like intervention,
- survival-like fieldcraft,
- diplomacy-like social handling,
- or engineering-like technical work.

A general capability should be broad enough to matter repeatedly, but not so broad that it silently absorbs unrelated disciplines.

### 4.2 Specialty
A narrower focus nested inside a broader capability family.

Specialties exist to preserve useful distinctions without forcing the framework to explode into hundreds of tiny core skills.

A specialty should:
- sharpen an area of practice,
- remain traceable to a broader capability family,
- and avoid becoming a disguised active ability.

### 4.3 Profession or trade capability
A profession-shaped or vocation-shaped expression of practical competence.

This may represent occupational bundles such as:
- medic,
- smuggler,
- machinist,
- diplomat,
- archivist,
- courtier,
- scout,
- pilot,
- ritual engineer,
- or similar donor forms.

Profession-shaped constructs may map to capability architecture without automatically becoming classes, paths, or full package layers.

### 4.4 Tool-linked capability
A capability that is meaningfully practiced through a tool, kit, instrument, interface, or device family.

Examples include:
- surgery through medical instruments,
- coding through system interfaces,
- lock work through specialist tools,
- ritual drafting through inscribed apparatus,
- vehicle operation through control systems.

Tool-linked capability is distinct from both:
- **tool access**, which belongs to access doctrine,
- and **tool abilities**, which belong to ability doctrine.

### 4.5 Knowledge-field capability
A body of applicable knowledge used in play as task-relevant know-how.

This includes fields such as:
- scholarship,
- sciences,
- occult understanding,
- legal interpretation,
- tactical knowledge,
- cultural interpretation,
- historical analysis,
- linguistic knowledge,
- and system literacy.

Knowledge capability should be grouped broadly unless finer division is genuinely necessary.

### 4.6 Social and relationship capability
Practical social capacity used to influence, interpret, manage, negotiate, direct, mislead, persuade, comfort, or navigate relationships and institutions.

Social capability is not lesser because it is not blade-shaped. It remains part of practical capability architecture when it influences adjudicated action.

### 4.7 Technical, research, and operational capability
A practical field centered on analysis, construction, procedure, experimentation, operation, maintenance, diagnosis, logistics, or systems management.

This class is particularly important for sci-fi, hybrid, and infrastructure-heavy donors.

### 4.8 Field, survival, and embodied capability
A practical field centered on embodied action or environmental procedure, including navigation, climbing, tracking, concealment, endurance practice, wilderness handling, and similar work.

### 4.9 Language, literacy, code, and fluency-like capability
Languages, literacies, codes, ritual literacy, legal fluency, cultural fluency, symbolic fluency, and similar capacities may be modeled inside the capability layer when they function as practical know-how relevant to tasks, interpretation, access preparation, or extended procedures.

These may appear as:
- standalone capabilities,
- specialty layers,
- capability-linked descriptors,
- or donor-local substructures,
so long as their job is clear.

### 4.10 Donor-local descriptor capability
Some donors use descriptors, aspects, tags, themes, or role phrases that partially function as practical capability.

A donor-local descriptor may map into capability architecture when it operates as actionable know-how or practiced method rather than merely as fiction color, identity text, or temporary scene truth.

## 5. Capability Granularity and Specialization Rules

### 5.1 Breadth-first rule
Astra prefers broad capability families first, then specialties where needed.

The framework should not create a new core capability merely because one donor names a narrow expertise separately.

### 5.2 When to split a capability
A broad capability should be split into narrower sub-capabilities or specialties only when one or more of the following are true:
- the donor or donors repeatedly preserve the distinction as mechanically meaningful,
- the merged form would be too vague to adjudicate fairly,
- the narrower distinction affects very different procedures or fiction spaces,
- or repeated conversion pressure would otherwise overload the broader capability beyond usefulness.

### 5.3 When not to split a capability
Astra should resist splitting when the pressure is merely:
- donor wording preference,
- aesthetic flavor,
- one-off setting jargon,
- or a tiny edge case that can be modeled as a specialty, descriptor, or situational qualifier instead.

### 5.4 Knowledge inflation restraint
Knowledge fields should prefer broad domains with optional specialties over endless micro-fragmentation.

A framework that creates separate permanent labels for every lore niche, repair niche, or historical splinter will become fragile and conversion-hostile.

### 5.5 Profession compression rule
Profession-shaped donor constructs may be preserved as bundled capability expressions where that better captures donor intent.

Astra should not force every profession bundle to be atomized unless doing so materially improves clarity and reuse.

## 6. Capability Expression Models

### 6.1 Lawful expression forms
Capabilities may be represented through multiple lawful forms, including:
- ratings,
- tiers,
- proficiency bands,
- expertise steps,
- specialties,
- descriptors,
- tags,
- profession bundles,
- role expressions,
- or other donor-local structures.

Astra classifies the capability by job first, then preserves the donor expression where useful.

### 6.2 Descriptor- and tag-based donors
Some donor systems do not use conventional skill lists at all.

They may express practical competence through:
- descriptors,
- aspects,
- themes,
- role tags,
- story tags,
- approaches,
- or similar freeform constructs.

These may map into Astra capability architecture when they function as practical know-how. They may also map elsewhere when they are better understood as:
- temporary narrative descriptors,
- access structures,
- passive traits,
- or mixed forms requiring split treatment.

### 6.3 Approach-style donors
Approach-like donor constructs may map into Astra as:
- capability expressions,
- style overlays,
- or mixed modifiers,
depending on whether they primarily describe method, trained practical capacity, or a broader resolution posture.

### 6.4 Representation does not determine ownership
A construct does not become capability merely because it is written as a rating, band, or tag.

Ownership still depends on doctrinal function.

## 7. Capability State and Ownership Models

### 7.1 Capability may be personal or distributed
A capability may belong primarily to:
- the actor,
- a linked frame,
- a companion,
- a crew,
- an AI assistant,
- a vehicle,
- a drone,
- a ritual apparatus,
- a med-bay,
- a toolkit,
- or another lawful external support structure.

The question is not simply **who is holding the sheet**, but **where the practical capacity is actually resident and how the actor lawfully accesses it**.

### 7.2 Shared and delegated capability
A capability may be shared or delegated across multiple participants or frames.

Examples include:
- crew operations,
- coordinated medical teams,
- drone-assisted technical work,
- bonded-companion sensory handling,
- platform-supported targeting or navigation,
- or ritual circles with multiple contributors.

Shared capability does not erase individual capability. It modifies how practical performance is sourced and adjudicated.

### 7.3 Tool-mediated and platform-supported capability
A capability may depend on tools, platforms, shells, or infrastructure.

The capability still belongs in this file when it represents know-how or practiced operation. The access to the relevant tool or platform belongs elsewhere.

### 7.4 Persistent, conditional, temporary, and suppressed capability
Capabilities may be:
- persistent,
- conditional,
- temporary,
- externally granted,
- overlay-granted,
- suppressed,
- replaced,
- or partially disabled.

This covers donor pressures such as:
- downloaded training,
- temporary battle modes,
- polymorph states,
- possession,
- curses,
- silence-like suppression,
- interface lockdown,
- injury,
- system damage,
- or environmental interference.

### 7.5 Baseline capability profile and active capability profile
Astra distinguishes:
- **baseline capability profile** — the actor's ordinary or standing capability expression,
- **active capability profile** — the currently operative expression during play,
- **overlay capability state** — a temporary modifier, replacement, or supplement that changes the active capability profile.

This distinction prevents later files from assuming all capabilities are static forever.

## 8. Boundary Rules: Capability vs Access vs Ability

### 8.1 Capability
Capability answers questions like:
- what does the actor know how to do,
- what has the actor practiced,
- what procedures can the actor perform competently,
- what fields can the actor interpret,
- what practical work can the actor attempt meaningfully.

### 8.2 Access
Access answers questions like:
- is the actor authorized, licensed, attuned, trained, cleared, qualified, or lawfully eligible,
- can the actor use this restricted tool, tradition, implant, ritual, or subsystem,
- is a gate satisfied.

Access does not by itself describe practiced execution quality.

### 8.3 Ability
Ability answers questions like:
- what discrete effect can the actor invoke,
- what activation produces a rules payload,
- what maneuver, spell, technique, cyberware action, or power object is being used.

Ability is effect-bearing. Capability is practice-bearing.

### 8.4 Split mixed donor constructs rather than importing them whole
If a donor-side construct performs more than one doctrinal job, Astra should split it.

For example, a donor “skill” might include:
- a practical field of training,
- permission to use certain equipment,
- and a menu of activated tricks.

Astra should not import that whole knot as one capability. It should split the practical, access, and ability components into their proper homes.

### 8.5 Capability does not grant effect payload by default
A capability may justify that an actor can attempt something or do it better.

It does not, by default, create:
- automatic effect text,
- special target logic,
- duration text,
- recharge logic,
- or status application payload.

If such content exists, another subsystem must own it.

## 9. Capability Use in Resolution

### 9.1 Capability informs resolution without replacing it
Capabilities interface with `batchA_03_resolution_framework.md` by helping determine:
- whether an attempt is meaningful,
- what kind of test or procedure applies,
- what level of preparation or expertise is present,
- what special interpretation or tool use is possible,
- and whether certain extended procedures can be advanced efficiently.

### 9.2 Atomic and extended use
Capabilities may matter in:
- **atomic resolution events** such as one-off tests, contests, or saves,
- and **extended or progressive structures** such as research, crafting, surgery, cultivation work, hacking operations, infiltration prep, diplomacy campaigns, engineering projects, or ritual sequences.

This file therefore supports both immediate and long-form applications of practical capacity.

### 9.3 Capability and lawful attempt doctrine
A capability may be required to make a lawful meaningful attempt in some situations.

In other situations, lack of capability may still permit an attempt but with degraded posture, higher uncertainty, greater risk, or weaker consequence control.

This file defines the architecture for such distinctions. Exact subsystem procedures belong elsewhere.

## 10. Reduced and Nonstandard Actors

### 10.1 Full player expression is canonical, not universal
The full capability architecture is the canonical player-reference model.

Not every actor needs to expose it in full detail.

### 10.2 Bundled and compressed capability expressions
NPCs, hazards, drones, vehicles, summons, environmental systems, and other nonstandard actors may use:
- bundled capabilities,
- inferred capability expressions,
- reduced capability profiles,
- or specialized capability blocks,
so long as they still map lawfully into the same architecture.

### 10.3 Specialized frames and external systems
A vehicle, drone, ritual circle, AI system, or bonded companion may carry capability expressions that are not identical to a humanoid player sheet.

That does not place them outside the doctrine. It means Astra must support specialized expressions without pretending everything is one neat list of skills on one neat body.

## 11. Donor Mapping Rules

### 11.1 First classification rule
When a donor presents a “skill” or similar construct, Astra must first ask:
- Is this a broad practical capacity?
- Is it an access gate?
- Is it an active ability?
- Is it a passive trait or boon?
- Is it a resource interaction?
- Is it a background or identity element?
- Is it a mixed construct that needs to be split?

No donor label overrides this step.

### 11.2 Donor forms Astra must be ready to classify
Astra capability architecture must be able to classify donor constructs presented as:
- skills,
- proficiencies,
- expertise ladders,
- disciplines,
- knowledges,
- trades,
- roles,
- approaches,
- descriptors,
- themes,
- tags,
- profession bundles,
- tool suites,
- language packages,
- and similar practical structures.

### 11.3 Mixed-construct handling rule
If a donor construct combines:
- practical capacity,
- permission logic,
- and activated effects,
Astra should split the construct into multiple Astra-native pieces rather than preserving the donor knot intact.

### 11.4 Descriptor and narrative truth caution
Not every descriptor, aspect, tag, or theme is a capability.

Some are:
- temporary narrative truths,
- identity declarations,
- status markers,
- access carriers,
- or scene-state descriptors.

Only those functioning as practical know-how or practiced method should map here.

### 11.5 Breadth-first conversion preference
When multiple lawful mapping options exist, Astra should prefer:
- a stable broad capability plus specialty structure,
- over uncontrolled proliferation of narrow permanent skills.

### 11.6 Donor preservation without donor capture
Astra should preserve meaningful donor distinctions where they matter.

Astra should not preserve donor terminology or donor fragmentation automatically when doing so would pollute the core architecture.

## 13. Non-Negotiable Doctrine Rules

1. A donor's use of the word **skill** does not control Astra classification.
2. Capability is the preferred Astra-native term for practical non-activated know-how.
3. Practical capacity does not automatically confer lawful access.
4. A capability does not become an ability merely because it is learned, upgraded, or called a skill by a donor.
5. Mixed donor constructs must be split when they perform multiple doctrinal jobs.
6. Descriptor-, tag-, theme-, and approach-based donors are lawful inputs, but they must still be classified by function.
7. Capabilities may be personal, shared, delegated, externalized, tool-mediated, platform-supported, temporary, suppressed, or overlay-granted.
8. Languages, literacies, codes, and fluencies may belong to the capability layer when they function as task-relevant know-how.
9. Capabilities may apply to atomic tests and extended progress structures alike.
10. Astra prefers broad stable capability families with specialties over uncontrolled micro-fragmentation.
