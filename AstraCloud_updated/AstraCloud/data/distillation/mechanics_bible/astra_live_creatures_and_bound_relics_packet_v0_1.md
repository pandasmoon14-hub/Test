# Astra Ascension — Creatures and Bound Relics Packet v0.1

## Scope and ownership

This packet is the live Batch C umbrella for Astra-facing creature, entity, resource, and bound-growth object doctrine.

It does **not** own every Batch C object class by itself. Its job is to establish the shared grammar that must survive both:

- organic ecologies,
- anomalous or spiritual ecologies,
- manufactured or installed asset ecologies,
- and owner-bonded growth objects.

This packet therefore sits above narrower file owners such as creature taxonomy, harvest and treasures, and bound relic doctrine. It should remain broad enough to host pressure from RHBF-style creature/harvest/relic loops and Shadowrun-style augmentation/device/platform/entity pressure without collapsing those unlike donor structures into one donor-shaped baseline.

## Cross-donor design law

Astra canonizes the **grammar** of creatures, entities, resource sources, and bound-growth objects before it canonizes donor-default packages.

This means:

- family, category, or classification must do mechanical work,
- resource sources must extend beyond slain actors,
- growth objects may evolve through typed inputs,
- organic and manufactured ecologies are both lawful,
- exact donor numbers, exact donor catalog names, and exact donor default posture bundles are not promoted automatically.

## Taxonomy and classification doctrine

Astra creatures and entities are not only narrative labels. Their family or category identity should perform mechanical work.

A classification can imply:
- likely damage or condition posture,
- likely vulnerabilities or restoration interactions,
- likely environmental or network dependencies,
- likely behavior under morale, pressure, or control disruption,
- likely harvest, salvage, or extraction outputs,
- likely compatibility with crafting, feeding, installation, or refinement loops.

### Seed classifications

Astra should be able to host at least the following broad families or categories:

- aberrant beings,
- constructs,
- plant entities,
- elementals,
- undead,
- astral beings,
- beast lineages,
- engineered hybrids,
- summoned or compiled entities,
- installed or platform-linked support entities,
- manufactured security or proxy actors.

The exact default package attached to a family or category remains open to donor pressure. Family **must** do mechanical work, but exact donor-default immunity, vulnerability, or posture packages are not yet Astra baseline.

## Stable actor and entity record doctrine

Every Astra actor or entity entry should preserve a stable record structure so the pipeline can validate and compare outputs cleanly.

A useful baseline record keeps:

**Name**  
**Category / Family**  
**Role / Function**  
**Attributes or Ratings**  
**Number**  
**Presence Pressure or equivalent threat signal**  
**Resistances / Defenses / Integrity posture**  
**Vitality / Structure / Stability state**  
**Defense Threshold or equivalent target profile**  
**Speed / Mobility / Access mode**  
**Stealth / Detection / Signal posture**  
**Size / Footprint / Platform class**  
**Actions**  
**Traits / Systems / Permissions**  
**Attacks / Techniques / Programs / Effects**

Not every donor uses every field. The important point is that Astra maintains a stable comparison grammar across living creatures, summoned entities, constructs, platforms, and other lawful actor records.

## Organic, anomalous, and manufactured ecologies

Astra must support more than one ecological model.

### Organic ecology

Organic ecologies include beasts, ascendant beasts, plant entities, mutated organisms, magical predators, and other living or once-living actors. These often pressure:
- family-driven posture,
- harvest categories,
- territorial or biome-linked behavior,
- and progression-significant natural treasure loops.

### Anomalous or spiritual ecology

Anomalous ecologies include astral beings, void-adjacent entities, undead, oath residues, dream entities, compiled or invoked intelligences, and similar actors. These often pressure:
- unusual resistance posture,
- identity-bearing residue,
- conditional manifestation,
- and extraction or containment rules beyond ordinary biology.

### Manufactured or installed ecology

Manufactured ecologies include constructs, security actors, rated devices, augment packages, support platforms, drones, credential-bearing assets, and other intentionally produced systems. These often pressure:
- installation or host-cost logic,
- platform versus operator separation,
- rated component hierarchy,
- salvage rather than harvest,
- and permission or access law rather than beast-family instinct.

Astra should support all three without forcing them into one donor’s worldview.

## Ascendant Beasts and thresholded actors

Some beasts or baseline entities do not merely grow stronger. They cross a threshold of identity, selfhood, authority, or transformative capacity.

An **Ascendant Beast** is one important example of this doctrine. Such actors are excellent bridges between:
- monster ecology,
- regional rulership,
- unusual faction actors,
- and late-game NPC design.

### Signs of thresholded ascent

An actor is a candidate for thresholded ascent doctrine when several of the following are true:
- it claims or defends territory with intent rather than instinct alone,
- it develops named Techniques, systems, or repeatable strategic behaviors,
- it acquires social, symbolic, ritual, or network significance,
- it can alter or partially refine its form,
- its remains, residue, codebase, or systems are unusually potent or identity-bearing,
- it can bargain, threaten, command, or build subordinate ecosystems around itself.

Not every elite beast, construct, or summoned entity is thresholded. The difference is not just power. It is **thresholded selfhood**.

## Resource-source ontology

Astra should not collapse into “monsters drop loot.” Every serious encounter, expedition, breach, or site should imply possible aftermath value, but the source of that value varies.

Lawful resource sources include:
- actors,
- sites,
- environments,
- anomalies,
- objects,
- and mixed sources.

### Actor-based outputs

Actor-derived outputs may include:
- blood,
- hide,
- claws,
- fangs,
- organs,
- cores,
- glands,
- spores,
- essence-bearing residues,
- rare bloodline matter,
- structural plating,
- astral fragments,
- soul-bearing components,
- code fragments,
- broken subsystems,
- authentication shards,
- or embedded hardware.

### Site- and environment-based outputs

Site or environment extraction may come from:
- plants,
- terrain,
- weather events,
- astral fractures,
- ruined infrastructure,
- dead god-sites,
- living biomes,
- faction-controlled extractive zones,
- decaying networks,
- foundries,
- or contaminated districts.

### Object- and anomaly-based outputs

Object- or anomaly-derived outputs may include:
- credential assets,
- sealed caches,
- resonance-bearing devices,
- artifact fragments,
- paradox matter,
- phase-stable materials,
- vow or memory residue,
- or lawful salvage from destroyed platforms.

## Harvest, salvage, and extraction doctrine

Astra should maintain a typed aftermath loop rather than a vague treasure pile.

A useful first-pass extraction sequence is:

1. identify what can be taken,
2. determine what tools, permissions, or expertise are required,
3. check stability, spoil risk, contamination risk, or alert risk,
4. extract in categories rather than as generic loot,
5. record how the result can be used:
   - consumed,
   - refined,
   - crafted,
   - installed,
   - fed into a bound-growth object,
   - stored for base operations,
   - exchanged through faction or contract channels.

### Extraction categories

To stay extensible across many donor systems, extraction outputs should belong to one or more broad categories:

- **Biological**: hide, blood, organs, venom, bone, tendon, seed, gland
- **Structural**: plating, stone-shell, alloy growth, crystal lattice, frame fragments, casing
- **Energetic**: cores, charged residue, storm-nodes, radiant ash, battery clusters, power cells
- **Spiritual**: soul shards, mnemonic residue, vow-echoes, dream pollen, oath fibers
- **Environmental**: growths, mineral blooms, biome salts, furnace ash, glacial glass, swamp resins
- **Anomalous**: paradox matter, time-bent fragments, nullstone, corruption pearls, phase silk
- **Technical**: processors, encrypted chips, sensor suites, identity keys, logic boards, interface assemblies
- **Credential / Access**: warrants, seals, coded permissions, passkeys, claim markers, authority fragments

These categories are deliberately broad so later donor systems with stranger resource economies do not break the system.

## Natural treasures and non-ordinary value objects

Natural Treasures are progression-significant value objects found in the world rather than manufactured through ordinary processes.

They are broader than simple monster harvests and broader than simple sale-value treasure. Their importance may be alchemical, spiritual, developmental, factional, ritual, or bound-growth related.

Natural Treasures may come from:
- creatures,
- plants,
- terrain,
- weather events,
- astral fractures,
- ruined infrastructure,
- living biomes,
- sanctified or profaned sites,
- and other high-pressure extraction zones.

Astra should preserve Natural Treasure as a meaningful object class while keeping exact donor reserve taxonomies, exact yield triplets, and exact numerical semantics provisional unless later donor pressure supports promotion.

## Bound-growth object doctrine

A **Bound Relic** is a major Astra example of a bound-growth object.

Bound-growth objects:
- are identity-bearing,
- are tied to an owner, bearer, lineage, or lawful permission state,
- may evolve through typed inputs,
- may solve combat, utility, navigation, defense, social, or factional problems,
- and may change their trait set as they grow.

Astra should preserve the grammar of bound-growth through typed feeding, inputs, milestones, or evolution triggers without automatically adopting one donor’s exact caps, bonus ladders, or component names.

### Bound Relic baseline

A Bound Relic may be:
- weapon-shaped,
- implement-shaped,
- tool-shaped,
- focus-shaped,
- companion-linked,
- or otherwise identity-bearing and growth-capable.

The important cross-donor law is the grammar of bonded growth, not the exact RHBF profile.

## Bound-growth versus augmentation and installed systems

Astra must keep these object classes distinct.

A Bound Relic is not the same thing as:
- an installed augmentation,
- a rated device,
- a credential asset,
- a platform subsystem,
- or a temporary consumable.

These classes may interact, but they should not collapse into one universal item model.

A good Astra distinction is:
- **Bound-growth objects** change through identity-bearing relation and typed feed or milestone logic.
- **Installed systems / augmentations** change through installation, replacement, compatibility, and host-cost or permission logic.
- **Rated devices / platforms** change through rating, subsystem loadout, access law, maintenance, and operator-platform relation.

## Reward ecology doctrine

Astra reward ecology must remain broad enough for:
- harvest loops,
- salvage loops,
- natural treasure loops,
- credential and access rewards,
- faction claim rewards,
- platform recovery,
- augmentation recovery or installation opportunity,
- and bound-growth progression.

This prevents Batch C from overfitting either to RHBF’s creature-harvest ecology or to Shadowrun’s manufactured/installable ecology.

## Anti-drift posture

This packet should explicitly resist the following drifts:

- treating one donor’s family defaults as universal Astra posture,
- treating all reward logic as slain-creature extraction,
- treating all valuable objects as market treasure,
- treating Bound Relics as the only meaningful growth-object class,
- treating manufactured systems as if they were merely construct-harvest variants,
- or letting donor catalog names outrank Astra schema grammar.

## Immediate cross-donor carry-forward

The following are strong cross-donor carry-forwards at the packet level:

- family or category doing mechanical work,
- stable actor/entity record grammar,
- resource-source ontology broader than slain actors,
- typed aftermath and extraction categories,
- natural treasure as progression-significant object class,
- bound-growth object grammar,
- and support for both organic and manufactured ecologies.

The following remain optional, donor-local, or provisional until wider donor pressure resolves them:

- exact family default posture bundles,
- exact reserve taxonomies,
- exact bound-growth caps and bonus ladders,
- exact item catalog names,
- exact manufactured-asset rating laws,
- exact host-cost math,
- and donor-specific progression payloads.
