# Astra Credential and Access Assets v0.1

Derived from cross-donor consolidation after RHBF, Shadowrun, and Lancer.

## Scope of this file

This file owns Astra's **credential and access asset grammar**.

It defines:
- what a credential asset is,
- what an access-shaping asset is,
- how credential/access assets differ from actor-side access law,
- how they differ from platform records, ordinary gear, and purely social standing,
- what kinds of credential/access assets Astra currently recognizes,
- how validity, scope, sponsorship, and revocation should be represented,
- and what remains optional-profile support or donor-local retention.

It does **not** own:
- full access-law doctrine,
- full faction or institution procedure,
- full legal-state systems,
- exact donor permit stacks,
- exact donor SIN/identity architecture,
- exact Matrix or host logic,
- exact travel-token economies,
- exact faction sponsorship tables,
- or exact civic/military bureaucracy catalogs.

This is an object-side grammar owner for access-shaping assets, not a full legal simulation and not a full actor-side authorization file.

## Why this file exists

The first three donors establish that Astra cannot treat all permission-bearing objects as either:
- pure flavor,
- mere money,
- or indistinguishable from actor-side access states.

RHBF proves that Astra must support:
- explicit access instruments,
- traversal or entry objects,
- opportunity-gating objects,
- and asset-side permission structures that matter in play.

Shadowrun proves that Astra must also support:
- credential-bearing objects,
- permits,
- legality-linked assets,
- identity-linked objects,
- and access-shaping items whose consequences depend on verification, exposure, and institutional response.

Lancer reinforces that:
- authorization-backed acquisition matters,
- mission-side support assets may shape access or deployment,
- and object-side permission structures cannot simply be folded into Path, competence, or role identity.

The stable conclusion is:

> Astra needs a Batch C owner for credential-bearing and access-shaping assets that remains distinct from actor-side access law, but interoperates with it cleanly.

## Core credential law

A **credential asset** is an object, record, token, permit, warrant, charter, seal, code, registry entry, or comparable asset that materially affects what access can be exercised, recognized, tolerated, verified, or contested.

An **access asset** is any object-side thing whose primary significance lies in:
- granting,
- signaling,
- carrying,
- proving,
- brokering,
- stabilizing,
- extending,
- narrowing,
- or mediating access.

The stable rule is:

> Actor-side authorization says whether access exists as a state.  
> Credential/access assets say what object-side carrier, proof, key, permit, token, or mediated instrument is involved.

Astra must preserve both layers.

---

## Credential/access assets are not the same as other doctrine owners

### Credential asset is not access law
Access law answers:
- whether an actor is allowed, keyed, licensed, attuned, connected, or recognized.

Credential/access assets answer:
- what object or record carries, proves, conveys, or mediates that relationship.

The two interact, but they are not identical.

### Credential asset is not simple wealth
A credential may be bought, forged, granted, inherited, requisitioned, earned, or bonded.

Its value does not reduce to sale price alone.

### Credential asset is not standing
Standing is a durable relational posture.
A credential asset is an object-side carrier or mediator.

Standing may influence whether a credential is granted.
A credential may influence how standing is exercised.
Neither collapses into the other.

### Credential asset is not ordinary gear
A keycard, transit seal, permit, bonded charter, gate token, deployment writ, or recognized registry object may look like “gear,” but its primary significance is access mediation, not ordinary utility.

### Credential asset is not platform doctrine
A credential may authorize a platform or channel, but it is not itself a platform record by default.

## Recognized credential/access asset families

These are the current Astra-facing families.

### 1. Identity-bearing credentials
These assets represent or mediate recognized identity.

They may include:
- identity tokens,
- registries,
- passes,
- papers,
- signatures,
- permits tied to a bearer,
- embedded identity carriers,
- or comparable recognition-bearing objects.

Shadowrun pressures this family strongly.

These should be broad enough to host civic, criminal, military, commercial, or covert identity families without making any one donor stack universal.

---

### 2. Traversal and entry instruments
These assets materially affect whether an actor may:
- enter,
- traverse,
- board,
- deploy,
- pass,
- route,
- or connect.

RHBF strongly validates this family through explicit travel/access instruments.

This family should remain broader than one donor token economy.

---

### 3. Operation and deployment authorizations
These assets shape whether an actor, group, or platform may:
- deploy,
- operate,
- carry,
- escort,
- claim,
- salvage,
- conduct business,
- or enter a restricted operation state.

Lancer and Shadowrun jointly pressure this family.

---

### 4. Permit and legality assets
These assets exist primarily to:
- render something recognized,
- tolerated,
- licensed,
- declared,
- exempted,
- or legally operable.

Shadowrun strongly validates this family.

They may apply to:
- objects,
- channels,
- routes,
- bodies,
- platforms,
- professions,
- or behaviors.

---

### 5. Sponsorship and charter assets
These assets convey access through:
- patronage,
- institutional backing,
- guild or faction approval,
- route sponsorship,
- warrant,
- or explicit charter.

These assets help mediate access without reducing everything to identity or money.

---

### 6. Channel and key assets
These assets act as:
- keys,
- ciphers,
- attunement anchors,
- codes,
- handshakes,
- gateway markers,
- relay-authorizers,
- or other channel-enabling carriers.

They may be physical, embedded, symbolic, bonded, temporary, or procedural-object hybrids.

## Required credential/access asset fields

A lawful Astra credential/access asset record should be able to identify at least the following fields where relevant:

- **identity**: what this asset is
- **family**: identity / traversal / operation / permit / sponsorship / channel-key
- **bearer relation**: who may carry, present, invoke, or claim it
- **scope**: what access or recognition it actually affects
- **verification mode**: how it is checked, recognized, or challenged
- **validity state**: active, expired, conditional, forged, unstable, revocable, contested, bonded, etc.
- **source**: who or what issued, granted, created, bonded, or authorized it
- **duration**: permanent, operation-bound, consumable, renewable, revocable, time-limited, or condition-limited
- **dependency**: what other state, location, permission, or channel it depends on
- **revocation condition**: how it can be lost, voided, exposed, or canceled
- **exposure consequence**: what happens if its status is challenged, detected, or falsified

Not every donor will use all of these fields, but this structure is broad enough to host the current donor set.

## Verification doctrine

Credential/access assets matter because they are often **verified**, not merely possessed.

Verification may be:
- passive,
- active,
- contested,
- automated,
- ritualized,
- social,
- technical,
- institutional,
- or mixed.

Astra should preserve the difference between:

- **having** a credential/access asset,
- **presenting** it,
- **being recognized through** it,
- **being challenged on** it,
- and **being exposed by** it.

The stable rule is:

> Possession is not the same as acceptance.  
> Acceptance is not the same as safety.

This matters strongly after Shadowrun, but should remain broad enough for non-cyberpunk donors.

## Validity states

A credential/access asset may be in one of several validity conditions, such as:

- valid
- expired
- revoked
- suspended
- conditional
- forged
- partial
- degraded
- borrowed
- bonded
- operation-only
- route-limited
- channel-limited
- unverified
- under dispute

Astra does not need one universal vocabulary, but it does need the grammar that validity can vary.

## Dependency doctrine

A credential/access asset may depend on:

- bearer identity,
- sponsorship,
- legal status,
- access state,
- route state,
- channel state,
- platform compatibility,
- mission phase,
- local standing,
- scrutiny level,
- trace exposure,
- location,
- time window,
- or other enabling conditions.

This prevents Astra from overfitting credential assets into “always works if possessed.”

## Credential/access assets and consequence layers

Credential/access assets often interact with:

### Standing
A respected actor may be granted stronger credential assets more easily.
A bad-standing actor may face revocation or refusal.

### Scrutiny
High scrutiny may increase checks, invalidate shortcuts, or challenge borderline assets more aggressively.

### Trace
Repeated use of the same access asset, channel, or forged identity may build trace.

### Faction pressure
Institutions and factions may:
- issue,
- deny,
- revoke,
- sponsor,
- counterfeit,
- or contest these assets.

### Tactical shells
Some shells may turn on whether a credential asset can be:
- maintained,
- delivered,
- captured,
- spoofed,
- defended,
- extracted,
- or invalidated under pressure.

## Common access outcomes mediated by assets

Credential/access assets may help mediate:

### 1. Entry
Who gets in.

### 2. Traversal
Who gets through or along a route.

### 3. Operation
Who may perform or activate something.

### 4. Acquisition
Who may lawfully obtain or claim something.

### 5. Deployment
Who may field or bring something somewhere.

### 6. Recognition
Who is believed, tolerated, or processed as legitimate.

### 7. Exception handling
Who gets to bypass, soften, or delay ordinary barriers.

## Design rules for Astra-facing credential/access assets

1. A credential/access asset should remain distinct from actor-side access state.
2. A credential/access asset should matter because it is verified, challenged, or recognized.
3. Possession, recognition, and legality should be separable.
4. Credential/access assets should be able to be forged, contested, revoked, borrowed, time-limited, or condition-bound.
5. A credential/access asset may shape routes, operations, and deployment without reducing to money.
6. A credential/access asset may apply to actor, group, platform, cargo, convoy, or channel.
7. Donor-specific bureaucracies should not outrank Astra grammar.
8. No one donor's verification architecture should become Astra universal law.

## Current Astra-facing summary

Astra now recognizes credential/access assets as a stable Batch C object family.

They may:
- carry identity,
- mediate entry or traversal,
- authorize operation or deployment,
- encode legality or exception,
- convey sponsorship or charter,
- or function as keys to restricted channels.

The final rule is simple:

> If an object matters primarily because it proves, carries, brokers, stabilizes, or contests access, Astra should treat it as a credential/access asset rather than flattening it into ordinary gear or actor-side permission alone.
