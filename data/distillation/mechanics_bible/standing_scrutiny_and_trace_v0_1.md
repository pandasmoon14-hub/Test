# Astra Standing, Scrutiny, and Trace Doctrine v0.1

Derived from cross-donor consolidation after RHBF, Shadowrun, and Lancer.

## Scope of this file

This file owns Astra's **standing, scrutiny, and trace doctrine**.

It defines:
- what standing is,
- what scrutiny is,
- what trace pressure is,
- how these layers differ from one another,
- how they interact with faction pressure, access law, contracts, tactical shells, and broader operation loops,
- what kinds of consequence states Astra currently recognizes,
- and what remains optional-profile support or donor-local retention.

It does **not** own:
- full faction doctrine,
- full access-law doctrine,
- exact legal-state catalogs,
- exact police/host/security subsystems,
- exact bounty tables,
- exact Overwatch-style math,
- exact donor-specific notoriety or heat numbers,
- or exact public-prestige economies.

This is a grammar owner for consequence layers, not a donor-specific score sheet.

---

## Why this file exists

The first three donors establish that Astra should not collapse all social, institutional, and operational aftermath into one undifferentiated reputation score.

RHBF proves that Astra must support:
- claim pressure,
- institutional response,
- world-loop consequence,
- and faction-facing aftermath that can shape future opportunity.

Shadowrun proves this more sharply by separating:
- longer-horizon standing and relationship pressure,
- immediate suspicion, heat, and institutional attention,
- and technical or operational exposure that can escalate independently.

Lancer reinforces that:
- operational aftermath matters,
- sponsorship and claimant relationships matter,
- and structured operations can create consequence layers that are not identical to simple moral reputation.

The stable conclusion is:

> Astra needs separate owners for durable standing, active scrutiny, and trace-like escalation pressure.

## Core distinction law

Astra currently recognizes three major consequence families.

### 1. Standing
Standing is a relatively durable relationship state between an actor and a social, institutional, factional, civic, military, professional, or operational body.

Standing answers questions like:
- how the actor is regarded,
- whether the actor is trusted,
- whether offers are likely,
- whether support is available,
- whether refusal or sponsorship becomes more likely,
- and whether the actor's prior conduct has durable consequences.

Standing is not necessarily public fame.  
Standing may be local, hidden, selective, institutional, or highly contextual.

---

### 2. Scrutiny
Scrutiny is active, present, or near-term attention directed toward an actor, group, operation, route, asset, or location.

Scrutiny answers questions like:
- who is watching right now,
- how aggressively systems or institutions are paying attention,
- whether response posture is tightening,
- whether movement is becoming harder,
- whether access is being narrowed,
- and whether intervention is becoming imminent.

Scrutiny is usually more immediate and situational than standing.

---

### 3. Trace
Trace is cumulative exposure pressure tied to operational visibility, technical signature, procedural leakage, evidentiary accumulation, or comparable pursuit vectors.

Trace answers questions like:
- how much actionable residue an operation has created,
- whether the actor or asset can be followed, identified, triangulated, or modeled,
- whether escalation is approaching because too much signal has accumulated,
- and whether continued action through the same channel is becoming unsafe.

Trace is not identical to moral blame, institutional trust, or public prestige.
It is an exposure/escalation family.

---

## Why these layers must remain separate

Astra should preserve these distinctions because they answer different questions.

An actor may have:
- high standing with one faction,
- high scrutiny from another,
- and low trace because the last operation was clean.

An actor may have:
- low standing,
- low scrutiny,
- but high trace because an operation is leaving technical or procedural evidence.

An actor may have:
- good standing with a sponsor,
- high scrutiny in a district,
- and rising trace in one access channel only.

The stable rule is:

> Standing, scrutiny, and trace may influence one another, but they are not the same layer and should not be collapsed by default.

## Standing doctrine

### What standing owns
Standing owns durable relational posture between actors and structured bodies.

Standing may be:
- faction-facing,
- sponsor-facing,
- institutional,
- civic,
- professional,
- regional,
- military,
- underworld,
- or network-local.

Standing may affect:
- offer frequency,
- sponsorship,
- contract quality,
- refusal rates,
- access opportunities,
- intervention willingness,
- leverage strength,
- cost or leniency,
- and escalation thresholds.

### What standing should not absorb
Standing should not become:
- all legal-state logic,
- all heat/suspicion logic,
- all trace logic,
- or all general social narrative.

Standing is one durable consequence family, not the only one.

---

## Scrutiny doctrine

### What scrutiny owns
Scrutiny owns active or near-term attention pressure.

Scrutiny may come from:
- institutions,
- security bodies,
- hostile factions,
- surveillance networks,
- claimant groups,
- civil authorities,
- operational rivals,
- or local defenders.

Scrutiny may affect:
- access checks,
- checkpoint intensity,
- response time,
- patrol density,
- surveillance posture,
- sponsor hesitation,
- target hardening,
- or operational freedom.

### What scrutiny should not absorb
Scrutiny should not become:
- long-term reputation,
- full legal identity doctrine,
- or purely technical evidence accumulation.

It is attention pressure, not every consequence at once.

---

## Trace doctrine

### What trace owns
Trace owns accumulated exposure pressure tied to operational residue.

Trace may be created by:
- excessive signal,
- unclean extraction,
- repeated use of the same channel,
- technical interface residue,
- witness chains,
- leaked identifiers,
- repeated movement patterns,
- asset reuse,
- procedural sloppiness,
- or environmental marks left behind.

Trace may affect:
- future detection difficulty,
- safety of repeated methods,
- escalation speed,
- response precision,
- exposure windows,
- pursuit confidence,
- or the viability of later covert action.

### What trace should not absorb
Trace should not automatically become:
- durable faction opinion,
- morality score,
- prestige,
- or generalized institutional trust.

Trace is exposure/escalation pressure.

## Recognized consequence scales

Astra does not currently require one universal number line for each family.

Instead, this file recognizes several lawful consequence-scale forms.

### 1. Tiered qualitative states
Examples:
- low / moderate / high
- tolerated / watched / hunted
- trusted / uncertain / refused

### 2. Track-based accumulation
Examples:
- boxes,
- clocks,
- segments,
- thresholds,
- escalation bands.

### 3. Hybrid structures
Examples:
- a standing tier plus a scrutiny clock,
- a regional standing note plus a channel-specific trace meter.

The stable rule is:

> Astra may host multiple consequence-scale forms without forcing one donor's exact math into universal law.

## Common standing families

Astra should currently support at least these standing families:

### 1. Sponsor standing
How likely sponsors, patrons, or brokers are to engage.

### 2. Faction standing
How a faction or institution regards the actor.

### 3. Regional standing
How the actor is regarded within one locale, polity, route, or district.

### 4. Professional standing
How the actor is regarded inside an operational/professional field.

### 5. Claim standing
How legitimate, tolerated, or contested the actor's claims appear.

No single donor family exhausts these.

---

## Common scrutiny families

Astra should currently support at least these scrutiny families:

### 1. Institutional scrutiny
Attention from formal power or security structures.

### 2. Operational scrutiny
Attention created by an active mission, intrusion, operation, or conflict.

### 3. Regional scrutiny
Localized attention in one district, settlement, route, or zone.

### 4. Asset-linked scrutiny
Attention tied to one item, platform, credential, convoy, or target.

### 5. Channel-linked scrutiny
Attention tied to one method of movement, interface, access, or communication.

---

## Common trace families

Astra should currently support at least these trace families:

### 1. Technical trace
Signal, network, interface, or systems residue.

### 2. Procedural trace
Repeated patterns, predictable operation flow, or recognizable method.

### 3. Witness trace
Human or social exposure chains.

### 4. Material trace
Physical residue, salvage residue, bodily trace, stripped components, marked routes, or environmental disruption.

### 5. Identity trace
Leaks tied to recognizable credentials, cover patterns, identifiers, or sponsored presence.

## Interaction rules

### Standing and scrutiny
Good standing may reduce scrutiny in some contexts.
Bad standing may amplify scrutiny.
But one does not automatically determine the other.

### Scrutiny and trace
Rising trace often creates future scrutiny.
Current scrutiny can make trace accumulation more dangerous.
But trace can exist before anyone is actively watching.

### Standing and trace
Good standing may help survive or absorb trace consequences.
High trace may damage standing later if the residue becomes attributable.
They remain distinct.

### Standing and access law
Standing may influence:
- who grants access,
- whether exceptions are tolerated,
- or how much friction an actor faces.

But standing is not access itself.

### Scrutiny and access law
High scrutiny may tighten active access conditions.

### Trace and access law
High trace may compromise one access channel without damaging every other channel equally.

### Contracts and opportunities
Successful work may improve standing while still creating trace.
Failed work may worsen standing and increase scrutiny.
Clean work may produce reward with low trace.
Messy work may succeed while creating severe consequence pressure.

### Tactical shells
How a tactical shell resolves may alter standing, scrutiny, or trace differently depending on:
- visibility,
- damage,
- witnesses,
- extraction quality,
- collateral,
- sponsor satisfaction,
- and surviving evidence.

## Consequence generation rules

Astra should ask separate questions after significant operations:

### Standing questions
- who approves,
- who resents,
- who now trusts or distrusts,
- who becomes more willing to offer work or support,
- who becomes more willing to deny or contest.

### Scrutiny questions
- who is now actively watching,
- what systems are tightening,
- where movement becomes harder,
- what channels are now dangerous.

### Trace questions
- what residue was left,
- what patterns repeated,
- what channel is now compromised,
- how much future precision the opposition gained.

This three-question split is preferable to one vague “reputation” update.

## Design rules for Astra-facing consequence layers

1. Standing should be durable but revisable.
2. Scrutiny should be immediate, directional, and situational.
3. Trace should be cumulative, channel-sensitive, and escalation-facing.
4. One successful operation should be able to improve standing while increasing trace.
5. One failed operation should be able to worsen standing without creating severe trace if it was quiet.
6. One loud operation should be able to create scrutiny even when the actor's standing remains good with a sponsor.
7. Different bodies should be allowed to hold different standing/scrutiny states simultaneously.
8. Consequence layers should be able to attach to actor, group, operation, route, asset, credential, or platform as needed.
9. No single donor score or timer should be universalized without further pressure.
