# Astra Tactical Shell Doctrine v0.1

Derived from cross-donor consolidation after RHBF, Shadowrun, and Lancer.

## Scope of this file

This file owns Astra's **tactical shell doctrine**.

It defines:
- what a tactical shell is,
- when Astra should treat a scene as a tactical shell rather than a loose encounter,
- what a tactical shell must contain,
- how shells differ from operation loops, faction pressure, and content-schema records,
- how shells interact with objectives, extraction, timing, spatial pressure, reserves, and support entities,
- and what remains optional-profile support or donor-local retention.

It does **not** own:
- full world-operation cadence,
- full mission structure,
- full faction doctrine,
- exact sitrep catalogs,
- exact deployment maps,
- exact turn economy,
- exact frame/platform math,
- exact extraction numbers,
- or exact donor-specific scenario names.

This is a procedural grammar owner, not a donor scenario catalog.

---

## Why this file exists

The first three donors establish that Astra cannot treat all operation scenes as undifferentiated combat.

RHBF proves Astra must support:
- structured confrontation spaces,
- escalating encounter pressure,
- trial-oriented scenario framing,
- and site-bound conflict states tied to larger operation loops.

Shadowrun proves Astra must support:
- layered infiltration and conflict states,
- negotiated objectives,
- conditional access pressure,
- pursuit/escalation risk,
- and scenes where success is not reducible to “defeat all enemies.”

Lancer proves this most cleanly by making structured objective-driven combat explicit:
- declared objective types,
- timed pressure,
- extraction or hold conditions,
- terrain/spatial stakes,
- and formal operation shells that are broader than “fight until one side is gone.”

The stable conclusion is:

> Astra needs a tactical shell owner for structured objective-driven conflict scenes.

---

## Core tactical-shell law

A **tactical shell** is a structured scene frame used when conflict, pressure, timing, and spatial stakes are all materially relevant to resolution.

A tactical shell is appropriate when a scene's meaning depends on more than simple opposition defeat.

A shell may be used for:
- hold/defend conditions,
- extraction/escape conditions,
- escort/movement pressure,
- control/claim pressure,
- delay/survival pressure,
- breach/infiltration under active resistance,
- recovery/salvage under threat,
- shutdown/disruption objectives,
- or comparable structured tactical problems.

The stable rule is:

> A tactical shell exists when the scene needs declared objective structure, not merely hostile presence.

---

## Tactical shell is not the same as other procedure owners

### Tactical shell is not the full operation loop
The world-operation loop owns:
- briefing,
- preparation,
- travel,
- legwork,
- operation entry,
- debrief,
- downtime,
- and broader procedural flow.

A tactical shell owns one structured conflict/problem scene **inside** that broader loop.

### Tactical shell is not faction doctrine
Factions may generate the shell, populate the shell, or influence the shell.

They do not own the shell's structural grammar.

### Tactical shell is not content schema
Maps, sites, platforms, enemies, hazards, and support assets may appear inside a shell.

Their records belong to other files.

### Tactical shell is not generic encounter prose
A shell is a formalized frame with explicit stakes, timing, and objective structure.

Not every encounter requires one.

---

## When to use a tactical shell

A tactical shell should be invoked when most of the following are true:

- the scene has a declared tactical objective,
- spatial positioning materially changes outcome,
- time pressure matters,
- the adversary or environment can respond dynamically,
- success/failure is not equivalent to simple annihilation,
- reinforcement, extraction, control, survival, or delay states matter,
- or support entities/platforms materially alter the scene structure.

If those conditions are absent, Astra may instead use looser encounter or obstacle procedure.

## Required shell components

A lawful Astra tactical shell should identify:

### 1. Objective state
What the actors are actually trying to accomplish.

Examples:
- hold,
- secure,
- escort,
- extract,
- delay,
- destroy,
- disable,
- survive,
- penetrate,
- reclaim,
- recover,
- escape.

### 2. Success condition
What must be true for the objective to count as achieved.

### 3. Failure condition
What ends the shell in loss, collapse, unacceptable compromise, or forced withdrawal.

### 4. Time pressure
What creates urgency:
- rounds,
- waves,
- countdown,
- deterioration,
- accumulation,
- exposure window,
- extraction deadline,
- or comparable pressure.

### 5. Spatial pressure
What makes location matter:
- zones,
- lines,
- anchors,
- choke points,
- threatened lanes,
- control areas,
- extraction points,
- fragile assets,
- or movement corridors.

### 6. Adversarial response
What can push back:
- hostile actors,
- hostile platforms,
- hazards,
- reinforcements,
- surveillance,
- denial systems,
- escalating clocks,
- environmental collapse,
- or institutional intervention.

### 7. Exit condition
How the shell ends:
- success,
- failure,
- forced break,
- negotiated exit,
- partial extraction,
- fallback,
- attrition threshold,
- or outside interruption.

## Common shell families

Astra does not universalize one donor catalog, but the following families are now stable enough to recognize.

### 1. Hold / defend shell
The actors must preserve a location, object, corridor, or person against pressure for a meaningful interval.

### 2. Extraction / escape shell
The actors must leave with themselves, a target, or an asset under active resistance.

### 3. Escort / transit shell
The actors must move something or someone through contested space.

### 4. Control / claim shell
The actors must seize, stabilize, or maintain control over a point, network, asset, or region of the scene.

### 5. Delay / survival shell
The actors must endure or stall until a threshold is reached.

### 6. Breach / infiltration shell
The actors must penetrate or bypass defended layers under time and response pressure.

### 7. Recovery / salvage shell
The actors must retrieve, strip, stabilize, or recover something while threat remains active.

### 8. Disruption / shutdown shell
The actors must interrupt a system, platform, ritual, node, convoy, or process before consequences lock in.

These are shell families, not fixed donor templates.

## Objective framing rules

A good tactical shell should frame the objective in operational terms, not only emotional or narrative terms.

The shell objective should answer:
- what matters,
- when it matters,
- where it matters,
- who can interfere,
- and how the shell ends.

Poor shell framing:
- “win the fight”
- “beat the enemies”

Better shell framing:
- “hold the extraction corridor until shuttle lock completes”
- “escort the asset across the breach line before surveillance escalation”
- “disable the relay before hostile reinforcement window closes”
- “recover the relic core and leave before containment failure”

The key rule is:

> Tactical shells should describe problem structure, not merely combat intent.

## Spatial pressure

A tactical shell should explicitly state what spatial relationships matter.

Spatial pressure may come from:
- defended anchors,
- fragile positions,
- moving convoys,
- firing lanes,
- sightline exposure,
- deployment constraints,
- difficult terrain,
- corridor bottlenecks,
- node-distance requirements,
- or unstable zones.

Astra should preserve spatial pressure as a shell concern without requiring one exact battle-map doctrine.

---

## Time pressure

A tactical shell should explicitly state how urgency is tracked.

Possible timing models include:
- fixed rounds,
- progressive escalation,
- wave thresholds,
- countdown clocks,
- deterioration tracks,
- event triggers,
- extraction windows,
- environmental collapse,
- or institutional response timers.

No single timing model is universal.

The important rule is:

> A shell needs real temporal pressure when the scene’s structure depends on urgency.

---

## Shell population

A tactical shell may include any mixture of:
- actors,
- platforms,
- deployables,
- hazards,
- surveillance systems,
- support entities,
- protected assets,
- civilians/noncombatants,
- extraction vehicles,
- ritual sites,
- or environmental pressures.

This reinforces that tactical shells are broader than duel logic or room-clearing combat.

---

## Shell interaction with other Astra doctrines

### With world-operation loop
The operation loop determines why the shell exists and what follows it.
The shell determines how the structured scene itself behaves.

### With faction pressure
Factions may:
- sponsor the shell,
- contest the shell,
- escalate the shell,
- or exploit the outcome.

The shell does not belong to faction doctrine.

### With access law
Some shell objectives may require:
- credentials,
- licenses,
- interface states,
- operational clearance,
- or compatible platforms.

Access law remains distinct from shell doctrine.

### With platform doctrine
Platforms, mounts, install capacity, deployables, and support entities can matter heavily inside shells.
Their object-side grammar belongs elsewhere.

### With standing/scrutiny doctrine
How cleanly or messily a shell resolves may affect later scrutiny, standing, or trace pressure.

## Optional-profile support

The following are lawful recurring shell profiles, but not universal shell law:

- exact six-sitrep catalogs,
- exact deployment-point structures,
- exact turn/round lengths,
- exact reserve-declaration windows,
- exact extraction distances,
- exact reinforcement schedules,
- exact pilot-vs-platform engagement assumptions,
- exact “combat starts now” donor triggers,
- exact mission-only cadence for every shell.

Astra should support these as donor-local or optional recurring patterns, not baseline doctrine.

## Design rules for Astra-facing shells

1. A shell should have a declared objective.
2. A shell should have meaningful failure, not only “did not kill enough enemies.”
3. A shell should expose time pressure when urgency matters.
4. A shell should expose spatial pressure when placement matters.
5. A shell should make adversarial response visible.
6. A shell should be allowed to end through extraction, collapse, fallback, or partial success.
7. A shell should not require one exact donor map or deployment doctrine.
8. A shell should not assume all actors are personal-scale organic combatants.
9. A shell should be able to host operators, platforms, support entities, hazards, and asset objectives together.
10. A shell should remain meaningful even when its participants are asymmetrical.
