# Astra Faction Pressure Engine v0.1

## Scope
This file defines the Astra-stable grammar for how organized actors create pressure, opportunities, obligations, leverage, access, and fallout in the world.

It does **not** define the full content schema for individual factions, polities, corporations, cults, crews, syndicates, fleets, houses, guilds, or institutions. It also does **not** replace broader world-activity doctrine. Instead, it clarifies the faction-facing layer inside that wider activity engine.

This cross-donor revision incorporates pressure validated by both RHBF and Shadowrun.

- RHBF proves that factional pressure must interact cleanly with opportunity structures, access structures, hub behavior, and persistent world escalation.
- Shadowrun proves that factional pressure must also support brokered work, layered loyalties, negotiated access, reputation consequences, deniable operations, and non-neutral institutional actors.

The result is a broader Astra-facing engine. Faction pressure is no longer framed as one donor’s mission-board ecology or one donor’s criminal-contract ecology. It is a general world-force grammar that can host both.

## Core law
Advancement and world movement should be tied to action under pressure, not only isolated stat gain.

Factions matter because they:
- generate opportunities,
- deny opportunities,
- gate access,
- alter consequences,
- attach obligations,
- produce allies and rivals,
- and change the operational shape of the world over time.

Astra therefore treats faction pressure as a real engine, not as flavor text layered over otherwise generic adventure structures.

## Faction pressure loop
The stable Astra-facing loop is:

1. a faction, intermediary, or institutional actor creates an offer, demand, restriction, warning, claim, or competing move,
2. the player side interprets the opportunity or threat through its current loyalties, needs, access state, and risk tolerance,
3. action is taken through direct engagement, indirection, negotiation, infiltration, competition, refusal, or counter-play,
4. the outcome changes standing, access, pressure, world-state, and future opportunity,
5. the faction field updates rather than resetting.

This means faction interaction is persistent by default. Even when a specific task ends, the faction layer should remain altered.

## Core components

### Operational Anchor
A faction-pressure engine needs a place, channel, or procedural layer through which pressure becomes legible.

This may appear as:
- a neutral or semi-neutral hub,
- a broker network,
- a settlement authority structure,
- a contract board,
- a gatekeeping institution,
- a frontier relay,
- a local patronage network,
- or a distributed intelligence / contact web.

`Nexus Hub` remains a valid Astra term for one lawful hub pattern, but Astra should not assume all faction pressure originates from a neutral central city or a single mission exchange node.

### Opportunity Structures
Faction pressure expresses itself through lawful opportunity forms. Current Astra-stable forms include:
- Faction Contract,
- Ascension Trial,
- Hunting Realm access,
- Expedition Site access,
- Mandate,
- brokered operation,
- claim dispute,
- escort or transport obligation,
- recovery or acquisition job,
- political request,
- investigative commission,
- retaliation or suppression action,
- and defensive support call.

No single donor-specific form is mandatory. The important thing is that factions can create structured work and structured obstruction.

### Access Law
Factions frequently determine what is reachable, who may enter, what may be purchased, what can be installed, what may be studied, and who is recognized as legitimate.

Access can be mediated by:
- seals,
- warrants,
- licenses,
- brokered introductions,
- contacts,
- standing thresholds,
- institutional approval,
- ownership,
- legal identity,
- technical interface,
- or territorial permission.

`Transit Seal` remains a valid optional profile. It is not the only Astra access model.

### Persistent Consequence Layers
Faction pressure should update more than immediate reward. It may change:
- standing,
- notoriety,
- heat,
- trust,
- legal exposure,
- jurisdictional access,
- market access,
- safehouse or anchor reliability,
- or future contract availability.

These layers should remain distinguishable. For example, standing and heat are often correlated, but they do not mean the same thing and should not be collapsed by default.

## Faction-facing record grammar
A faction-facing content record should be able to express at least:
- name and role,
- organizational type,
- jurisdiction or operational footprint,
- aims and claims,
- relationship posture toward player actors,
- opportunity types issued,
- access controls enforced,
- preferred leverage methods,
- escalation responses,
- allied and rival relationships,
- and persistent consequence effects.

This file owns the pressure grammar for those fields, not their final content schema. Exact record layout remains a content-schema concern elsewhere.

## Offer, denial, and leverage logic
Faction pressure does not only appear as “quests.” It also appears as:
- access granted conditionally,
- access denied until terms are met,
- rivals contesting the same objective,
- pressure applied through third parties,
- delayed payment or partial payment,
- obligation attached to aid,
- retaliation for refusal,
- and soft leverage such as social dependency, debt, or credential reliance.

Astra should preserve both direct and indirect leverage.

RHBF strongly supports access-gated opportunities and changing world conditions around faction action.
Shadowrun strongly supports brokered work, layered deniability, contacts as leverage vectors, and fallout that persists after the operation.

Both donors therefore support the same broader Astra rule: faction pressure is an engine of world change, not merely a source of jobs.

## Brokered work as lawful profile
Astra should explicitly support brokered contracts as one major faction-pressure profile.

In that profile:
- a principal, broker, fixer, patron, or intermediary frames the work,
- the actors undertaking the work may not fully own the information picture,
- deniability, layered motives, or incomplete truth may be part of the structure,
- and payout, standing, and fallout may update on separate tracks.

This should be treated as an Astra-valid pattern, but not the only faction-work pattern.

## Competing claimants and multi-sided pressure
Astra must support cases where more than one organized actor has a claim on the same site, asset, route, relic, person, platform, or investigation result.

That means faction pressure should support:
- contested rights,
- overlapping jurisdiction,
- shifting alliances,
- conditional support,
- and rival offers aimed at the same objective.

This keeps faction play from flattening into one-side-issued tasks.

## Contacts and relational access
Contacts remain a lawful bridge between Batch A and Batch B.

Batch A may treat contacts, resources, identity channels, or social capital as build pressure.
Batch B then treats those as operational levers that influence:
- information quality,
- access paths,
- broker reliability,
- emergency support,
- cover maintenance,
- market reach,
- and fallout buffering.

This file therefore assumes contacts can matter mechanically without requiring every donor to use Shadowrun’s exact contact model.

## Investigation, legwork, and prep pressure
Faction pressure often begins before direct action.

Astra should preserve these pre-operation pressures as legitimate parts of the faction engine:
- identifying who is involved,
- learning motives and constraints,
- negotiating terms,
- gaining credentials,
- securing routes,
- testing loyalty,
- arranging support,
- and discovering what happens on success, failure, or betrayal.

This means faction pressure is inseparable from information pressure in many donors, even when the two files remain distinct.

## Remote and delegated action
Some donors require faction pressure to interact with remote, delegated, or platform-mediated action.

This engine should therefore support factional relevance to:
- remote platforms,
- hired crews,
- summoned entities,
- companion actors,
- drones,
- mechs,
- vehicles,
- distributed teams,
- or other delegated assets.

This file does not solve the full operator/platform ontology. It only establishes that faction pressure must remain compatible with it.

## Base operations and return states
`Stronghold Phase` should not be treated as mandatory Astra cadence.

However, Astra should support an optional `Base Operations Overlay` or return-state support loop where faction pressure continues through:
- upkeep demands,
- territorial response,
- resource claims,
- structural support requests,
- new obligations,
- and retaliation or alliance offers.

This keeps RHBF’s useful return-loop pressure without forcing every donor into home-base management.

## Anti-drift rules
Do not let one donor’s faction ecology become Astra baseline.

Specifically:
- do not assume all faction play is issued from a neutral capital,
- do not assume all access is tokenized,
- do not assume all contracts are brokered criminal work,
- do not assume all world pressure requires a stronghold return loop,
- do not assume all factions are anti-technology, techno-optimist, religious, corporate, or state-like,
- and do not assume exact donor names, ideologies, or politics survive promotion.

Promote the grammar of leverage, offer, denial, access, consequence, and persistence. Keep donor-specific ideology, exact identities, and exact local politics donor-local unless later canonized.

## Current promoted Astra-facing constructs
The following are currently stable enough to carry as Astra-facing faction-pressure terms or patterns:
- Operational Anchor,
- Faction Contract,
- Pressure Event,
- access law as faction-facing doctrine,
- offer / denial / leverage logic,
- persistent standing and heat-like consequence layers,
- brokered work as one lawful profile,
- contested claimant structures,
- optional base-operations overlay,
- and faction pressure as a persistent world-state engine.

The following remain lawful but optional or donor-local depending on context:
- Nexus Hub as one anchor type,
- Transit Seal as one access profile,
- Mandate as one formal authority pattern,
- exact broker idioms,
- exact faction identity models,
- exact return-loop cadence,
- and exact donor politics.

## Design summary
A faction engine is not just a mission board.
It is the doctrine layer that explains how organized actors shape access, opportunity, pressure, consequence, and persistence across the world.

RHBF proves that faction pressure must integrate with access structures, world escalation, and repeatable opportunity loops.
Shadowrun proves that faction pressure must also absorb contacts, brokered work, deniable operations, layered fallout, and non-neutral institutional ecologies.

Astra therefore adopts a broader rule:
organized actors must be able to change what the world offers, what it forbids, what it demands, and what it remembers.
