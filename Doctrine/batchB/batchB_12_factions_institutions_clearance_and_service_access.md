# batchB_12_factions_institutions_clearance_and_service_access.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s doctrine for **organized gatekeeping, structured opportunity, clearance, and service access under procedural play**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a **universal organized-access layer** capable of absorbing donor systems built around factions, guilds, sects, ministries, corporations, churches, academies, embassies, hospitals, archives, tribunals, teleporter authorities, quartermasters, gatekeepers, patronage networks, and other organized actors that open or close meaningful opportunities.

This file is not merely a factions section.
It is the doctrine layer for:
- organized actors and durable organized structures,
- authority-bearing offices and gatekeeping nodes,
- venues in which access is mediated,
- channels through which access is granted, denied, contested, borrowed, or abused,
- standing, legitimacy, clearance, and service eligibility,
- membership, affiliation, sponsorship, patronage, and proxy access,
- sanctions, obligations, scrutiny, and institutional fallout,
- neutral grounds and cross-faction environments,
- and conversion of organized structures into lawful opportunity and denial outputs.

In Astra terms, this file governs how:
- groups and institutions structure opportunity rather than merely expressing opinions,
- standing differs from attitude,
- legitimacy differs from clearance,
- access differs from the effect of the thing being accessed,
- players can use rank, sponsorship, contracts, embassies, escorts, credentials, favors, or exceptions without leaving doctrine,
- temporary, partial, proxy, illegal, and contested access are handled lawfully,
- and organized structures create both opportunities and pressure without turning B12 into a lore encyclopedia or scenario-writing file.

## Core doctrine terms

### Faction
A **faction** is an organized actor with interests, resources, reach, internal structure, and the ability to create opportunity, pressure, or obstruction.
A faction may be formal or informal, local or translocal, lawful or illicit.

### Institution
An **institution** is a durable organized structure with recognized function, authority, procedures, and continuity beyond any one member.
Institutions can mediate access even when personal attitudes vary.

### Office
An **office** is a specific authority-bearing node within an institution or factional structure that can process, recommend, deny, or enforce access.
An office may be a person, post, desk, council seat, ministry branch, adjudicator, registrar, warden, or equivalent gatekeeping role.

### Venue
A **venue** is the place, site, district, chamber, checkpoint, portal, archive, clinic, exchange, embassy, hall, station, or similar context in which access is materially mediated.
A venue is not merely location flavor; it is an access environment.

### Channel
A **channel** is the actual route by which access is pursued or obtained.
Examples include formal application, sponsor introduction, escort privilege, token presentation, permit issuance, treaty claim, emergency exception, payment, bribe, credential theft, or institutional order.

### Standing
**Standing** is a medium- to long-term relational status between an actor and an organized body.
Standing affects future access, trust, tolerance, response, and obligation, but is not identical to momentary attitude.

### Attitude
**Attitude** is the present emotional or dispositional stance of an individual or body toward a person, request, proposal, or issue.
Attitude can influence an exchange without determining institutional validity.

### Legitimacy
**Legitimacy** is whether a claim, role, right, title, authority, or procedural standing is recognized as valid within the relevant organized context.
Legitimacy is not the same thing as popularity.

### Clearance
**Clearance** is the recognized procedural permission to enter, use, redeem, request, view, learn, transport through, purchase from, or otherwise interact with a controlled opportunity, service, site, or asset.

### Service access
**Service access** is the ability to lawfully use an organized capability such as treatment, training, archive use, transport, market privilege, hearing time, protection, or credential processing.

### Sponsorship
**Sponsorship** is access support granted through endorsement, responsibility, patronage, guarantee, escort, or backing by a recognized person or body.

### Patronage
**Patronage** is structured support from a more powerful actor or institution that grants opportunity, protection, legitimacy, or resource access in exchange for service, loyalty, debt, prestige, or political positioning.

### Jurisdiction
**Jurisdiction** is the recognized boundary within which an organized actor or institution can lawfully decide, enforce, authorize, or deny.

### Sanction
A **sanction** is any organized restriction, punishment, review, blacklisting, revocation, censure, escort condition, monitoring order, or similar pressure imposed by a faction or institution.

## Core principles

### Organized access principle
Groups and institutions do not merely have opinions.
They structure what can be used, entered, learned, claimed, purchased, redeemed, transported through, or protected.

### Access-effect separation principle
Access to a thing is not the same as the effect of that thing.
B12 governs access and gatekeeping.
Adjacent files govern what accessed services, routes, archives, markets, or infrastructures actually do.

### Standing-attitude-legitimacy-clearance separation principle
Standing, attitude, legitimacy, and clearance must remain distinct.
A favorable attitude may not produce lawful access.
Valid clearance may be honored even under hostile attitude.
Standing may improve without expanding clearance.
Legitimacy may be recognized without granting broader support.

### Office-venue-channel principle
A faction or institution rarely grants access in the abstract.
Access is mediated through concrete offices, venues, and channels.
This doctrine must therefore model not only who controls access, but how and where that control is exercised.

### Opportunity-and-pressure principle
Organized structures create both opportunity outputs and pressure outputs.
They can open doors, but they can also impose scrutiny, delay, debt, surveillance, sanctions, escort requirements, and long-tail fallout.

## Faction, institution, office, venue, and channel doctrine

### Factions and institutions are related but not identical
A faction is an organized actor.
An institution is a durable structured framework.
A faction may operate within an institution, control an institution, contest an institution, or depend on institutional channels.
An institution may host multiple factions, regulate them, arbitrate among them, or remain formally neutral.

### Offices are operational gatekeeping nodes
Access is often mediated through offices rather than through an entire faction or institution acting as one mind.
An office may:
- process applications,
- validate credentials,
- issue permits,
- approve or deny access,
- register claim rights,
- sponsor hearings,
- monitor restricted use,
- or enforce sanctions.

### Venues are access environments
A venue may contain its own access rules independent of broad organizational standing.
Examples include:
- restricted districts,
- clinics,
- libraries,
- archives,
- teleport hubs,
- diplomatic compounds,
- markets,
- shrines,
- academies,
- hearing chambers,
- barracks,
- vaults,
- and controlled transit gates.

### Channels define how access is actually obtained
Common channels include:
- membership,
- title or rank,
- permit or token,
- official application,
- sponsor endorsement,
- escort passage,
- payment,
- treaty recognition,
- merit qualification,
- legal exception,
- emergency authorization,
- black-market workaround,
- forged credential,
- borrowed credential,
- coercive exception,
- or captured administrative authority.

Astra must preserve donor access channels instead of flattening all access into “have enough reputation” or “make a persuasion roll.”

## Standing, attitude, legitimacy, and clearance doctrine

### Standing
Standing tracks durable organizational relationship state.
Standing may be:
- hostile,
- strained,
- neutral,
- tolerated,
- respected,
- trusted,
- indebted,
- honored,
- or similarly donor-appropriate.

Standing affects:
- expected treatment,
- willingness to process requests,
- tolerance for exception handling,
- sponsor availability,
- sanction severity,
- and future access channels.

### Attitude
Attitude is immediate and scene-sensitive.
Attitude may shift quickly through B10 procedure without necessarily altering long-term standing.
Attitude belongs in the interface between B10 and B12, not as a replacement for organizational state.

### Legitimacy
Legitimacy answers whether a claim is recognized as procedurally or normatively valid.
Astra should support legitimacy derived from:
- law,
- title,
- office,
- treaty,
- ritual right,
- inheritance,
- contract,
- recognized merit,
- emergency authority,
- or equivalent donor-native sources.

### Clearance
Clearance is permission status relative to a controlled opportunity.
Clearance may be:
- full,
- partial,
- limited by purpose,
- limited by escort,
- limited by time,
- one-use,
- revocable,
- provisional,
- inherited,
- delegated,
- or contested.

Clearance is not automatically permanent, broad, or socially approved.

## Membership, affiliation, sponsorship, and patronage doctrine

### Membership
Membership is formal or semi-formal inclusion within an organized body.
Membership may grant identity, obligations, access, resources, or authority.

### Affiliation
Affiliation is looser association that may create sympathy, recognition, or partial access without full membership rights.

### Sponsorship
Sponsorship grants access through endorsement or backing.
Sponsorship can be:
- personal,
- office-based,
- factional,
- institutional,
- temporary,
- conditional,
- or debt-bearing.

### Patronage
Patronage is structured backing with persistent consequences.
Patronage may produce:
- access,
- training rights,
- introductions,
- market privileges,
- protected lodging,
- diplomatic shielding,
- or project support,
while also creating:
- obligations,
- political positioning,
- service expectations,
- debt,
- or rivalry attention.

### Proxy access
Proxy access occurs when one actor uses another’s sponsorship, escort, permit, or office backing to enter a controlled channel.
Proxy access is lawful in some systems and suspect or illegal in others.
Astra must support both.

## Access-channel doctrine

Astra must support multiple concurrent ways of obtaining organized access.
Common access channels include:
- membership channel,

- escort channel,

- payment channel,
- reputation channel,
- merit or qualification channel,
- emergency channel,

- black-market channel,

### Access-channel compatibility
Channels may:
- reinforce one another,
- substitute for one another,
- conflict with one another,
- override one another in emergencies,
- or create layered access where one channel grants entry and another grants service use.

### No single-channel assumption
Astra must not assume that every donor body uses the same route to opportunity.
One donor may require payment.
Another may require title.
Another may require sponsor endorsement.
Another may require ritual right or one-use permit.
All of these must land lawfully.

## Temporary, partial, proxy, contested, and illegal access doctrine

### Temporary access
Temporary access is bounded by time, task, escort, or event.
It does not imply durable standing or broad clearance.

### Partial access
Partial access grants only part of a controlled opportunity.
Examples include:
- district but not archive,
- lobby but not vault,
- hearing but not vote,
- clinic intake but not restricted treatment,
- transport hub but not premium route,
- or market entry but not closed exchange.

### Proxy access
Proxy access is granted through sponsor, escort, patron, office order, treaty delegation, or equivalent support.
Proxy access may be lawful, conditional, or suspect.

### Contested access
Contested access occurs when:
- authorities disagree,
- channels conflict,
- a permit is challenged,
- a treaty claim is disputed,
- a rival office attempts override,
- or local and supralocal authorities diverge.

### Illegal or irregular access
Astra must support access that is effective but not lawful.
Examples include:
- forgery,
- bribery,
- stolen credentials,
- black-market permits,
- coerced authorization,
- administrative corruption,
- or opportunistic exploitation of crisis exceptions.

Illegal access may still create real opportunity while sharply increasing scrutiny, sanctions, or long-tail fallout.

## Personal, party, and organizational relationship scale

B12 must support organized access relationships at multiple scales:
- one individual to one office,
- one individual to one institution,
- one party to one institution,
- one party member as sponsor for another,
- many factions in one neutral venue,

## Live-play pressure and unusual player behavior

Players routinely use organized structures in indirect ways.
B12 must therefore support behaviors such as:
- triangulating one organization against another,
- using a neutral venue to bypass direct faction hostility,
- buying temporary access instead of seeking membership,
- borrowing clearance through escort or sponsor,
- seeking partial access when full access is impossible,
- weaponizing institutional conflict,
- using public legitimacy to overcome poor standing,
- using standing to secure a service without changing attitude,
- taking illegal but effective access routes,
- or deliberately accepting debt, scrutiny, or sanction risk to gain short-term opportunity.

These behaviors are not edge cases.
They are normal organized-access play at scale.

## Invariants

### Organized-opportunity preservation invariant
Conversion must preserve donor organized-access pressure.
Do not flatten title-gated, sponsor-gated, treaty-gated, permit-gated, service-mediated, or institution-heavy systems into generic “talk to the faction and maybe they say yes.”

### Access-route preservation invariant
If a donor distinguishes between membership, permit, sponsor, treaty, payment, clearance, escort, or black-market workaround, Astra must preserve that distinction unless explicit normalization is warranted.

### Standing-clearance separation invariant
Do not collapse standing, attitude, legitimacy, and clearance into one score or one response state.

### Opportunity-and-fallout invariant
Organized access must always allow room for opportunity outputs and pressure outputs.
A granted access route may still create scrutiny, debt, escort, or political heat.
A denied route may still create an alternate lead.

## Mandatory subsystem handoff map

B12 must hand off cleanly to adjacent files.

- **B10 social pressure, leverage, negotiation, trust, and immediate audience management:** B10 owns moment-to-moment social pressure. B12 owns durable organizational shells, access structures, and gatekeeping states.

- **B04 wealth, payment, requisition, and value semantics:** B04 owns economic law. B12 owns whether organized structures accept payment, permits, or subscriptions as access channels.
- **B11 downtime, scheduling, project time, and service-use timing:** B11 owns when services are used and how non-encounter time is spent. B12 owns who grants access to those services and under what channels.

## Lawful incomplete mapping and anti-hallucination rules

Astra must allow organized-access constructs to map lawfully even when donor detail is incomplete.

Permitted mapping outcomes include:
- **Directly mapped:** the donor clearly defines the body, office, channel, and access result.
- **Normalized:** the donor construct is translated into Astra-native terminology while preserving the organized-access relationship.
- **Channel-assigned:** the donor clearly implies gatekeeping but leaves the exact route underspecified; the construct is assigned to the most lawful Astra access channel.
- **Placeholder-classified:** the body or service class is clear, but the exact office, venue, or access route remains intentionally open pending later conversion detail.
- **Quarantined pending later doctrine:** the donor construct depends on unresolved framework questions or conflicting institutional assumptions and should not be invented by decorative certainty.

### Anti-hallucination rule
When donor material clearly implies organized gatekeeping but does not specify whether access comes through membership, payment, permit, sponsorship, title, or exception, Astra must not fabricate false certainty.
It must map the construct into the most defensible lawful incomplete state or escalate for later doctrine.

## Closing doctrine statement

B12 defines Astra’s grammar for **organized gatekeeping and structured opportunity**.
It exists so that factions, institutions, offices, venues, and access channels can be converted lawfully across a large donor corpus without collapsing into vague “reputation,” generic politics, or ad hoc yes/no permissioning.

Its job is not to write political plots.
Its job is to ensure that when organized power grants, denies, routes, sponsors, restricts, or surveils meaningful opportunity, Astra has a stable doctrine for how that works.
