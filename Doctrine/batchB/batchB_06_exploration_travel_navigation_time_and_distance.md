# batchB_06_exploration_travel_navigation_time_and_distance.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s doctrine for **exploration, travel, navigation, transit, time expression, and distance expression**.

Its role is to provide Astra with a **universal traversal grammar** capable of absorbing donor systems built around overland journeys, site exploration, urban movement, wilderness expeditions, route networks, portal access, carrier transport, orbital transit, keyed destinations, unstable topology, and other movement-centered pressures without collapsing them into a single donor-shaped travel chapter.

This file is not merely a wilderness movement section.
It is the procedure layer for **movement through uncertain, pressured, permissioned, or topology-dependent space** across multiple scales.

In Astra terms, this file governs how:
- exploration state is entered and maintained,
- traversal is expressed across local, site, overland, expeditionary, and long-range scales,
- routes are known, inferred, navigated, lost, or denied,
- transit vectors are identified and procedurally resolved,
- time and distance are measured without requiring one donor’s metric assumptions,
- pace, delay, fatigue, and travel pressure are expressed,
- travelers perform roles and activities while moving,
- arrival state is determined,
- and traversal hands off cleanly into hazards, encounters, stealth, investigation, social entry, carrier operations, and destination procedures.

## Core doctrine terms

### Exploration state
An **exploration state** is a structured mode of play in which movement through space, uncertainty, discovery pressure, route choice, and traversal risk matter procedurally.

### Transit state
A **transit state** is the specific active condition of moving from one position, layer, site, region, node, destination, or access threshold toward another.

### Arrival state
An **arrival state** is the procedural condition in which travelers reach, partially reach, misreach, fail to reach, or are denied entry to a destination.
Arrival state includes not only destination resolution, but also the condition in which the travelers arrive.

### Traversal
**Traversal** is the broader Astra term for movement through operationally meaningful space, regardless of whether that space is physical, socialized, keyed, abstracted, unstable, or topology-breaking.

### Travel
**Travel** is sustained traversal between positions or destinations across one or more time units beyond immediate encounter-space movement.

### Navigation
**Navigation** is the process of determining, maintaining, correcting, or verifying movement toward a destination, route, or operational objective.

### Route
A **route** is any known, inferred, mapped, signaled, guided, keyed, or otherwise followable path between traversal points.

### Topology model
A **topology model** is the structure by which traversable space is represented in procedure.
Examples include exact routes, hex grids, nodes, districts, layers, abstract legs, or unstable path networks.

### Traversal scale
A **traversal scale** is the procedural magnitude at which travel is being handled.

### Transit vector
A **transit vector** is the enabling mode by which traversal is occurring.
A vector identifies what kind of movement is happening, what medium or permission enables it, what topology it uses, what can interrupt it, and what arrival logic applies.

### Carrier
A **carrier** is a host or medium that transports travelers or cargo through a transit state.
A carrier may be biological, mechanical, arcane, infrastructural, or abstracted.

### Access instrument
An **access instrument** is any token, permit, key, route-right, attunement, gate-right, authorization, or destination-enabling construct required for a transit vector or arrival state.

### Route knowledge state
A **route knowledge state** is the degree to which a route or destination is known, inferred, incomplete, unstable, hidden, restricted, or dynamically generated.

### Pace
**Pace** is the procedural expression of how quickly traversal is attempted relative to relevant burdens, caution, activity load, and pressure.

### Delay
**Delay** is any loss, diversion, interruption, slowdown, or misalignment that prevents expected traversal progress or alters the arrival state.

### Deviation
**Deviation** is any departure from intended route, heading, sequence, layer, or destination.

### Traversal chain
A **traversal chain** is a multi-stage journey composed of more than one vector, scale, topology, or carrier relation.

## Doctrine commitments

### 1. Traversal is broader than overland travel
Astra must not treat exploration and travel as synonymous with wilderness walking.
Traversal includes urban movement, site movement, network movement, portal movement, token-enabled movement, carrier movement, and topology-breaking movement.

### 2. Transit vector matters as much as speed
The question is not only how fast movement occurs, but how movement is being enabled, what medium it depends on, what gates it, and what happens if it fails.

### 3. Scale must remain plural
Astra must not assume one universal travel scale.
Local movement, site movement, overland movement, expeditionary travel, inter-settlement movement, and long-range transit all require lawful expression.

### 4. Navigation is not decorative
Astra must preserve route-following, heading-based travel, landmark navigation, guided travel, instrument navigation, map-based travel, and unstable-route correction as real procedure families.

### 5. Time and distance must remain expressible in multiple ways
Astra must not enforce false numeric precision or one donor’s favored measurement structure.
Distance may be measured in meters, miles, hexes, nodes, districts, route legs, segments, watches, hours, days, clocks, or other lawful units.

### 6. Arrival state matters
Astra must preserve not only whether a destination is reached, but also how travelers arrive: concealed, delayed, split, exposed, exhausted, denied, scattered, contaminated, carrier-bound, or otherwise procedurally altered.

### 7. Travel pressure must be preserved
Conversion must preserve donor traversal pressure.
Astra must not normalize route uncertainty, forced movement, expedition logistics, keyed access, portal risk, hostile transit, or topology instability into frictionless scene-to-scene movement.
Astra must also not inflate lightweight donor travel assumptions into bookkeeping-heavy simulation unless the donor truly relies on that pressure.

### 8. Transit is sometimes the scenario
Astra must support journeys where the primary pressure is the act of traveling itself, rather than treating travel only as a silent bridge between scenes.

## Exploration state, transit state, and arrival state

### Exploration state doctrine
Exploration state applies when movement through a space or route is procedurally meaningful because one or more of the following are true:
- the route is uncertain,
- the destination is not fully secured,
- the area is only partially known,
- movement choices change risk or opportunity,
- discovery pressure exists,
- pacing and route management matter,
- pressure may arise during travel,
- or arrival condition materially affects what happens next.

Exploration state is broader than encounter state and narrower than unrestricted narrative montage.

### Transit state doctrine
Transit state begins when a specific traversal attempt is in progress.
A transit state must identify:
- current position,
- intended destination,
- active vector,
- current carrier or medium if any,
- active route knowledge state,
- transit permissions if any,
- current pace,
- active roles,
- and known or unknown interruption classes.

### Arrival state doctrine
Arrival state resolves at the end of a transit state or when that transit is broken, denied, diverted, or completed.
Arrival state must determine:
- whether the destination was reached,
- whether the intended destination was the actual destination,
- whether entry was lawful, blocked, partial, or misaligned,
- and the operational posture of the travelers upon arrival.

Arrival states may include:
- stable arrival,
- concealed arrival,
- exposed arrival,
- delayed arrival,
- split arrival,
- scattered arrival,
- exhausted arrival,
- contested arrival,
- contaminated arrival,
- unstable arrival,
- misrouted arrival,
- denied arrival,
- partial arrival,
- or interrupted non-arrival.

## Traversal scale classes

Astra must support at least the following traversal scales.

### Local traversal
Immediate movement through a small area, structure, room network, lane set, canyon floor, or comparable space.
This is broader than encounter movement but smaller than full site traversal.

### Site traversal
Movement through a bounded exploration space such as a ruin, district complex, wilderness pocket, temple, station, mine, undercity segment, floating island, trial layer, or similar structure.

### Overland traversal
Movement across open terrain, linked routes, wilderness regions, or large terrestrial zones.

### Expedition traversal
Extended traversal in which route management, supply pressure, environmental risk, delay, attrition, or multi-day objectives matter materially.

### Inter-settlement transit
Travel between socialized destinations such as villages, cities, capitals, ports, orbital districts, enclaves, or other established destination nodes.

### Long-range transit
Travel where vector enablement, access rights, carrier dependency, route networks, or topology changes are more important than raw physical distance.
This includes interplanetary, interregional, interlayer, interdomain, or similarly broad movement classes.

Traversal scales may be chained.
A single journey may legitimately move from local traversal to overland travel to carrier transit to keyed portal entry to site traversal without changing doctrine language.

## Topology models

Astra must not assume one mandatory exploration topology.
At minimum, the following topology models are recognized.

### Exact-route topology
Space is represented through explicit measured routes, corridors, distances, or segments.

### Hex topology
Space is represented through contiguous hexes or equivalent area cells.
Hex topology is valid but not universal.

### Node-and-connection topology
Space is represented through points, nodes, landmarks, or destinations linked by traversable connections.

### District or network topology
Space is represented through streets, districts, corridors, traffic channels, sectors, social zones, tunnels, rail lines, or other structured networks.

### Layered-site topology
Space is represented through ordered levels, chambers, tiers, strata, decks, loops, or vertical/hierarchical exploration structures.

### Abstract-leg topology
Space is represented as route legs, travel segments, milestones, or progress chunks rather than literal geometry.

### Unstable topology
Space changes while being traversed, whether through movement of the environment, phase shift, maze logic, dream logic, hostile anomaly, or other mutable structure.

A donor topology should be preserved when it materially matters.
Otherwise it may be normalized into the most lawful Astra-native topology model.

## Navigation doctrine

Navigation doctrine must distinguish among multiple navigation families.

### Route-following navigation
Travelers follow a known or designated route such as a road, tunnel, shipping lane, pilgrimage path, corridor, or marked trail.

### Landmark navigation
Travelers orient by visible or known landmarks, beacons, skyline anchors, terrain signatures, or natural markers.

### Heading-based navigation
Travelers move by direction rather than by fully known route.
This may involve compass bearing, celestial guidance, directional instinct, or equivalent means.

### Map-based navigation
Travelers rely on maps, charts, route records, diagrams, node matrices, or previously documented route knowledge.

### Instrument-guided navigation
Travelers rely on technological, mystical, biological, or abstract instruments that reveal direction, route stability, environmental drift, or destination lock.

### Guided navigation
Travelers rely on a guide, escort, convoy lead, local authority, summoned intelligence, route spirit, convoy AI, or comparable guidance source.

### Topology-reactive navigation
Travelers must adapt to routes or spaces that mutate, collapse, loop, reorder, disguise themselves, or react to the travelers.

Navigation is not merely a speed modifier.
It is the procedure layer that determines whether intended movement remains aligned with route, destination, and access state.

Route knowledge states may include:

- whether arrival is guaranteed, probabilistic, conditional, or impossible,
- and whether the route can be shared, taught, sold, inherited, recorded, or only personally held.

## Transit vector doctrine

Transit vector doctrine is mandatory in Astra.
A transit vector identifies:
- what kind of traversal is occurring,
- what enables it,
- what medium or carrier it depends on,
- what topology it uses,
- what permissions or keys it requires,
- what can interrupt it,
- and how arrival state is determined.

At minimum, Astra recognizes the following vector families.

### 1. Local locomotion vector
Traversal occurs through the traveler’s direct movement capability.
Examples include walking, climbing, swimming, crawling, gliding, leaping, burrowing, drifting, and comparable personal movement.

### 2. Route-following vector
Traversal occurs by following an established route, corridor, lane, road, rail, tunnel, river path, or known trail.

### 3. Navigation vector
Traversal occurs by continuous orientation rather than by simply following a stable route.
This includes heading-based travel, landmark travel, charted travel, and instrument-guided travel.

### 4. Carrier-based vector
Traversal occurs through a carrier medium such as a mount, caravan, elevator, shuttle, ship, convoy platform, transport beast, or comparable host.
B06 owns the vector relation and arrival consequences, while detailed operation of such carriers hands off to B14 when required.

### 5. Access-instrument vector
Traversal occurs because a token, permit, key, destination-right, waypoint credential, or similar access instrument authorizes it.
This vector is especially important for portal travel, keyed location entry, mission access, and restricted route use.

### 6. Topology-breaking vector
Traversal occurs by bypassing ordinary contiguous space.
Examples include portals, gates, folds, tears, phase corridors, extradimensional passage, worm-like transit, and other non-contiguous movement.

### 7. Reality-shifting vector
Traversal occurs through or across spaces whose navigability changes in response to instability, corruption, dream logic, living topology, non-Euclidean structure, hostile anomaly, or other mutable principles.

### 8. Permissioned-destination vector
Traversal is primarily constrained not by physical distance but by eligibility, legal access, rank, attunement, faction standing, metaphysical qualification, or keyed worthiness.

A single transit state may use more than one vector at once.
For example, a carrier-based vector may also be permissioned, topology-breaking, or instrument-guided.

## Permissioned and keyed transit doctrine

Some traversal is gated primarily by permission rather than raw mobility.
This file must therefore distinguish between:
- being physically able to move,
- being legally allowed to move,
- being metaphysically eligible to move,
- and being correctly keyed to move.

Permissioned and keyed transit may rely on:
- rank,
- clearance,
- token possession,
- route attunement,
- personal binding,
- destination registration,
- faction authorization,
- trial eligibility,
- ritual condition,
- or environmental alignment.

B06 owns the meaning of those constructs as **transit gates**.
It does not own the broader item, economy, or institutional-law definition of the gating instrument itself.

## Topology-breaking and reality-shifting transit doctrine

Astra must support traversal where contiguous geography is not the governing model.

### Topology-breaking transit
This includes any vector where movement bypasses ordinary intervening space or uses exceptional access structures.
Examples include gates, folds, keyed waypoints, jump routes, transit tears, non-local passages, and other discontinuous movement structures.

### Reality-shifting transit
This includes any vector where the route itself can mutate, loop, react, corrode, invert, reassign destination, or otherwise refuse stable mapping.
Examples include shifting labyrinths, dream routes, mirror regions, corrupt anomalies, living jungles, impossible corridors, and hostile transit spaces.

B06 must support these as doctrine families without forcing one shared cosmological explanation.
Detailed planar law, anomaly lore, or metaphysical source explanations do not belong here.

If corpus pressure later causes these sections to swell beyond sustainable scope, a dedicated sister file may be reserved for deeper topology-breaking transit doctrine.

## Anti-hallucination and preservation doctrine

### Donor traversal pressure preservation
Conversion must preserve donor traversal pressure.
Do not flatten route uncertainty, token-gated access, convoy dependence, portal risk, hostile transit, topological instability, or expedition burden into generic “you arrive after X time.”

### No false topology inheritance
Do not silently universalize donor-local assumptions such as fixed hex size, one standard pace, one sector-wide clock, mandatory contiguous geography, or one privileged route model.

### No false precision
Do not invent exact distances, route times, or navigation certainty where the donor text is abstract, symbolic, variable, or intentionally incomplete.

### No false continuity
Do not assume that all spaces share one seamless cosmological or logistical model.
A traversal construct may connect radically different destination regimes without implying one donor’s metaphysics has become Astra canon.

## Lawful incomplete mapping

When a donor traversal construct cannot be mapped with full confidence, it may be classified as:
- **directly mapped**,
- **normalized**,
- **scale-assigned**,
- **vector-assigned**,
- **abstracted**,
- or **quarantined pending further doctrine**.

Astra must prefer lawful incompleteness over false certainty.
