# batchB_02_health_injury_healing_death_and_recovery_framework.md

## Purpose

This file defines Astra Ascension’s **survivability-state doctrine** for structured play and conversion work. It governs how Astra expresses harm, impairment, instability, injury, healing, restoration, death, reversibility, and recovery without collapsing those functions into a single donor-shaped hit point chapter.

This file is not a generic damage chapter, not a condition glossary, and not an environmental hazard chapter. It is the **survivability translation layer** for Batch B.

Its purpose is to provide a conversion-stable framework capable of absorbing vitality-pool systems, wound and injury systems, stress-plus-consequence systems, durability-state systems, horror attrition systems, heroic recovery systems, and mixed survivability models without silently inheriting donor assumptions as hidden Astra law.

Astra must define survivability as a **translation grammar** before it maps donor numbers, tables, wound boxes, trauma clocks, or stress tracks into that grammar.

Donor constructs may pressure Astra doctrine, but no donor expression becomes hidden law by default. This includes:

## Core doctrine terms

### Survivability state
A **survivability state** is the current functional and existential condition of an entity with respect to continued operation, impairment, collapse, death risk, and recoverability.

### Holder
A **holder** is the body, structure, vessel, pattern, swarm, frame, or other substrate that bears survivability consequences.

The holder concept exists so Astra can express survivability for biological beings, synthetic entities, vehicles, distributed swarms, summoned forms, and other non-identical constructs without pretending they all use one identical anatomy.

### Holder class
A **holder class** is the abstract type of substrate through which survivability is expressed.

Holder class is not the same thing as ancestry, kinform, creature taxonomy, profession, or vehicle category. It exists only to state what kind of survivability bearer is involved.

### Vitality
**Vitality** is the immediately spendable survivability margin that can be depleted without automatically implying lasting structural injury.

Vitality may be expressed as hit points, stress, stamina, coherence, integrity margin, shield load, or other donor-equivalent measures.

### Buffer
A **buffer** is a temporary survivability layer that absorbs pressure without counting as true restoration of the holder.

Buffers may include temporary hit points, shield overlays, ablative plating, ward screens, backup tissue bloom, or similar temporary protections.

### Injury
An **injury** is a meaningful survivability consequence that persists beyond ordinary momentary vitality loss and normally requires treatment, recovery time, restoration, or accommodation.

### Affliction
An **affliction** is an ongoing adverse survivability burden driven by an active state, source, contamination, exposure, curse, infection, toxin, radiation burden, psychic rupture, or comparable continuing process.

### Impairment
**Impairment** is the reduction of function caused by harm, injury, affliction, or degradation.

### Instability
**Instability** is the state in which a holder is no longer safely self-maintaining and may worsen, collapse, or die without intervention.

### Stabilization
**Stabilization** is the act of halting or containing immediate worsening without necessarily restoring full function.

### Healing
**Healing** is any process that restores survivability state, vitality, or function.

### Restoration
**Restoration** is higher-order recovery that may remove injury, reverse lingering loss, restore lost maximum capacity, regrow structure, reconstruct damaged systems, or reverse otherwise nontrivial survivability consequences.

### Recovery
**Recovery** is the time-based process by which a holder returns toward functional stability after harm.

### Recovery burden
A **recovery burden** is the remaining cost, limitation, or obligation that persists after immediate survival is secured.

### Incapacitation
**Incapacitation** is the state in which the holder cannot currently function as an effective participant in ordinary action.

### Dying state
A **dying state** is an unstable terminal survivability condition in which the holder faces imminent death or irreversible loss unless something changes.

### Death state
A **death state** is the point at which the holder has crossed from living or functioning continuity into loss of life, continuity, or viable operation.

### Reversibility
**Reversibility** is the degree to which a death state, injury state, or survivability loss can be undone by available means.

## Translation posture: universal survivability model support

This file must support at least the following donor survivability patterns:
- **vitality-pool systems**, where survivability is primarily tracked through an expendable pool,
- **wound or injury systems**, where discrete injuries matter more than a large pool,
- **stress-plus-consequence systems**, where short-term endurance and lasting consequences are separated,
- **durability or state-track systems**, where performance degrades across explicit bands or conditions,
- **hybrid systems**, where a pool, thresholds, consequences, and death pressure coexist.

No one of these models is Astra’s universal default. Astra doctrine must be able to host each as a lawful translation target.

## Threat-preservation and lethality-conversion doctrine

### Threat-preservation invariant
Conversion must preserve the intended survivability pressure of the donor construct.

Do not normalize lethal, attritional, consequence-heavy, or horror-leaning systems into harmless Astra padding.

Do not inflate forgiving, heroic, cinematic, or recovery-generous systems into accidental meat grinders.

Do not erase the donor distinction between:
- momentary setback,
- meaningful injury,
- prolonged danger,
- death pressure,
- lasting consequence,
- recoverability.

### Lethality-conversion doctrine
When converting survivability material, Astra must preserve all of the following whenever the donor meaningfully distinguishes them:
- expected time-to-collapse,
- expected time-to-treatment,
- expected time-to-recovery,
- expected persistence of consequences,
- expected risk of permanent loss,
- expected reversibility ceiling.

If donor survivability is intentionally swingy, harsh, forgiving, attritional, or cinematic, that tone must survive translation.

## Survivability profiles and holder classes

### Survivability profiles
A **survivability profile** is a conversion and doctrine tool for expressing how much punishment, collapse pressure, and recovery complexity a holder is expected to carry inside the same survivability framework.

Profiles are not separate rule systems for protagonists versus everyone else. They are lawful expressions of the same doctrine shell.

Core profile classes are:
- **fragile**,
- **standard**,
- **heroic**,
- **resilient**,
- **structural / platform**,
- **swarm / distributed**,
- **elite / boss**, when required by donor structure.

Profiles govern translation expectations such as:
- how much survivability margin is normal,
- how quickly collapse pressure appears,
- how much injury complexity is expected,
- how much temporary buffering is typical,
- how forgiving recovery usually is,
- how much lasting harm can be meaningfully carried.

Profiles do not excuse lawlessness. A fragile profile still uses lawful survivability states. A heroic profile does not become secretly immune to consequence unless the donor truly says so.

### Holder classes
Core holder classes include:
- **biological holder**, for ordinary living bodies,
- **synthetic holder**, for machine, artificial, or fabricated bodies,
- **hybrid holder**, for bodies combining biological and non-biological survivability layers,
- **structural holder**, for platforms, fortifications, large devices, or constructions,
- **distributed holder**, for swarms, colonies, cloud minds, nanite masses, or other non-single-body entities,
- **ephemeral or manifested holder**, for projected, summoned, energetic, spectral, or pattern-bound forms.

Holder class affects how survivability is described, not whether survivability doctrine applies.

Example: a biological standard profile, a synthetic resilient profile, and a structural/platform profile may all suffer impairment, instability, collapse, repair needs, and irrecoverable loss, but the means and language of treatment differ.

## Health-state ladder

Astra survivability doctrine recognizes a general ladder of states.

### 1. Stable / functional
The holder is operational and not currently at material risk of collapse.

### 2. Pressured / impaired
The holder remains active but has suffered meaningful degradation, strain, or reduced functional margin.

### 3. Critical
The holder remains active or barely active, but collapse or disabling loss is near.

### 4. Incapacitated
The holder is no longer functioning as a meaningful active participant.

### 5. Unstable / dying
The holder is in active danger of worsening into death or irrecoverable loss without intervention.

### 6. Dead / destroyed / terminated
The holder has crossed into actual loss of life, operative continuity, or platform viability.

### 7. Irrecoverable / functionally lost
The holder has crossed beyond currently available restoration or continuity recovery.

Not every donor system names each rung, but Astra conversions must respect them when donor logic implies them.

## Buffer, vitality, injury, and affliction separation

This distinction is mandatory.

### Buffer versus body
A temporary buffer is not the same thing as restored vitality, and restored vitality is not the same thing as repaired structure.

Buffers may absorb pressure without removing injury.

Losing a buffer does not automatically mean the holder is wounded.

Regaining a buffer does not automatically count as healing.

### Vitality versus injury
Vitality loss represents reduced survivability margin. It does not automatically imply lasting trauma.

Injury represents structural, biological, psychic, systemic, or functional harm that exceeds ordinary expendable survivability margin.

### Injury versus affliction
Injury is harm that has already been done.

Affliction is an active adverse burden that continues to impose harm, deterioration, or treatment burden until removed, stabilized, resisted, burned out, or otherwise resolved.

Affliction may create injury. Injury may carry affliction. They are not the same thing and must not be merged casually.

### Max-capacity loss versus ordinary loss
A reduction in maximum survivability capacity is not the same thing as ordinary current depletion.

Astra must preserve donor distinctions between:
- current loss,
- maximum capacity loss,
- suppressed capacity,
- blocked recovery,
- interrupted regeneration.

## Harm outcome doctrine

When harm enters the survivability layer, Astra must distinguish between four broad outcome classes.

### 1. Margin loss
Immediate depletion of vitality, stress, coherence, shield load, or equivalent survivability margin.

### 2. Impairment
Reduction in function, control, mobility, coherence, throughput, or other operative capability.

### 3. Injury creation
Creation of a lasting harm object or state that persists beyond ordinary short-term depletion.

### 4. Destabilization
Movement toward collapse, incapacity, dying state, death, or irrecoverable loss.

A single event may produce more than one outcome class.

## Incapacitation and collapse doctrine

### Incapacitation
A holder becomes incapacitated when it can no longer participate as an effective acting entity under the current procedural mode.

Incapacitation may represent unconsciousness, paralysis, shutdown, overload, catastrophic distraction, structural immobility, soul-severance, command failure, swarm dispersal, or another donor-equivalent state.

### Collapse thresholds
Astra does not hardcode one universal collapse trigger.

Collapse may be triggered by:
- exhaustion of vitality,
- accumulation of wounds,
- threshold crossings,
- failed death-pressure checks,
- state-track progression,
- catastrophic hit outcomes,
- environmental overwhelm,
- donor-specified collapse events.

### Dying pressure
When donor logic distinguishes between “incapacitated but stable” and “actively dying,” Astra must preserve that distinction.

An incapacitated holder is not automatically dead.

A dying holder is not automatically recoverable without intervention.

## Stabilization and triage doctrine

### Stabilization
Stabilization halts or slows worsening. It does not necessarily restore consciousness, remove injury, or return combat readiness.

### Triage
Triage is the prioritization and classification of treatment under pressure.

Astra must support the doctrinal distinction between:
- immediate life-saving intervention,
- short-term field treatment,
- post-encounter treatment,
- formal recovery care.

### Time sensitivity
Time to treatment matters.

When donor logic changes outcome severity based on how quickly aid arrives, Astra must preserve that pressure.

### Field stabilization versus full recovery
Field stabilization may:
- halt dying progression,
- prevent worsening,
- restore minimal function,
- buy time for later care.

Field stabilization does not automatically:
- clear afflictions,
- remove lasting impairment,
- repair destroyed structure,
- restore full capacity,
- erase recovery burden.

## Injury architecture

### Injury classes
Astra recognizes at least three injury persistence classes:
- **transient injury**,
- **lingering injury**,
- **permanent or semi-permanent injury**.

### Transient injury
A transient injury imposes meaningful short-term limitation but is expected to clear with ordinary treatment or recovery.

### Lingering injury
A lingering injury remains after immediate danger passes and requires explicit recovery, accommodation, or restoration.

### Permanent or semi-permanent injury
A permanent or semi-permanent injury alters the holder until major restoration, reconstruction, or explicit reversal occurs.

### Structural loss
Some injuries represent actual loss or destruction of function-bearing structure. Astra must preserve such consequences when donor logic requires them.

### Recovery burden attachment
Every meaningful injury should carry an appropriate recovery burden classification such as:
- light,
- moderate,
- severe,
- restoration-gated,
- unresolved until reconstructive action.

## Affliction doctrine

Affliction is not just “a condition.” It is a survivability burden that persists because something remains active.

Afflictions may include:
- toxin burden,
- disease burden,
- radiation burden,
- bleeding-type deterioration,
- burning or corrosion-type persistence,
- curse burden,
- psychic rupture,
- contamination,
- suffocation or deprivation progression,
- other donor-equivalent ongoing threats.

This file does not define the source mechanics of these afflictions. It defines how afflictions relate to survivability pressure, stabilization, treatment, and recovery.

## Healing channels and restoration classes

Astra must classify healing and restoration by **channel**, not merely by flavor label.

Core channels include:
- **natural recovery**,
- **assisted mundane treatment**,
- **medical or clinical intervention**,
- **alchemical or consumable intervention**,
- **regenerative biological repair**,
- **ritual or metaphysical healing**,
- **technological repair or reconstruction**,
- **structural repair**,
- **affliction-clearing restoration**,
- **major reconstruction or reconstitution**,
- **resurrection or return-of-continuity effects**, when applicable.

### Healing versus restoration
Healing restores function or margin.

Restoration may reverse deeper losses such as:
- maximum-capacity reduction,
- destroyed limbs or components,
- scarred systems,
- curse-bonded harm,
- soul-separation,
- structural collapse,
- donor-level irreversible consequences that become reversible only through exceptional means.

### Channel compatibility
Not every holder class or injury class responds to every channel equally.

A biological injury may prefer medical or regenerative healing.

A structural holder may require repair rather than biological healing.

A hybrid holder may require layered care.

An affliction may need removal before ordinary healing becomes effective.

## Reversibility classes and restoration tiers

### Reversibility classes
Astra survivability doctrine recognizes at least the following reversibility classes:
- **ordinary reversible**,
- **treatment-gated reversible**,
- **restoration-gated reversible**,
- **exception-only reversible**,
- **currently irreversible**.

### Restoration tiers
Astra should recognize restoration tiers so conversion work can map donor survivability ceilings without flattening them.

Useful tier language includes:
- **stabilizing restoration**,
- **functional restoration**,
- **full restorative recovery**,
- **major reconstruction**,
- **continuity return**,
- **identity-risk or cost-bearing return**,
- **forbidden or unavailable restoration**.

Restoration tiers should preserve donor distinctions between “you survive,” “you are functional again,” and “you are made whole again.”

## Death-state doctrine

### Death is not one singular law
Astra must not assume one universal dead/not-dead binary for all donor systems.

### Core death-state classes
At minimum, Astra doctrine must support:
- **incapacitated but stable**,
- **incapacitated and dying**,
- **dead but recoverable**,
- **dead and restoration-gated**,
- **destroyed or continuity-broken**,
- **irrecoverable under current means**.

### Continuity concerns
Some donor systems tie reversibility to body continuity, soul continuity, pattern continuity, component retention, clone availability, vessel integrity, or ritual compatibility.

Astra must preserve those dependencies when they materially matter.

### Death pressure versus destruction pressure
Death for a biological holder and total destruction for a structural or synthetic holder may occupy analogous survivability positions without requiring identical flavor or causation.

## Recovery cadence doctrine

Astra must recognize multiple recovery timescales.

### Immediate recovery
Recovery that occurs during or immediately after active structured procedure.

### Short recovery
Recovery available after a short operational pause, field treatment window, or brief rest period.

### Full rest recovery
Recovery that requires meaningful rest, sleep cycle, maintenance cycle, or safe downtime.

### Extended convalescence
Recovery that requires repeated time investment, supervision, treatment, or sheltered conditions.

### Rehabilitation
Recovery that restores higher function after major injury, destabilization, or system loss.

### Major restoration
Recovery that requires exceptional intervention and exceeds ordinary convalescence.

Recovery cadence must preserve donor pressure around:
- how fast margin returns,
- how slowly injuries clear,
- whether afflictions worsen without care,
- whether complete recovery is expected, delayed, partial, or impossible without restoration.

## Clearance semantics

Astra must define clearance language clearly so donor text does not silently smuggle in inconsistent assumptions.

### Until stabilized
The holder remains in worsening danger until stabilization occurs. Stabilization halts worsening but does not imply full healing.

### Until healed
The holder remains under a harm consequence until an applicable healing channel restores the relevant survivability loss.

### Until restored
The holder remains under a deeper survivability consequence until a restoration-grade effect or process reverses it.

### Until recovered
The holder remains under a recovery burden until enough time, treatment, rehabilitation, or equivalent process has resolved the burden.

### Until repaired
A structural, synthetic, or gear-adjacent survivability consequence remains until suitable repair occurs.

### Until cleared or removed
An affliction or contamination remains until its active burden is specifically neutralized, resisted out, burned off, detoxified, decontaminated, exorcised, or otherwise ended.

These terms must not be treated as decorative synonyms. They express materially different survivability states and obligations.

## Lawful incomplete mappings

Not every donor text is explicit enough to support perfect survivability conversion.

When survivability meaning is incomplete, Astra must use lawful incomplete mapping categories rather than invent false certainty.

### Directly mapped
The donor survivability construct is clear enough to convert without doctrinal guesswork.

### Normalized
The donor construct is translated into Astra doctrine with conservative normalization that preserves threat and consequence intent.

### Profile-assigned
The donor construct is incomplete, but survivability pressure can be preserved by assigning a lawful survivability profile and holder class.

### Quarantined pending doctrine
The donor construct cannot yet be translated without inventing unsupported law. It must be flagged for later doctrine or later corpus handling.

Quarantine is preferable to false confidence.

## Conversion guidance

### Preserve distinctions that matter
If the donor distinguishes any of the following, Astra should preserve them:
- knockout versus dying,
- injury versus affliction,
- current loss versus maximum loss,
- temporary shielding versus true recovery,
- stabilization versus recovery,
- recovery versus restoration,
- recoverable death versus final loss.

### Do not flatten profile expectations
Do not force all protagonists, major NPCs, beasts, summons, swarms, machines, ships, and structures into one exact survivability expression.

### Do not create donor drift through omission
If the donor is harsh, preserve the harshness.

If the donor is forgiving, preserve the forgiveness.

If the donor is consequence-heavy, preserve the consequence density.

If the donor is cinematic, preserve the cinematic recovery assumptions.
